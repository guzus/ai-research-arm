  <p class="twitter-story-lead">OpenAI announced that an internal model has disproven the Erdős unit distance conjecture, a central open problem in discrete geometry first posed by Paul Erdős in 1946. For nearly 80 years, mathematicians believed optimal solutions looked roughly like square grids. The AI discovered an entirely new family of constructions that performs better — the first time AI has autonomously solved a prominent open problem central to a field of mathematics. The result was peer-reviewed by mathematicians including Jacob Tsimerman (University of Toronto), who wrote that "AI tools are capable of changing research in mathematics in a dramatic way."</p>

      <p><strong>Counter / contradicting:</strong> The model is "internal" — not publicly available, not reproducible by external researchers. Without access to the model, the architecture, or the inference setup, independent verification is limited to checking the mathematical correctness of the output, not the process that produced it. The companion paper co-author (Tsimerman) is human — the AI proposed the construction, but mathematicians validated and contextualized it. The distinction between "AI discovered" and "AI proposed and humans verified" matters for claims of autonomy. Also: AI models have previously solved competition-level math problems (IMO problems, formal theorem proving), but those are closed-form challenges, not open research problems. The Erdős result is genuinely different in kind — but the gap between "disproved a conjecture" and "built a new mathematical theory" remains significant. The next test is whether the AI can produce novel conjectures, not just disprove existing ones.</p>
