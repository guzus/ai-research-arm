## 01. From a Tsinghua lab to a public company

Zhipu's thesis sentence is that a university research group can become a national AI champion in six years. Zhipu AI was founded in 2019 as a spin-out of the Knowledge Engineering Group (KEG) in Tsinghua University's Department of Computer Science, by professors Tang Jie and Li Juanzi.[^1,31] Its legal vehicle, Beijing Zhipu Huazhang Technology, was later converted into the joint-stock "Knowledge Atlas Technology" for its public listing.[^9] The academic DNA is not marketing varnish: founder and chief scientist Tang Jie is a Tsinghua professor with an ACM/AAAI/IEEE fellowship and a citation record in the tens of thousands, and was a technical lead on BAAI's "WuDao" effort before GLM became the company's product spine.[^31] Day-to-day the company is run by CEO Zhang Peng, also a Tsinghua computer-science PhD, while Liu Debing chairs the board.[^9]

- {date: 2021-03, headline: "GLM pretraining method", body: "Autoregressive blank-infilling objective published (ACL 2022)."}

- {date: 2025-01, headline: "US Entity List", body: "Ten Zhipu entities blacklisted; pivot to domestic compute."}
- {date: 2025-07, headline: "GLM-4.5 + Z.ai rebrand", body: "First MoE flagship, open-weight under MIT."}

Why this matters: most "China's OpenAI" labels are interchangeable hype, but Zhipu's combination of a genuine pretraining-research lineage, an open-weight release habit, and a public listing is structurally distinct from its peers. The counterpoint — which the rest of this article develops — is that the operating business under that lineage looks far more like a government systems-integrator than like OpenAI, and the public valuation has detached from both.[^11,13]

## 02. The GLM family: the technical spine

The thesis here is that GLM is a real research program, not a wrapper. The original "GLM" is a pretraining method: a 2021 paper (ACL 2022) introduced *autoregressive blank infilling* with 2D positional encodings, unifying understanding and generation in one objective rather than copying the GPT decoder recipe.[^3] That method scaled into GLM-130B (2022), an open bilingual 130-billion-parameter dense model notable less for raw benchmark wins than for reaching INT4 quantization "with almost no performance loss" — the first 100B-scale model that could be run on commodity GPUs.[^2] The open consumer line followed: ChatGLM-6B and successors drew over ten million Hugging Face downloads in 2023 alone, seeding a developer base.[^38]

The architecture has not stood still. GLM-4 (2024) was a dense flagship trained on roughly 10 trillion tokens;[^38] GLM-4.5 (July 2025) was the discontinuity — Zhipu's *first* Mixture-of-Experts model at 355B total / 32B active parameters, trained on 23T tokens and released under the permissive MIT license, alongside a compact GLM-4.5-Air at 106B/12B.[^4] GLM-4.6 (October 2025) pushed context from 128K to 200K tokens and claimed over 30% better token efficiency.[^5] By mid-2026 the family had reached GLM-5.2: a 744B-total / 40B-active MoE with a one-million-token context window, still MIT-licensed.[^6]

:::stats
- {label: Pretraining objective, value: "blank infilling", note: "GLM, 2021"}
- {label: First open scale, value: 130B, unit: params, note: "dense, INT4-capable"}
- {label: First MoE flagship, value: "355B/32B", note: "GLM-4.5, MIT"}
- {label: Latest context, value: 1M, unit: tokens, note: "GLM-5.2"}
:::

Two precision points the popular coverage routinely garbles. First, the licensing shift is recent: GLM-4-9B (2024) shipped under a custom non-MIT license; *MIT* open-weighting is specifically a GLM-4.5-era change, and it is the flagship itself — not just a small sibling — that is now open.[^4,6] Second, "130B" and "744B" are different *kinds* of model (dense vs sparse MoE); conflating the parameter counts across generations overstates the scaling. The counterpoint to the bull case: open weights are a real moat for adoption, but giving away the frontier model is an odd choice for a company that urgently needs revenue — a tension Section 05 returns to.

## 03. How good are the models, really?

The thesis: GLM is genuinely frontier-class *among open weights*, and genuinely a notch below the closed Western frontier — and almost every "parity" table you will see is vendor-measured. Zhipu's own GLM-4.5 technical report put the model 3rd of 14 on its averaged benchmark suite, with 91.0% on AIME 24 and 64.2% on SWE-bench Verified.[^4] But on coding and agentic tasks — the benchmarks that matter for the product — GLM trailed: on Zhipu's own GLM-4.6 grid, SWE-bench Verified came in at 68.0% versus Claude Sonnet 4.5's 77.2%.[^37] Independent signals agree on the ranking: on the human-voted LMArena leaderboard GLM-4.6 sits clearly below the frontier Western cluster.[^36]

The strongest independent data point is also the most favorable to Zhipu. Artificial Analysis rated the June-2026 GLM-5.2 the *leading open-weights model* on its Intelligence Index (51), placing it fourth overall — behind Fable 5, Claude Opus 4.8 and GPT-5.5, but ahead of DeepSeek V4 Pro and MiniMax-M3.[^7]

:::rank-list
- {label: "Fable 5 (closed)", value: "60", pct: 100}
- {label: "Claude Opus 4.8 (closed)", value: "56", pct: 93}
- {label: "GPT-5.5 (closed)", value: "55", pct: 92}
- {label: "GLM-5.2 (open, MIT)", value: "51", pct: 85, highlight: true}
- {label: "DeepSeek V4 Pro (open)", value: "44", pct: 73}
- {label: "MiniMax-M3 (open)", value: "44", pct: 73}

The honest caveat cuts both ways. Zhipu's headline coding claims for GLM-5.2 — that it beats GPT-5.5 on some long-horizon coding benchmarks — are *vendor-scaffold* numbers with no standardized third-party replication yet, and GLM-5.2 burns far more output tokens per task (~43k) than peers, hurting its intelligence-per-dollar.[^7] What is independently verifiable is the price: GLM-5.2 lists at roughly $1.40/$4.40 per million input/output tokens, about one-sixth to one-seventh the cost of GPT-5.5 or Claude Opus.[^7] Why this matters: Zhipu's competitive claim is not "best model" but "90% of the frontier at a fraction of the cost, with weights you can self-host" — a claim the independent data largely supports for the open-weight tier.

## 04. The money: funding, valuation, and a 1,600% stock

The thesis of this section is that Zhipu's financing story split cleanly into two eras — a normal venture build-up, then a post-IPO market dislocation. Before the listing it raised across eight equity rounds. The marquee moments: Saudi Aramco's Prosperity7 joined a ~$400M financing in 2024 that valued the company near $3B — among the first foreign capital into a major Chinese generative-AI firm;[^16] a December 2024 round took ¥3B (US$412M) from Alibaba, Tencent and state vehicles;[^17] and after the January 2025 US blacklisting, capital turned domestic, with ~US$257M of local-government money from Hangzhou, Chengdu and Zhuhai in March 2025.[^18]

Then came the IPO. On 8 January 2026 Zhipu listed on the Hong Kong Stock Exchange (ticker 2513) at HK$116.20 a share, raising about HK$4.35B (US$560M) and closing its first day at HK$131.5, up 13.17%, for a market capitalization near HK$57.89B.[^9,10] What happened next is the single most important fact about the company today.

The disconnect from fundamentals is stark — and a sell-side analyst named it.

That is roughly a thousand times revenue. The counterpoint a bull would offer: Hong Kong's thin post-IPO float and lock-ups genuinely distort price discovery, the A-share "H-to-A" return (Section 10) could add liquidity, and the market is pricing AGI optionality and national-champion status, not this year's income statement. The counter-counterpoint is simply that ~$105M of revenue and a widening loss leave an enormous amount of optionality to deliver.[^13,14]

## 05. The business-model paradox

Here is the thesis most coverage misses: Zhipu markets itself as a token-selling "Model-as-a-Service" lab, but its revenue reality is the opposite. In H1 2025, about 84.8% of revenue came from on-premise/private deployment — high-touch, project-based systems work for Chinese government and state-owned buyers — while the cloud API that *is* MaaS contributed only 15.2%.[^13] This is a services business wearing a software-company narrative.

:::donut(center-label="H1 2025")
- {label: "On-prem / private deployment", value: 84.8}
- {label: "Cloud API (MaaS)", value: 15.2}
:::

The financials underline the strain. FY2025 revenue grew 131.9% to ¥724M (≈$105M), but the statutory net loss widened to ¥4.72B (the adjusted loss was ~¥3.2B), with R&D alone running about ¥3.18B — roughly 439% of revenue.[^12,11] Cumulative losses from 2022 through H1 2025 totalled roughly ¥6.2B.[^15] Worse for the software thesis, margins moved the wrong way as the company scaled: blended gross margin fell from 56.3% in 2024 to 41.0% in 2025 as lower-margin cloud and delivery-heavy deployment grew, and the cloud-API line itself ran a *negative* gross margin in H1 2025.[^14]

:::slope(left-label=2024, right-label=2025, unit=%)
| Item         | 2024 | 2025 |
|--------------|------|------|
| Gross margin | 56.3 | 41.0 |
:::

The pricing strategy explains both the adoption and the losses. Zhipu helped ignite China's 2024 LLM price war, cutting GLM API prices to as low as 0.1 yuan (~$0.014) per million-token prompt — about 90% below the industry average.[^19] Its sharpest distribution play is the GLM Coding Plan: an Anthropic-protocol-compatible endpoint marketed as a drop-in Claude Code replacement at roughly one-seventh the price, with Lite/Pro/Max tiers around $18/$72/$160 a month.[^20,21] That plan, plus open weights, drove real third-party usage — Chinese models reached ~61% of OpenRouter token consumption by early 2026, and Zhipu's own provider page shows GLM-5.2 at 1.29T cumulative tokens.[^22,23] The counterpoint, and the bull's best card: on-prem gross margins (~59%) are actually healthy, enterprise revenue is sticky, and customer concentration is *falling* (top-five customers reportedly dropped from ~55% to ~40% of revenue).[^13,32] Why this matters: the question is not whether Zhipu has demand — it does — but whether token-selling can ever carry a software multiple when the cash comes from government projects.

## 06. Compute under sanctions

The thesis: the US Entity List was meant to constrain Zhipu and instead forced the clearest domestic-silicon pivot in Chinese AI. On 16 January 2025 the US Bureau of Industry and Security added ten Zhipu-affiliated entities to the Entity List (90 FR 4617).[^8] The stated reason is worth quoting precisely, because press summaries embellish it.

:::quote(attr="US BIS final rule, Federal Register, 16 January 2025")
These additions are being made because these entities advance the People's Republic of China's military modernization through the development and integration of advanced artificial intelligence research.
:::

The legal effect is a license requirement for *all items subject to the EAR* with a *presumption of denial* — the broadest standard entry, foreclosing legal access to Nvidia's leading-edge accelerators and US software.[^8] One correction worth flagging: contrary to some reporting, the Zhipu entries carry ==no "Footnote 4" or military-end-user designation== — they reference only Section 744.11 of the EAR.[^8] Zhipu publicly called the listing baseless and said it would not materially affect operations.[^11]

The substantive response was a compute migration. Zhipu's GLM-Image was, per the company and Chinese state media, trained end-to-end on Huawei Ascend Atlas 800T A2 hardware with the MindSpore framework and no US chips;[^26] GLM-5 then completed full-stack adaptation across seven domestic chip platforms — Huawei Ascend, Moore Threads, Hygon, Cambricon, Kunlunxin, Muxi and Enflame.[^28]

:::callout(kind=warn, label=Precision)
A widely-repeated claim is that the GLM-5 *language model* was trained entirely on Huawei Ascend with zero Nvidia. The verifiable version is narrower: that full-stack-on-Ascend claim is solid for **GLM-Image**, while the GLM-5 LLM's own model card discloses training tokens but **not** training hardware — Ascend appears there under *inference/deployment*.[^27] Treat "GLM-5 trained Nvidia-free" as unconfirmed.
:::

The counterpoint is a hard engineering ceiling. Independent analysis finds Huawei's flagship CloudMatrix 384 (Ascend 910C) matches Nvidia GB200-class *system* performance only by drawing about 4.1x the power and at 2.5x worse power-per-FLOP, and that some Chinese labs struggled to complete training runs on Ascend even with Huawei engineers on site.[^29] Why this matters: the sanctions did not stop Zhipu, but they imposed a structural cost and efficiency tax that compounds the unit-economics problem in Section 05.

## 07. The competitive landscape

The thesis: Zhipu's real peer set is smaller and tougher than the "six tigers" cliché implies. The label (六小龙) bundles Zhipu with Moonshot, MiniMax, Baichuan, 01.AI and StepFun, but two of those have exited the frontier race — 01.AI stopped pretraining frontier models and Baichuan pivoted to vertical medical AI.[^32] Zhipu's actual competition is segmented: DeepSeek (the open-weight cost/research disruptor), Alibaba's Qwen (the scale-and-distribution incumbent), and surviving startups Moonshot (Kimi) and MiniMax.

On the two distribution channels Zhipu plays in, giant-backed rivals lead. Alibaba's Qwen captured over 50% of global open-weight downloads in early 2026 (153.6M in February alone), dwarfing GLM's volume; and in China's consumer app market, Zhipu is absent from the top-five MAU ranking led by ByteDance's Doubao (~345M monthly active users).[^24,25] Zhipu's differentiation is therefore not consumer reach or download share but a full-stack, state-aligned posture: open frontier weights *paired with* a commercial API and direct enterprise/government sales — distinct from both the pure research lab (DeepSeek) and the cloud-scale incumbent (Qwen).

:::callout(kind=info, label="Read this way")
"Market share" has no single winner here. By global API tokens Zhipu is mid-pack; by consumer MAU it is absent; by open-weight downloads Qwen dominates. Zhipu's distinctive lead is on the *capital-markets* narrative (first AI-tiger IPO) and on frontier *coding/agentic quality* among open models — two narrow axes, not the whole board.
:::

The counterpoint: the consumer-MAU absence is partly by design — Zhipu deliberately bet on B2B and agents, not a chatbot land-grab. But "by design" does not change that the company gave up the highest-frequency surface of AI to better-funded rivals, and that its open-weight downloads trail the very rival (Qwen) it most needs to beat on ecosystem.

## 08. Partnerships and global ambition

The thesis: Zhipu's best-verified partnerships are about *silicon*, and its international story is real but tiny. The domestic-chip co-optimization cluster is the strongest: training collaboration with Huawei on Ascend, GLM-4.6 reaching FP8+Int4 inference on Cambricon, and seven-platform adaptation for GLM-5.[^28] Distribution partnerships exist too — China Telecom bundles GLM-5 into its AI "token plans" alongside its own model and DeepSeek.[^32]

On customers, Zhipu reported its GLM models served over 12,000 enterprise clients and more than 45 million developers by September 2025; a Ningxia branch of China Telecom was its second-largest customer, and named logos are otherwise scarce in the disclosures — buyers are reported as sectors (telecom 13.6%, other public sector 29.4% of on-prem revenue), not brands.[^33,32] Internationally, the company has opened offices in the Middle East, the UK, Singapore and Malaysia and pitches governments on "sovereign AI."[^34]

- {term: Enterprise clients (Sep 2025), def: "12,000+"}

Why this matters, and the counterpoint: the "sovereign AI alliance" framing is ambitious, but with overseas revenue at ~11.6% of a ~$105M base — and Southeast Asia contributing only ~$2.5M — the international business is still branding more than book.[^34] The durable asset is the domestic compute alliance, which the Entity List made strategically necessary.

## 09. What could break the thesis

A serious bear case does not require the models to be bad — they are good. It requires the *business and the valuation* to be the weak points. The strongest disconfirming facts:

- {flag:red} **Valuation.** A ~$112B market cap on ~$105M revenue is roughly 1,000x sales, and UBS attributes it to scarcity and lock-ups rather than fundamentals.[^13]
- {flag:red} **Unit economics.** A ¥4.72B statutory loss on ¥724M revenue, R&D at 439% of revenue, and a *negative* cloud-API gross margin in H1 2025.[^14,12]
- {flag:red} **Margin direction.** Gross margin compressed from 56.3% to 41.0% as revenue scaled — the opposite of software operating leverage.[^14]
- {flag:yellow} **Compute ceiling.** Domestic accelerators carry a ~4.1x power penalty at frontier scale, raising the cost of every future training run under the Entity List.[^29]
- {flag:yellow} **Commoditization.** Open-weight LLM prices are racing toward zero (DeepSeek V4 priced ~97% below GPT-5.5), yet Zhipu gives away MIT-licensed frontier weights while needing revenue; it even raised GLM-5.1 API prices by about 83% to escape the race, risking competitiveness.[^11,35]
- {flag:yellow} **Distribution.** Out of the consumer top five; behind Qwen on open-weight downloads.[^24,25]

:::statement(attr="ARA Research")
The models are frontier-class; the income statement and the share price are the speculative parts.
:::

The bull rebuttal deserves a fair hearing: frontier-lab losses are an investment phase, not distress; revenue more than doubled; customer concentration is falling and on-prem margins are healthy; cheap, abundant Chinese power offsets some of the silicon efficiency gap; and national-champion status brings sticky state demand and strategic capital that Western-exposed rivals lack.[^32,29] The case is genuinely two-sided. What is not in dispute is that the market has priced the optimistic branch almost in full.

## 10. Outlook

The forward thesis: Zhipu's next catalysts are financial-engineering and silicon, not just model releases. The company is pursuing a STAR Market (Shanghai) "H-to-A" listing targeting up to ¥15B, with Guotai Haitong and CICC as sponsors; its CSRC tutoring reached "acceptance" status on 17 June 2026, enabled by China's July-2025 expansion of STAR's pre-profit "fifth set" listing standards.[^30] A successful A-share float would add domestic liquidity and a second capital base — useful for a company burning cash and locked out of US markets.

Three scenarios frame the next two years. In the bull case, GLM stays at the open-weight frontier, the coding/agent products convert OpenRouter and developer mindshare into durable API revenue, and the A-share listing cements national-champion funding. In the base case, Zhipu remains a strong open-weight lab and a profitable-ish government systems-integrator, but the software multiple compresses toward the services reality. In the bear case, commoditization and the compute tax erode both margin and differentiation while the stock re-rates toward fundamentals. The deciding variables are concrete: whether cloud-API margins turn positive, whether domestic silicon can sustain frontier *training* economically, and whether the open-weight strategy can be monetized before the next cheaper model resets the price floor.[^14,29]

:::callout(kind=success, label="Red-team note")
The three load-bearing claims of this article — the ~$112B/1,600% valuation dislocation, the ~85% on-prem revenue mix, and the Entity-List effect with no Footnote 4 — were independently re-checked against primary or wire sources before publication.
:::

Why this matters, finally: Zhipu is the cleanest test case for a question the whole industry faces. If an elite lab can build frontier open models on sanctioned, less-efficient hardware but cannot turn that into software-grade economics, then the value of "frontier-class and open" is smaller than the market currently believes — in China and everywhere else.
