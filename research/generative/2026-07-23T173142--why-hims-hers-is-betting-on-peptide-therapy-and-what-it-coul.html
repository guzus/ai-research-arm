Why Hims & Hers Is Betting on Peptide Therapy—and What It Could Mean for Everyday Patients

A telehealth giant whose GLP-1 engine is stalling is positioning for the next wave of injectables — just as the FDA reconsiders seven unproven peptides.

On July 23–24, 2026, a little-known FDA advisory committee began reviewing whether seven injectable "research peptides" — compounds with names like BPC-157 and TB-500 — should be legal for compounding pharmacies to make and sell by prescription.12 Hims & Hers, the $2.3-billion telehealth platform, has spent eighteen months buying a peptide facility, hiring a longevity-medicine chief medical officer, and publicly cheering the review.91011 The stakes are real: Wall Street analysts think telehealth peptide prescribing could become a $2.2 billion market, and Hims alone could capture up to $440 million of it.3 But the committee's vote is advice, not approval — and the FDA's own career scientists have recommended against loosening access to every one of the seven compounds.12

01Short chains, long claims

Peptides are short chains of amino acids — smaller than proteins — that act as signaling molecules in the body, helping regulate the immune system, metabolism and other core functions.4 Some are among the most familiar medicines on earth: insulin is a peptide, and so are the blockbuster GLP-1 weight-loss drugs semaglutide (Ozempic, Wegovy) and tirzepatide, which are modified, longer-lasting versions of naturally occurring peptides.45 Those drugs went through years of phased human trials before the FDA approved them.

The peptides now in the spotlight are a different breed. BPC-157, derived from a protein fragment found in human stomach juice, is marketed online as the "wolverine peptide" for injury recovery; TB-500 is a synthetic relative of thymosin beta-4; others on this week's docket promise help with sleep, cognition, inflammation and metabolism.13 None of the seven has undergone the large-scale human trials required for FDA approval, and the evidence behind them — depending on the compound — ranges from thin to nonexistent.13 Yet demand has exploded, fed by podcasters and influencers such as Joe Rogan, and supply has flowed through a gray market of "research grade" vials labeled "not for human use," much of it sourced from overseas suppliers.35

02Approved drug vs. compounded drug: the distinction that decides everything

Everything in this story hinges on a regulatory seam most patients never see. An FDA-approved drug has passed phased clinical trials that establish safety, efficacy, dosing and labeled uses; the manufacturer can make specific, regulated health claims.3 A 503A compounded drug is something else entirely: a state-licensed pharmacy mixes a medication for an individual patient, based on a specific prescription, under Section 503A of the Food, Drug and Cosmetic Act. Compounded products are not tested or approved by the FDA, are held to lower regulatory standards, and exist legally to serve patients whose needs cannot be met by an approved drug — not as a mass-market alternative to one.56

Under a 1997 law, compounding pharmacies may only use bulk ingredients that are components of an approved drug, carry a USP monograph, or appear on an FDA-approved "bulks list."5 After a 2012 fungal-meningitis outbreak traced to a compounding facility killed more than 60 people, Congress tightened the rules and pushed the FDA to build that list.5 The agency sorted nominated substances into interim categories, and in 2023 it placed 19 popular research peptides in Category 2 — judged too risky for compounding, citing injectable-peptide immune-reaction risks, impurities, and a lack of human data.58 To date, only six substances have completed the full process onto the bulks list; none is a peptide.5

Read this before any headline

The July 23–24 meeting of the Pharmacy Compounding Advisory Committee is a non-binding review. The panel advises; the FDA decides, usually within months, and is not required to follow the recommendation.12 Even if the agency eventually moves a peptide to Category 1, that would make it legal to compound — it would not mean the FDA approved it as a drug.36

The politics are unusual. Health Secretary Robert F. Kennedy Jr. has called himself a "big fan" of peptides, says he has used them, and told Rogan in February 2026 that the 2023 restrictions were "illegal"; in February he moved 14 of the 19 peptides to a less restrictive category, though formal compounding access still requires this committee process.2519 In June, the FDA overhauled the committee's roster — several new members run peptide clinics, sell peptide injections or promote the substances online — and this week it added temporary academic voting members.12 The committee currently has just three standing voting members, six vacancies and one non-voting industry representative.3

03The Hims bet — and why it comes at a vulnerable moment

Hims & Hers has been laying peptide infrastructure in plain sight. In February 2025 it acquired a US-based peptide facility in California — adding to earlier 503A and 503B pharmacy acquisitions — and said the deal positioned it to explore "preventive health, metabolic optimization, cognitive performance, recovery science, and biological resistances."9 In April 2026 its global chief medical officer, Dr. Pat Carroll, publicly applauded the FDA's re-evaluation as a path to move peptides "out of the gray market, and into more trusted channels overseen by vetted healthcare professionals."10 In June it named Dr. Anant Vinjamoori, a Harvard-trained longevity-medicine specialist, as chief medical officer of the Hims brand, explicitly citing "longevity medicine and peptide therapy" among his remits; Vinjamoori has since said Hims plans to offer the peptides if the FDA reclassifies them.111

The commercial logic is easy to see, because the company's core engine is sputtering. Hims grew revenue 59% to $2.35 billion in 2025, with 2.51 million subscribers, powered substantially by personalized and compounded GLP-1 weight-loss products.12 Then the regulatory ground shifted: in September 2025 the FDA sent warning letters to roughly 50 marketers of compounded GLP-1s — Hims received two — and in February 2026 the agency issued a statement, naming Hims directly, signaling a crackdown on GLP-1 ingredients mass-marketed as alternatives to approved drugs.12 Novo Nordisk sued Hims for patent infringement on February 9, 2026, and the stock, which closed near $57 in September 2025, fell to a monthly close of $14.52 in February 2026.1213

04The evidence gap the FDA's own scientists keep pointing at

The uncomfortable core of this story is that the FDA's career reviewers looked at the same data the industry touts and recommended against compounding access for all seven peptides.12 Their late-June briefing documents found the supporting data comes primarily from animal studies and is "quite limited for humans."1 The committee is reviewing specific uses — ulcerative colitis for BPC-157; wound healing and inflammatory conditions for KPV; wound healing for TB-500; obesity and osteoporosis for MOTS-c; opioid withdrawal, insomnia and narcolepsy for emideltide; cerebral ischemia, migraine and trigeminal neuralgia for Semax; insomnia for epitalon.6

The safety concerns are structural, not hypothetical. Injectable peptides are engineered to resist the rapid breakdown natural peptides undergo, so the immune system can treat them as foreign — reactions can range from rashes to anaphylaxis — and injection bypasses the body's natural defenses; manufacturing can introduce bacteria or heavy metals, and peptides degrade if mishandled.5 The nonprofit Institute for Safe Medication Practices warned this year that the evidence base is mostly preclinical; its president, Rita Jew, put the risk arithmetic bluntly: "These peptides really do not have established effectiveness, so the only thing you have is risk."417 In a 2025 incident, two women became critically ill after injections of Category 2 peptides at a Las Vegas longevity conference.5 One Sermo poll of 500 physicians found roughly 90% worried about patients' self-directed peptide use, and the AMA has urged caution.31516

05The case for legalization — and its limits

The industry's strongest argument is not that the peptides are proven — it is that prohibition failed. When regulated compounders were pushed out, "demand didn't go away. It went into the underground," as podcaster Gary Brecka put it at an industry briefing.1 A state-licensed compounding pharmacy using FDA-registered ingredient suppliers is "a heck of a lot better" than chat-room-sourced vials, argues Scott Brunner of the Alliance for Pharmacy Compounding.5 And there is a real economic reason the trials never happened: many of these peptides are unpatentable natural sequences, so no company will fund the studies. "These products will likely never go through an FDA approval process," says industry attorney Lee Rosebush. "So without allowing compounding pharmacies to do this, patients will never get access."4

Each of those claims deserves scrutiny. Harm reduction addresses product quality — contamination, dosing accuracy — but says nothing about whether the drugs work or are safe at the pharmacological level; a cleaner vial of an inert or harmful compound is still an inert or harmful compound.5 The patent argument explains the missing trials but does not substitute for them; as former FDA acting commissioner Janet Woodcock warns, widening access without evidence "would be a disruption of the societal pact we have had since 1962 that drugs will be studied to see if they work before they are marketed in the U.S."5 And the anecdotal evidence cuts both ways: an industry survey of 508 peptide patients found 19% reported uncomfortable side effects.5 Vinjamoori's own framing concedes the gap even while defending access: "the evidence base is still developing... What exists is largely preclinical, but preclinical evidence isn't no evidence."3 His parallel claim of "close to a decade — thousands of physicians, close to millions of patients" of accumulated experience is precisely the kind of uncontrolled, testimonial-grade data regulators say cannot establish safety.15

06Legitimacy, access, or commercialization?

So could Hims make peptides legitimate — or just commercialize an under-tested trend? The honest answer separates three things that headlines will blur. Legality: a favorable committee vote, then an FDA category change, would make prescription compounding legal; the FDA usually follows its panels, and a final determination is expected within months, with five more peptides due for review by February 2027.126 Access quality: a platform with 2.58 million subscribers, its own 503A pharmacies and a domestic peptide facility would replace anonymous gray-market sellers with physician consultations, pharmaceutical-grade sourcing and adverse-event accountability — a genuine upgrade over the status quo.913 Evidence: nothing in this process generates it. If the FDA reclassifies the peptides, clinicians could legally prescribe them well beyond the narrow reviewed uses — the drugs would remain unapproved, and off-label discretion would rest with prescribers.16

What patients should watch for
  • A committee vote is a recommendation, not an FDA decision — final determinations typically follow in three to six months.2
  • "Legal to compound" never equals "FDA approved"; compounded peptides carry no efficacy guarantee and lower oversight standards.56
  • Doctors who prescribe off-label owe you candor about sparse human data; medical providers should be honest about the "relatively sparse clinical evidence," as even Hims's own CMO says.1
  • Products sold online without a prescription remain illegal regardless of the outcome — the gray market does not become legal.5

Hims's own history is the best evidence for both readings. The bull case: no company has done more to normalize regulated, physician-gated access to stigmatized care, and its verticalized supply chain could set a quality floor the gray market never will.912 The bear case: this is the same company that kept mass-marketing "personalized" compounded GLP-1s after the shortage ended until the FDA named it directly — a pattern that suggests commercial incentives can outrun clinical caution.12 The Alabama Board of Medical Examiners has already warned physicians against "recommending, supplying, prescribing or administering" unapproved peptides, and copycat telehealth startups are raising money on the same thesis.14 Whether Hims becomes the peptide market's sheriff or its loudest storefront depends less on this week's vote than on choices the company makes after it.
