## 01. A 2,069% premium and a 95-year-old record broken

Closed-end funds are supposed to trade at a 5-15% discount to net asset value. The mechanism is textbook: when a CEF's market price exceeds the value of its underlying portfolio, arbitrageurs short the fund, buy the components, and pocket the spread until the gap closes. The discount is what keeps the system honest. Destiny Tech100 walked into the public market on March 26, 2024 and detonated this entire mechanism.

The numbers from the listing week read like a misprint. NYSE set DXYZ's reference price at $4.84, anchored to a March 2024 NAV of roughly that figure [^1][^2]. The stock opened at $8.25 (+70.5%) and closed its first session at $9.00 on just 540,000 shares — already at an 86% premium to NAV before any real demand materialized [^3]. Over the following nine trading days, the price ran to an intraday peak of **$105.00 on April 8, 2024**, against a quarter-end NAV of $5.07 per share. That works out to a **~1,970-2,069% premium** depending on which NAV anchor you use [^4][^5]. Owen Lamont, writing for Acadian, documented this as the highest closed-end-fund premium in U.S. history — eclipsing the prior record of 1,235% set in 1929, when the same retail-mania mechanics produced the leveraged trusts that helped crater the Dow [^5].

The mechanic Lamont identified is one of those rare moments where a single dataset cleanly violates the Law of One Price. In April 2024, Baron Partners Fund — a publicly traded mutual fund with a meaningful SpaceX position — implied a SpaceX share price of roughly **$97** to any retail investor buying Baron Partners. A retail investor buying DXYZ at the $105 print was implicitly paying **$864 per SpaceX share** for the slice of SpaceX inside DXYZ's portfolio [^5]. Same underlying asset, two publicly tradable wrappers, a 9x price gap. Not a thesis difference. Not a tax difference. A pure mechanical failure to converge.

:::quote(attr="Owen Lamont, Acadian Asset Management, Apr 2024")
DXYZ represents a 2,000% violation of the Law of One Price. The previous record was set in 1929. The conditions that produced it then are present now: noise-trader demand, prohibitive arbitrage costs, and belief heterogeneity amplified by social media.
:::

What followed was not arbitrage. The premium compressed to ~900% by April 16, ~810% by year-end 2024, ~500% through 2025, and ~116% by mid-May 2026 [^4][^6][^7]. Compression came from two places: the underlying portfolio repriced upward as SpaceX, OpenAI, and Anthropic each marked dramatically higher in 2025 secondary rounds, and Destiny finally activated a $1B at-the-market shelf and started selling shares into the demand it had bottled up. The premium is being closed by issuance, not by shorts. That distinction matters — and it shapes everything that follows.

## 02. Inside the basket: "Tech100" in name only

The marketing tagline implies a passive index of the 100 leading private tech names. The reality at year-end 2025 is **roughly 32 underlying portfolio companies** (about 38 fund-level line items once SPV share classes are split), and almost half of the fund's investments by value are sitting in a Treasury money market [^8]. The fund's October 14, 2021 launch announcement explicitly promised "a portfolio of the top 100 venture-backed private technology companies" [^9]. Four-plus years and two years of public trading later, Sohail Prasad reframes the 100 as a forward "target" rather than a delivered product [^10].

The Schedule of Investments in the FY2025 N-CSR — the canonical disclosure — breaks NAV into six buckets [^8]:

The 36.26% in SPVs is the operating reality: DXYZ does not own SpaceX or OpenAI shares directly. It holds **interests in SPVs** that hold the underlying securities, and in OpenAI's case, the SPV holds "Profit Participation Units" rather than equity — DXYZ OAI I LLC, acquired 12/18/23, fair value $9.03M against $2.01M cost (~4.5x), routed through a footnote chain that the auditor flags as an SPV-of-SPV structure [^8]. Anthropic exposure was *not* in the YE2025 portfolio; it was added in January 2026 as Destiny's "largest investment in the history of the company" — a $100M+ SPV that immediately attracted Bloomberg scrutiny over its accounting treatment [^12].

Two facts compress the brand-versus-reality gap. First, the **xAI exposure is distinct from X/Twitter** — DXYZ owns X.AI Corp Series E Preferred via Snowpoint Growth 2.6 LLC, not the social-media entity that retail often conflates with it [^8]. Second, names retail investors think they're buying — Anthropic at YE2025, X/Twitter ever, Reddit, Instacart, Chainalysis, Yuga Labs — were **not in the portfolio** as of the most recent audited disclosure [^8]. The fund is concentrated, not diversified; it is concentrated in SpaceX and an emerging AI-defense sleeve (Shield AI, xAI, Skild AI, Beast Industries, OpenEvidence) acquired in a Q4 2025 deployment spree of roughly $15M per name [^8].

This concentration is the single most important fact the "Tech100" name obscures. A 50% drawdown in SpaceX alone would impair roughly **8% of NAV before contagion** [^8]; the Aviation/Aerospace bucket was 54.47% of NAV at June 30, 2025 before the ATM-funded diversification push, so the structural exposure to SpaceX repricings has been larger than the year-end 16% figure suggests [^13]. The "Rhenium Bolt 2021" SPV — 3.86% of NAV, no underlying asset since 2024 disposition — is essentially a residual fair-value claim hanging in the schedule, which says something about how granular the audit gets on Level 3 SPV residuals [^8].

What is not in dispute: this is a concentrated AI/aerospace bet wrapped in cash, sold under a "100 names" label, marked quarterly by a process the manager has discretion over.

## 03. The architecture of impossible arbitrage

CEF premiums die for the same reason most market anomalies die: somebody can profit from killing them. A trader shorts the over-priced fund, buys the underlying portfolio, and waits for the gap to close. DXYZ broke this loop on three independent margins, and the breakdown is what kept the premium at 500%+ for the better part of two years.

The first margin is **borrow**. Closed-end funds are held overwhelmingly by retail investors through broker-dealer accounts — they are not held by the natural lenders that supply the securities-lending market (insurance balance sheets, mutual fund cash-collateral pools, index funds). With no natural lender, there is no borrow; with no borrow, there is no short. In mid-April 2024, the Interactive Brokers borrow fee on DXYZ was reportedly **above 100% annualized** — Lamont's calculation from a quoted 30¢/day fee on a $105 share — and brief intraday quotes reached 200%+ [^5]. A short paying 100% APR on a fund with a 2,000% premium still has positive expected value, but the carry burns through capital faster than most arbitrageurs can sustain.

The second margin is **float**. Destiny issued **zero new shares for 21 months** after listing. The share count sat at 10,879,905 from before the listing through the end of Q3 2025 — a deliberate decision by management, given the shelf registration filed within weeks of the squeeze peak [^14]. With a fixed share count, retail demand pressed against an inelastic supply curve. The borrow that did exist on the secondary lending market got rationed by price, not quantity.

The third margin is **gating**. In May 2024, Robinhood removed the buy button for DXYZ while leaving sells enabled — a deliberate broker-level circuit breaker on the premium [^15]. Prasad attacked the move publicly ("Don't build walls where there were bridges @RobinhoodApp"), but the practical effect was to remove the marginal new buyer at exactly the moment the premium needed continuous retail inflow to defend itself. Robinhood later relisted DXYZ in October 2025 — same week Prasad nominated Robinhood's founding COO, Nate Rodland, to the DXYZ board [^15][^16]. The choreography speaks for itself.

The short-interest data captures the regime change. In August 2024, DXYZ short interest was 239,100 shares — less than 1% of float. Through 2025 it oscillated between 600,000 and 1.4M shares as borrow gradually normalized. Then in early 2026 the borrow loosened materially, the SpaceX IPO chatter intensified, and short interest **quadrupled in 90 days** — from 651,597 shares on 1/30/26 to 3,034,399 on 4/15/26, or 27.9% of free float, with days-to-cover under 1.5 [^17]. That is squeeze fuel built up in the opposite direction: shorts are now structurally crowded into a tight float, and the May 8 spike to $71.24 (a +21% gap up after a seven-session run from $29) was the consequence [^18][^19].

:::callout(warn, label="Limits to arbitrage, applied")
DXYZ is the cleanest modern textbook case of De Long-Shleifer-Summers-Waldmann noise-trader risk. The premium did not arbitrage away because the arbitrage was literally impossible. It is compressing now only because Destiny itself is finally issuing supply via the $1B ATM — endogenous supply response, not exogenous short-sale pressure.
:::

The structural diagnosis matters because it tells you what is actually happening in 2026. The premium is no longer being squeezed by retail demand against a fixed float. It is being managed downward by management issuing shares above NAV, capturing the spread, and accruing the accretion to existing holders. The shorts in the May 2026 print are betting that the ATM overhang plus normalized borrow will finally complete the compression. Bulls are betting that Anthropic's next round and a SpaceX IPO will keep marking NAV up faster than the ATM can dilute the premium. Both are correct, structurally — and the path between them is going to be violent in either direction.

## 04. The wrapper plumbing: fees, conflicts, and Marcum

The wrapper architecture explains where the economics flow once you strip the premium narrative. Destiny Tech100 Inc. is the Maryland-domiciled, 1940 Act-registered closed-end fund (SEC file 811-23802, CIK 0001843974); its investment adviser is Destiny Advisors LLC; Destiny Advisors LLC is wholly owned by Destiny XYZ Inc., which is in turn wholly owned by Sohail Prasad [^20]. The 2.50% annualized management fee — paid quarterly on **average gross assets**, not net or invested capital — flows from the fund to the adviser to the holding company to Prasad personally. On the year-end 2025 NAV of $438M, that fee runs at roughly **$11M/year**; on the 3/31/26 portfolio value of $742.5M, closer to **$18.5M/year** [^8][^11].

The fee structure is unusual in three ways. First, **2.50% is among the highest base management fees in registered fund-land** — diversified closed-end funds typically charge 1.0-1.5%, and equity ETFs charge 0.05-0.50%. Second, it is **on gross assets including leverage** — the fund can borrow up to the 1940 Act 200%/300% asset-coverage limit and the adviser still earns the fee on the borrowed dollars. Third, the prospectus discloses but does not eliminate a stack of related-party conflicts that flow directly from Prasad's prior life as a Forge Global co-founder [^21].

The N-2/A is unusually candid. From the Conflicts of Interest section: *"Mr. Prasad is a shareholder of Forge and SharesPost, preeminent private securities marketplaces, which we may utilize as a means to acquire equity and equity-related interests, subject to our best execution policy"* [^21]. Forge Global and SharesPost (now merged) are the secondary marketplaces DXYZ uses to source its private positions. Each transaction generates fees for the marketplace — and Prasad owns equity in the marketplace [^22]. The mitigation is a "best execution policy" applied by an Investment Committee whose sole member is Prasad himself.

:::quote(attr="Destiny Tech100 Inc., Form N-2/A Risk Factors, 2025")
The determination of fair value, and thus the amount of unrealized appreciation or depreciation we may recognize in any reporting period, is to a degree subjective, and our Adviser has a conflict of interest in making recommendations of fair value... The determinations of fair value... may differ materially from the values that would have been used if an active market and market quotations existed for such investments.
:::

The valuation conflict is also disclosed in the prospectus, not hidden. The adviser proposes fair values quarterly; the board — three independent directors (Travis Mason, Lisa Nelson, Lee Daley) and one interested director (Prasad) — approves them with input from a third-party valuation firm that "assists" rather than independently marks the book [^21]. Marcum LLP, the auditor since 2022, audits the *process* of fair-value determination — not the marks themselves [^8]. Marcum is the same audit firm the SEC sanctioned with a $10M penalty in June 2023 for systemic quality-control failures across hundreds of SPAC audits (SEC Release 2023-114) — a fact worth knowing when underwriting comfort that "the auditor signed off" on Level 3 marks [^23].

:::callout(danger, label="Related-party stack")
Single founder controls the adviser, sits on the investment committee, owns equity in the trading venues, and operates without a performance fee that would create skin-in-the-game on bad marks. The prospectus discloses each piece; nothing in the structure eliminates the conflict, only documents it.
:::

The capital structure makes the dynamics worse, not better. There is no incentive/performance fee at the fund level — Prasad explicitly emphasized in his May 12, 2026 defense of the Anthropic SPV that *"there are no ongoing management fees or carried interest on the investment"* [^12]. That sounds shareholder-friendly until you notice the implication: the adviser is paid on AUM, regardless of whether marks are realized. NAV markups grow the fee base. Realizations do not. The structural incentive is to add and hold marks, not to liquidate at the marks.

What pulls in the other direction is reputation and the visible track record of Prasad's prior public vehicle, Forge Global (NYSE: FRGE). Forge went public via SPAC in March 2022 at peak market value ~$6.4B and fell to ~$229M in fall 2022 — **a >95% drawdown** [^24]. The 2025 partial recovery (+231% on a trailing-365-day basis at one snapshot) does not change the post-listing base rate for buy-and-hold investors. Same founder, same theme (retail access to private markets), now running DXYZ. That is the comp.

## 05. The unlock: $1B ATM and the doubling of supply

For 21 months Destiny refused to issue new shares. The shelf was filed within weeks of the April 2024 squeeze peak; nothing was actually drawn until late 2025 [^25][^26]. The corporate-action timing is the cleanest evidence that the premium was being deliberately preserved — and the eventual reversal is the cleanest evidence that the strategy reached its breaking point.

The ATM economics are extraordinary in retrospect. Q4 2025: roughly $244.6M raised against a $70M NAV at the start of the period — the issuance was on the order of the fund's entire pre-issuance net asset base, sold into a market where the price was 3-5x NAV [^11]. The issuance at premium is **accretive to NAV per share** for existing holders, which is the management argument and is mathematically correct: if you sell new shares above NAV, the new investors fund some of your existing NAV. Q1 2026 added another 8.49M shares at a weighted-average price of $28.76 for net proceeds of ~$244.1M, when end-of-2025 NAV was $19.97/sh — another ~44% capture spread.

The visible NAV growth — from $6.44/sh at YE2024 to $19.93 at YE2025 to $24.56 at 3/31/26 — is largely accretion from issuing into the premium, not portfolio repricing. SpaceX's mark-up from the December 2025 tender at $800B contributed materially, as did the Anthropic SPV in January 2026, but the largest single driver was the math of selling 8.49M shares at $28.76 each when the existing NAV was $19.97. The share count grew 33% YoY, but the NAV/share grew faster — that is the textbook accretion machine running cleanly.

The strategic question is whether the ATM is run as a permanent feature or as a one-time supply response. The May 11, 2026 prospectus supplement maintained the full $1.0B authorization, of which only ~$300M has been deployed [^26]. At current prices that residual authorization equals roughly **35M more shares** at $28+ versus the current 14.4M outstanding — a 2.4x potential dilution overhang that the premium has to absorb if Destiny chooses to deploy it. Management has not signaled a deployment pace. They do not need to: the option value of an open ATM at a 116% premium is itself a structural cap on how high the premium can run.

## 06. Look-through value: what the NAV actually tracks

NAV per share grew from $6.44 at YE 2024 to $19.93 at YE 2025 to **$24.56 at 3/31/26** [^11]. About half of that growth was accretion from ATM issuance above NAV; the other half was real portfolio repricing driven by a cluster of private-market valuation step-ups in DXYZ's largest underlying positions. Understanding what the NAV represents requires walking through those step-ups in detail, because the marks are the entire investment case.

The SpaceX repricing alone drove most of the visible NAV uplift in the second half of 2025. DXYZ SpaceX I LLC's fair value rose from $29.3M at June 30, 2025 to **$54.5M at December 31, 2025** — +86% in six months — while MWAM VC SpaceX-II added another $15.9M [^8][^13]. That single position contributed ~$25M to NAV growth in H2 2025, against an $11M management fee accrual on the full fund. The $400B → $800B private mark-up was the largest single quarter-over-quarter step in SpaceX's secondary history.

Anthropic is the more nuanced story. The position was added in **January 2026** as DXYZ's largest single investment ever — a $100M+ commitment via a DXYZ-controlled SPV. Q4 2025 NAV was reported up "76% QoQ and 210% YoY" largely because of the Anthropic mark — a magnitude that, in the words of one buy-side note, "is itself a red flag for an unaudited closed-end fund of Level 3 private positions." Bloomberg's reporting questioned the SPV's accounting treatment, prompting Prasad to publicly defend the structure on X [^12]:

:::quote(attr="Sohail Prasad, CEO Destiny Tech100, May 12 2026")
This investment was made after thorough diligence of the investment structure and the underlying documentation. There are no ongoing management fees or carried interest on the investment. We are confident in the investment and its accounting treatment, which is consistent with applicable methodologies. Any reporting that implies otherwise lacks factual knowledge of our actual investment.
:::

The OpenAI exposure is materially smaller than retail typically assumes. DXYZ OAI I LLC, the holding vehicle, owns **Profit Participation Units** — not equity — and was carried at $9.03M (2.06% of NAV) at YE2025 against a $2.01M cost [^8]. Profit Participation Units are the contractual instrument OpenAI uses to share its capped-profit returns; they trade in secondary markets at substantial discounts to implied equity value because of structural caps and uncertain capture rates on any IPO conversion. The October 2025 secondary at $500B and the March 2026 primary at $852B both lift the *implied* value of Profit Participation Units, but the mark-up flows through the SPV layer with a discount, and the audit treatment of that discount is one of the items Bloomberg pressed on.

There are also write-downs. The same FY2025 N-CSR that captured the SpaceX mark-up captured a series of brutal markdowns on positions acquired during the accredited-only era. **Impossible Foods**: $3.37M cost vs $0.23M fair value (-93%). **Automation Anywhere**: $2.61M cost vs $0.34M FV (-87%). **Flexport**: $0.52M cost vs $0.08M FV (-84%). **Klarna common** (pre-IPO basis): $4.66M cost vs $1.07M FV (-77%) — and Klarna IPO'd in 2025, so this is now a marked-to-market loss, not a Level 3 guess. **Plaid SPV** -67%, **Discord** -59% [^8][^21]. These markdowns are real, audited, and tell you that the SPV portfolio is not a one-way bet on AI compute — it is a venture book with the full venture-book mortality profile, just with the wins concentrated in two enormous names.

The honest take on NAV is this: roughly half of the 2025-2026 NAV growth was accretion engineering (selling shares above NAV), and the other half is genuine repricing concentrated in SpaceX, Anthropic, and OpenAI marks that themselves rest on secondary-tender prices set by a relatively small set of insiders and Thrive-led syndicates. If those marks hold, the NAV is real. If a SpaceX IPO prices below $800B, or an Anthropic round comes in below $400B, the marks compress immediately and visibly.

## 07. Peers and counterfactuals: same assets, different wrappers

What DXYZ delivers — retail-accessible, exchange-listed exposure to a basket of pre-IPO tech — is genuinely scarce. But the scarcity is in the *wrapper structure*, not the underlying assets. SpaceX appears in DXYZ (16.07%), in ARK Venture Fund (~11% by recent N-CSR disclosure), and in Fundrise Innovation Fund (5.0%). OpenAI appears in DXYZ (2.06% via PPUs), ARKVX (~3% via current filings), and VCX (9.9%) [^29][^28]. Anthropic appears in VCX as the **top position at 20.7%** and in ARKVX, while DXYZ added it only in January 2026. The differentiation that matters is the wrapper economics.

| Vehicle | Listed? | Pricing | Redemption | Total ER | Accredited only? |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| * DXYZ (CEF direct listing) | NYSE | Market price (premium peaked >2,069%) | Sell on exchange daily | 4.98–6.28% | No |
| ARKVX (interval fund) | None | Daily NAV | Quarterly tender at NAV, ~5% cap | 2.90% net | No (~$500 min) |
| VCX (Fundrise) | NYSE (since 3/19/26) | Market price now; was NAV pre-listing | Exchange-sale | 1.85% mgmt | No |
| PEAX (Hamilton Lane) | None | Monthly NAV | Board-discretionary tender | 3.38–4.08% | Suitability-gated |
| Forge / EquityZen / Hiive | N/A | Negotiated bid/ask | Hold to IPO | Per-deal carry | Yes |

The fee column understates DXYZ's cost: total expense ratio in the FY2024 N-CSR was 4.98%, and recent disclosures range 4.98-6.28% depending on which expense base is used [^1][^21]. ARK Venture Fund's net 2.90% (gross 3.49%) is materially cheaper, and Fundrise Innovation Fund's 1.85% management fee is less than half DXYZ's headline 2.50% — though VCX's recent NYSE listing has introduced premium/discount volatility of its own, with a reported ~45% drawdown from initial post-listing price as the "fresh-listing frenzy" faded [^27][^28].

The deeper distinction is **pricing mechanism**. ARKVX, the closest substantive comp, is a *continuously offered closed-end interval fund* that does not list on an exchange — investors transact at daily NAV via Titan and select brokerages, redemptions happen quarterly via Rule 23c-3 repurchase offers at NAV, and the price simply cannot detach from the underlying portfolio value [^29][^30]. PEAX (Hamilton Lane) uses the *tender offer fund* mechanism — monthly NAV-priced subscriptions, with board-discretionary tender offers rather than the shareholder-right redemptions of an interval fund. Tender funds give managers more flexibility but give shareholders less protection [^31].

DXYZ chose neither. As a direct-listed CEF, it gets to hold ~98% private illiquid assets (no redemption obligation ever) and gives shareholders exchange-traded liquidity (sell on NYSE anytime). The cost is the persistent premium/discount risk — which played out in spectacular form in 2024. **This combination of (private holdings + exchange liquidity) is what makes DXYZ structurally unique**: ARKVX has the private holdings but no exchange liquidity; secondary marketplaces (Forge, EquityZen, Hiive) have the underlying exposure but only for accredited investors at $5,000-25,000 minimums [^32].

:::callout(info, label="The wrapper is the trade")
Bullish case: DXYZ is the only liquid, fractional, no-accreditation-required public vehicle that holds a basket of pre-IPO megacaps. That access is genuinely scarce and the premium reflects scarcity rent on the wrapper.
Bearish case: The same assets are available cheaper through ARKVX (no premium, half the fees) or through ARK Innovation ETF and IPO ETF for post-IPO exposure. The premium is paying for daily exchange liquidity on assets that almost no shareholder actually redeems intra-day.
:::

Pershing Square's two vehicles offer the clearest mechanical contrast. PSTH — Ackman's SPAC — peaked at a **46% premium** to its $20 cash trust before the UMG deal was blocked and capital returned at NAV. Pershing Square Holdings (PSH.AS), Ackman's actual closed-end fund holding public equities, currently trades at a **35-40% discount** to NAV. Same manager, same brand, premium and discount of completely different magnitudes — driven by structure. DXYZ's ~116% current premium (and historical ~2,069% peak) is not in the same equivalence class as either; it sits beyond the historical range of even retail-driven CEF mispricings, which is what makes the "limits to arbitrage" diagnosis structural rather than incidental.

## 08. What could break the thesis

Three thesis-breakers are worth taking seriously, plus a fourth that the prospectus already concedes.

**The SpaceX-IPO mismatch risk.** If SpaceX prices an IPO in 2026 *below* the December 2025 $800B private tender, the largest single DXYZ position re-rates downward on the day of pricing. The bull narrative — Musk targeting $1.5T at IPO — assumes the public market validates or extends the private mark [^33]. The base rate is the opposite: 70% of unicorn IPOs since 2021 priced below their last private round, including Instacart, Klaviyo, and Reddit. A SpaceX IPO at $1.0-1.2T is bullish in headline ($800B → $1.1T) but bearish for DXYZ's premium because the underlying becomes liquid [^33], *and the wrapper has nothing scarce to offer anymore*. The premium thesis depends on SpaceX staying private.

**The Anthropic-SPV accounting risk.** Bloomberg's May 2026 reporting on the $100M+ Anthropic SPV — DXYZ's largest single investment, marked at a 76% Q/Q NAV gain — pressed on the accounting treatment because the SPV structure may amplify gains relative to the underlying equity's mark-to-market. Prasad's defense was the categorical denial that "any reporting that implies otherwise lacks factual knowledge of our actual investment" [^12]. The SEC has not opened a public action. But a single staff comment letter requiring restatement of Q4 2025 or Q1 2026 NAV would unwind a measurable fraction of the visible NAV gain and would arrive without warning.

**The Forge precedent (FRGE).** Prasad's prior public vehicle, Forge Global, listed via SPAC in March 2022 at peak market value of ~$6.4B and fell to ~$229M in fall 2022, a **>95% drawdown** [^24]. Cash burn took Forge's liquid reserves from ~$207M to ~$93M between 2022 and 2025. The 2025 partial recovery does not change the post-listing base rate for a buy-and-hold investor. Same founder, same theme (retail access to private markets), the same broad backer pool that anchored both vehicles. The base rate matters.

**The Ramadurgam control dispute.** On January 23, 2024 — two months before NYSE listing — Forge co-founder and former D/XYZ co-CEO Samvit Ramadurgam sued Prasad and Destiny XYZ Inc. in Delaware Chancery (Case 2024-0057), alleging he was fired in November 2023 and stripped of a ~32% stake in the parent through an "improper stock-split scheme" [^34]. The complaint targets the parent and individuals, not the fund — but a court reversing the dilution and reinstating a 32% stake in Destiny XYZ Inc. would create a material ownership-change overhang at the adviser, and the litigation has the lifespan of Delaware Chancery (~12-24 months).

The same N-CSR that captured the SpaceX mark-up captured the writedowns that show the venture book functioning as a venture book:

:::rank-list
- {label: "Impossible Foods", value: "-93%", pct: 100, highlight: true}
- {label: "Automation Anywhere", value: "-87%", pct: 94}
- {label: "Flexport", value: "-84%", pct: 90}

These six positions together represent roughly $14.9M of cost basis written down to roughly $2.2M of fair value — net $12.7M of realized markdowns absorbed by SpaceX and Anthropic gains. The point is not that the writedowns are large in dollars (they are not, relative to a $438M NAV), but that the manager's stock-selection skill — outside the SpaceX/OpenAI/Anthropic megacap names — looks closer to base-rate venture mortality than to the alpha the Tech100 branding implies.

Prasad's counter-argument to the premium-narrows-toward-discount base case is to attack the peer interval funds for offering "fake liquidity," and the framing is worth quoting in full because it tells you how management thinks about the structural debate:

:::quote(attr="Sohail Prasad, CEO Destiny Tech100, Dec 1 2025 (responding to ARK)")
From end of Q3 2024 to end of Q3 2025, $DXYZ NAV is +114%, and market price is also +114% (rounded to the nearest %). All while being liquid, with over $9B traded on the @NYSE in that timeframe. Didn't you only provide your $ARKVX shareholders $20M in total liquidity for your ENTIRE fiscal year, and block some shareholder repurchases in Q3 2024? Seems like FAKE LIQUIDITY to me.
:::

The framing is rhetorically effective but factually slippery: ARKVX shareholders transact at NAV and have a contractual right to quarterly redemption at NAV, whereas DXYZ shareholders transact at whatever premium or discount the market sets, with no right to redeem at NAV ever. "$9B traded on the NYSE" is true and "DXYZ NAV +114% matches market price +114%" is true on one rolling window, but the market price still sits 116% above NAV and there is no structural commitment that it will not drift back to discount as the ATM keeps issuing.

:::callout(danger, label="What would falsify the bull case")
A SpaceX IPO priced below $1T; an SEC staff letter requiring restatement of the Anthropic SPV mark; full deployment of the remaining ~$700M ATM authorization (which would 4x the share count at current prices); a Delaware Chancery ruling that reinstates Ramadurgam's 32% Destiny XYZ stake. Any one of these compresses the premium toward parity. Two of them compress it past parity into discount.
:::

## 09. Counter-arguments and the path forward

The single strongest argument *for* DXYZ at $53 against $24.56 NAV is that the premium is no longer an arbitrage failure — it is option value. The fund holds a basket whose underlying marks are advancing measurably faster than the share count can dilute. Q1 2026 NAV grew 23% Q/Q. If Anthropic prices at $900B+ in 2026 H2, if SpaceX IPOs at $1.2T+, and if OpenAI's $852B holds through any near-term mark, the NAV could plausibly reach $35-40/sh by year-end 2026 — at which point the *current* $53 price implies roughly 35-50% premium, well inside historical CEF mispricing ranges and much more defensible. The premium is paying for forward-mark optionality, and forward marks have direction.

The single strongest argument *against* is the supply response. Management has demonstrated, via the Q4 2025 and Q1 2026 ATM tranches, that they will issue into demand at any premium above zero. The remaining ~$700M ATM authorization is roughly 3-4x the current outstanding share count at recent prices. Even on the bull case for NAV growth, management's ability to absorb that demand through issuance puts a structural cap on how high the price can run — and every share issued is accretive to NAV but dilutive to the premium. The "limits to arbitrage" condition that enabled the original 2,069% premium has fundamentally changed: borrow has normalized to ~27% short interest, float has tripled, and Robinhood (whose former COO now sits on the DXYZ board) has restored buy access. The conditions that made the squeeze impossible are gone.

The clean trade thesis, if there is one, runs through the calendar of underlying marks. A confirmed SpaceX IPO date [^33], the Anthropic next round priced above $900B [^35], an OpenAI primary that holds the $852B mark [^36] — each is a discrete NAV-uplift event that the ATM cannot anticipate. The asymmetry favors holders into those events and disfavors them after. The "buy and hold for three years" thesis runs into the headwind of the ATM gradually pulling the premium toward discount; the "trade around catalysts" thesis runs into the friction of a 4.74-beta name where intraday moves of ±15% are routine [^19].

What we know with high confidence: the wrapper is structurally expensive (4.98-6.28% total expense ratio against ARKVX's 2.90% net), the manager has demonstrated willingness to issue at premium (Q4 2025 and Q1 2026 ATM tranches), the largest underlying positions have repriced enormously over twelve months, and the legal and accounting overhangs (Ramadurgam, Bloomberg's Anthropic scrutiny, Marcum's audit history) are non-zero. What we do not know: where the premium settles when the ATM is fully deployed, how the SpaceX IPO will price, whether the next NAV markup absorbs the next ATM tranche, and whether the SEC takes a position on the Level 3 valuation framework. Those four unknowns are the entire investment.

What is most worth saying clearly: this is not an index fund. It is a concentrated bet on three private megacaps (SpaceX, Anthropic, OpenAI), held through SPV layers, marked by an adviser whose economic interests are disclosed but not eliminated, in a wrapper whose price has detached from NAV by historic margins and is now being deliberately re-anchored by issuance. None of that is hidden. All of it is documented. The market keeps paying the premium anyway — and the only honest reason it does is that, for retail investors who cannot access secondary marketplaces directly, there is no closer substitute. The premium is the access tax. The question is what it ought to be, and whether $24.56 of NAV plus that access tax is worth $53.
