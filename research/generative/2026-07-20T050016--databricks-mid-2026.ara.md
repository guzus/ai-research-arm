## 01. The IPO that isn't happening, on purpose

Databricks is not failing to go public. It is rationally declining to. The distinction matters, because the company now has three functioning substitutes for an IPO: abundant private capital at escalating marks, employee liquidity built into the rounds themselves, and a debt market willing to lend against its revenue. The Series L alone expanded from >$4 billion at announcement in December 2025 to roughly $5 billion of equity plus ~$2 billion of credit capacity — led by JPMorgan Chase alongside Barclays, Citi, Goldman Sachs and Morgan Stanley — by the February 2026 close.[^4,5] Both the Series L and the February top-up explicitly earmarked proceeds for employee liquidity.[^4,5]

The ladder is steep. Databricks raised a record $10 billion Series J at $62 billion in December 2024; a $1 billion Series K at over $100 billion in September 2025; the >$4 billion Series L at $134 billion three months later; and now the $188 billion strategic round.[^1,5,6,22] Investors in the December round included Insight Partners, Fidelity, J.P. Morgan Asset Management, Andreessen Horowitz, BlackRock, Blackstone, Coatue, GIC, Temasek and Thrive — a cap table that already looks like an index.[^5]

CEO Ali Ghodsi has told investors the company is IPO-bound, potentially as soon as 2027, and called 2026 — with SpaceX's record $2 trillion debut in June and confidential filings from OpenAI and Anthropic — the "worst year" to list, citing the crowded calendar.[^7,12,27,28] Read that as pricing discipline, not reluctance: at $188 billion the private market values Databricks at roughly 27 times its $6.9 billion annualized revenue, while Snowflake — the closest public comp — traded at an ~$83 billion market cap on about $5.6 billion of annualized revenue in mid-June, roughly 15 times.[^7] A 2026 IPO would force a public re-rating of that premium; a 2027 IPO lets another year of 65–80% growth catch up to the mark.

:::callout(kind=warn, label=Caveat)
Private marks are set by the marginal buyer in a structured round, not by continuous price discovery. The $188 billion figure comes from a signed term sheet, not a closed round, and Databricks itself emphasized the money "isn't in its hands yet."[^1,3] Treat it as a negotiated ceiling, not a market price.
:::

## 02. The revenue engine, and what "AI revenue" actually is

The growth series is the strongest part of the story. Databricks crossed a $4 billion revenue run rate in its fiscal Q2 (reported September 2025), growing >50% year over year; $4.8 billion in Q3 at >55%; $5.4 billion in Q4 at >65%; then $6.9 billion annualized by June 2026 at >80%.[^4,5,6,7] Growth is accelerating at scale — an unusual shape for a company this size, and the core bull argument.

The classic analytics lines are not shrinking to make room: the data warehousing product passed a $1 billion run rate in under four years from general availability, and Lakebase — the serverless Postgres built from the ~$1 billion Neon acquisition of May 2025 — had thousands of customers in its first six months and was growing revenue at twice the pace of the warehousing product.[^5,17]

The customer cohort data says the expansion is broad, not a few whale contracts. Net revenue retention has sustained above 140% for three consecutive disclosures; more than 800 customers consume at a $1 million-plus annual run rate and over 70 at $10 million-plus, up from 650 at the $1 million mark in September 2025.[^4,5,6] The company claims 20,000-plus organizations and 70% of the Fortune 500 as customers, and — rare in the 2026 AI cohort — positive free cash flow over the last twelve months, a streak it has now reported for three straight quarters.[^1,4,5,6]

The June 2026 Data + AI Summit filled out the product surface behind those numbers: Genie One, an "agentic coworker" for business teams with a self-updating context layer called Genie Ontology, went generally available with consumption-only pricing; Lakehouse//RT, a real-time engine promising sub-100-millisecond queries at 12,000 queries per second, entered beta; and the company pushed into verticals with the CustomerLake marketing CDP, the Lakewatch security line launched in March, and an agreement to acquire detection startup Panther.[^7,8,9]

:::callout(kind=info, label="Definition risk")
The "$1.7 billion AI revenue" figure is unaudited and its boundary is drawn by the seller. Model-serving revenue includes pass-through inference spend, and platform deals increasingly bundle AI credits. The direction is credible; the precision is marketing.[^4,7]
:::

## 03. The margin paradox

The most important sentence Ghodsi said at the June summit was not about growth. He told CNBC that gross margin "will go lower," and explained why: "It's the consumption-based business model, agentic AI coming. The agents are generating way more queries."[^7] Every autonomous agent that cleans data, answers questions, or runs a workflow is a query generator, and queries are cost of goods sold. Snowflake, for comparison, guides to a 75% non-GAAP product gross margin for fiscal 2027.[^10] Databricks declined to disclose its own.[^7]

:::quote(attr="Ali Ghodsi, co-founder and CEO, Databricks — CNBC interview, June 16, 2026")
It's the consumption-based business model, agentic AI coming. The agents are generating way more queries.
:::

The company's strategic answer is to become model-agnostic infrastructure and let customers arbitrage models themselves. Its Unity AI Gateway governs spend across multiple model providers; Ghodsi's framing is that enterprises have stopped "tokenmaxxing" and started "valuemaxxing" — reserving frontier models for hard tasks and routing mundane work to cheap open models, with Chinese open-weight models "extremely popular" on the platform.[^1,7] Databricks put its own money behind the argument, publishing an internal benchmark across its multi-million-line codebase that found open models — Z.ai's GLM 5.2 in particular — handling the hardest coding tasks at lower total cost than proprietary frontier models, with the agentic harness choice mattering as much as the model.[^3,24]

This is also a quiet admission of where Databricks sits in the AI stack. It no longer tries to win the foundation-model race. After the $1.3 billion MosaicML acquisition in 2023, its AI chief Naveen Rao departed in September 2025 to found Unconventional AI — a hardware startup that was reportedly raising $1 billion at a $5 billion valuation within weeks, with Databricks itself investing.[^19,20] The frontier models it does resell arrive through deals like the five-year, $100 million Anthropic agreement of March 2025, which puts Claude inside Mosaic AI — meaning a slice of that $1.7 billion AI run rate is inference resold at pass-through economics.[^21] The bull case is that Databricks is the tax collector on all enterprise AI consumption regardless of which model wins; the bear case is that a tax collector with falling gross margins has to keep the volume growing forever. Both are true.

## 04. The moat: from format wars to catalog wars

The defensibility question has changed shape. In 2023–24 the fight was table formats: Databricks' Delta Lake against Apache Iceberg. Databricks ended it by buying the other side — acquiring Tabular, the company founded by Iceberg's original creators, in June 2024 for a reported ~$2 billion (Bloomberg's figure; Databricks confirmed only "more than $1 billion") — a startup doing about $1 million of ARR at the time, announced pointedly in the middle of Snowflake's annual summit.[^15,16,25] By June 2025 Databricks had folded the result into Unity Catalog: managed Iceberg tables, an Iceberg REST catalog API, and UniForm translation, declaring itself "the most complete catalog for Apache Iceberg and Delta Lake."[^13,14]

:::statement(attr="ARA Research")
The format war ended in a truce; the catalog war is the moat fight now. Whoever governs the metadata governs the workloads, whoever governs the workloads collects the consumption.
:::

The logic is first-principles: storage formats commoditize, compute engines are rented, but the governance layer — permissions, lineage, auditing, discovery — is where switching costs actually live. Lakehouse//RT extends that bet into real-time: the Reyden engine queries governed Delta and Iceberg tables directly, eliminating the separate serving layer (and its separate permissions stack) that agents cannot tolerate, with customers reporting up to 16x better performance than specialized real-time systems.[^9] Snowflake has made the mirror-image move with its Horizon catalog and by donating its Polaris catalog to the Apache Software Foundation; AWS entered with S3 Tables — managed Iceberg storage integrated into SageMaker Lakehouse, GA since March 2025.[^10,18] Everyone now agrees the lakehouse won the architecture argument; the contest is over who controls the catalog on top of it.

The strongest evidence the moat is real is behavioral, not architectural: 140%-plus net retention means existing customers expand consumption faster than any churn, and >800 seven-figure customers means the governance layer is already embedded where the money is.[^4] The honest counterweight is that openness cuts both ways — a catalog that reads anyone's Iceberg tables also lets anyone else's engine read yours. Databricks is betting that governance gravity beats format gravity.

## 05. Snowflake, the public mirror

Snowflake is the control group: same consumption model, same enterprise buyers, six years of public-market seasoning — and the comparison has flipped. In 2020 Snowflake was the hypergrowth debut and Databricks the private challenger; by mid-2026 Databricks discloses a $6.9 billion annualized revenue figure growing >80% while Snowflake guides to $5.84 billion of product revenue growing 31% for fiscal 2027.[^7,10] Snowflake's latest quarter was genuinely strong — product revenue of $1.334 billion, up 34%, the strongest sequential dollar growth in its history, with remaining performance obligations of $9.21 billion up 38% — and still half Databricks' disclosed growth rate.[^10]

Snowflake is not standing still. Its AI features reach 13,600-plus accounts, Snowflake Intelligence usage doubled quarter over quarter, and it signed a $6 billion multi-year AWS agreement, deepened its OpenAI partnership, and agreed to acquire MCP-platform startup Natoma in May 2026.[^10] But its net retention of 126% trails Databricks' >140% by a structural margin, and the market noticed: SNOW fell roughly 50% from its October 2025 close to an April 2026 trough of $136.47, then roughly doubled to $268.90 by July 20, 2026 after the blowout May quarter.[^10,26]

The multiple gap is the tell. Private investors are paying ~27 times run-rate revenue for Databricks against ~15 times for Snowflake — a premium of about 1.8 times the multiple for roughly 2.3 times the growth rate.[^1,7,10] Either the private market is correctly pricing a durable growth differential, or Databricks' eventual S-1 will be a down-round public baptism. The retention and cohort data support the first reading; the margin trajectory (next section's risk) supports the second.

## 06. The hyperscaler pincer

Databricks' deepest structural tension is that its landlords are its competitors. The platform runs on AWS, Azure and Google Cloud; all three sell rival lakehouse stacks. AWS has pushed S3 Tables and SageMaker Lakehouse since re:Invent 2024, reaching general availability in March 2025; Microsoft bundles Fabric into its enterprise agreements; Google pairs BigQuery with its Gemini stack.[^18] Snowflake's answer to this gravity was a $6 billion AWS commitment announced in May 2026 — procurement as alliance.[^10] Databricks' answer has been the opposite posture: present itself as the neutral layer across all three clouds, and take their money — Microsoft participated in the February 2026 close, and Databricks expanded partnerships with Microsoft, Google Cloud, Anthropic, SAP and Palantir through 2025.[^4,6]

The JPMorganChase investment is the sleeper signal here. The bank's Security and Resiliency Initiative chose Databricks as an initial strategic investment in February 2026, explicitly framing the platform as critical-sector backbone — the kind of sovereign-adjacent endorsement no hyperscaler can offer a rival.[^4]

## 07. What would break the thesis

Three fragilities deserve more weight than the fundraising headlines give them.

- **The margin glide path has no disclosed floor.** Ghodsi says gross margin "will go lower," and agent-driven query volume compounds the effect mechanically.[^7] If AI revenue mix rises toward a third of the total while carrying pass-through inference costs, the growth re-acceleration and the margin compression are the same event — and the 27x private multiple is being paid against deteriorating unit economics, not despite them.[^1,7]
- **The mark is negotiated, not discovered.** The $188 billion round is a signed term sheet with undisclosed size; reported proceeds of ~$3 billion would represent barely 1.6% of the valuation.[^1,3] Small, insider-led rounds (Coatue is an existing investor) set headline marks far more cheaply than public float does. The same mechanism built the $100B → $134B → $188B staircase in ten months.[^1,5,6]
- **Key-person and M&A integration risk is compounding.** Rao's exit for Unconventional AI followed the MosaicML integration; the company is simultaneously digesting Neon, Tabular, and now Panther while launching into cybersecurity and marketing software — categories with entrenched incumbents and no natural Databricks gravity.[^7,19,20]

The counter-thesis, in one line: Databricks is asking public-market-bound investors to pay a software multiple for what is increasingly an AI-consumption utility — but it is the only company in the 2026 AI cohort that is growing 80% with positive free cash flow, and it gets to pick its own IPO window precisely because of that combination.[^4,7] Watch two numbers from here: the gross margin Ghodsi declines to state, and whether the 2027 window he has signaled survives contact with whatever the public market is doing by then.[^7,12]
