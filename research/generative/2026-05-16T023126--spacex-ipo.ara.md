That single sentence contains three separate claims worth pulling apart: that the businesses inside the wrapper can support a near-$2T price, that public-market governance has been written in a way few institutional investors would normally tolerate, and that retail demand plus index mechanics will absorb the float anyway. Each is conditional on something that could fail in the next thirty days.

## 01. The numbers as filed

Five banks anchor the syndicate — Bank of America, Citigroup, Goldman Sachs, JPMorgan, Morgan Stanley.[^1] CFO Bret Johnsen has told staff that retail will be *"a bigger part than any IPO in history,"* with 1,500 retail investors invited to an 11 June investor day and allocations carved out for participants in the UK, EU, Canada, Australia, Japan and Korea.[^2] That is not a courtesy. The deal cannot clear without a retail wall.

The secondary market has been pricing the move for months. A July 2025 employee tender struck at $212/share for a $400B valuation; a December 2025 tender doubled to $421/share at $800B.[^3] Then a February 2026 all-stock merger with xAI revalued the combined entity at $1.25T.[^4] The current $1.75T target is therefore a fourth re-rating in twelve months, with each step set by SpaceX itself rather than by an arms-length market.

## 02. The business beneath the price tag

Strip the consolidated entity into segments and the story sharpens. **Starlink** is the only profitable line and the only one whose unit economics are clearly working at scale: revenue grew 50% YoY to $11.4B in 2025 at a 63% EBITDA margin, with subscriptions crossing 10M across 160 countries by early 2026.[^5] The launch business is bigger than the rest of the world's commercial launch capacity combined — 50 missions through late April puts SpaceX on pace for ~160 in 2026, against Gwynne Shotwell's stated target of "140 to 145-ish" Falcon 9 flights.[^6] Federal contracts across NASA, the Space Force, NRO and SDA total roughly $22B.[^7]

xAI, by contrast, drags. The merged-in AI unit is projected at less than $1B of 2026 revenue against the multi-billion compute spend that consolidates into SpaceX's P&L.[^1] Starship development has consumed more than $15B in cumulative capex with five test flights in 2025 versus a stated target of 25 — a fivefold cadence miss.[^8] The inaugural V3 flight is reportedly targeted for "early to mid-May," meaning it may or may not have flown by the time the roadshow opens.[^6]

| Segment | 2025 revenue | 2026e revenue | Profitability | Plausible standalone value |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Starlink | $11.4B | ~$17B | 63% EBITDA margin | $500–700B |
| Launch (Falcon + Dragon) | ~$6.5B | ~$3–4B | Cash-generative; capex absorbed by Starship | $150–300B |
| Starship | ~$0 | ~$0 | $15B+ sunk; pre-revenue | Pure optionality |
| xAI / Grok | ~$0.4B | <$1B | Heavy losses | Negative on standalone basis |

The residual the buyer is asked to accept — roughly **$800B to $1T in "everything else" value** — has to be justified by Starship optionality, the Mars roadmap, and the idea that a stack of orbital data centers, lunar logistics and a planetary-scale satellite mesh is something only SpaceX can build. That is a defensible view. It is not a financial view. It is a bet on Musk's execution as a multiplier on capital, the same wager Tesla shareholders made and were rewarded for.

:::callout(kind=warn, label="The honest one-liner")
At $1.75T, you are paying roughly 25× Starlink's 2026e EBITDA for Starlink itself, plus close to $1T for an unproven launch architecture, a loss-making AI lab, and a mandate to spend the $75B raise on more of the same. None of that is irrational. It is just not a discount.
:::

## 03. The governance compromise

The structure laid out in the S-1 is unusually friendly to insiders even by 2026 standards. Musk will hold roughly 42% of the equity but around **79–84% of the voting power**, via Class B shares carrying ten votes apiece.[^9] SpaceX will file as a "controlled company" under Nasdaq rules, which removes the independent-majority board requirement. Mandatory arbitration applies to most shareholder claims. The shell is incorporated in Texas, the same jurisdiction Musk migrated Tesla to after Delaware's Chancery Court voided his 2018 compensation package — a move whose subtext is hard to miss.[^10]

The combined effect is that public Class A holders are being offered economic exposure with effectively no governance rights. You can sell the stock, and that is the entirety of your enforcement mechanism.

:::kv
- term: Voting structure
  def: 'Class A: 1 vote · Class B (insider only): 10 votes'
- term: Musk equity / vote
  def: ~42% economic, ~79–84% voting
- term: Controlled-company status
  def: Yes — exempt from independent-board majority rule
- term: State of incorporation
  def: Texas (post-Delaware migration)
- term: Shareholder lawsuits
  def: Mandatory arbitration for most claims
- term: Removal of CEO
  def: Requires majority vote, which only Musk can deliver
:::

S&P, MSCI and FTSE Russell have tightened the screws on dual-class issuers since 2017, but the rules carve out for sufficiently large issuers, and a $1.75T market cap clears any threshold. Nasdaq-100 inclusion could trigger as early as fifteen trading days after listing if the stock ranks inside the top 40 by market cap — which it would on day one.[^11] That gives passive vehicles an obligation to buy a share class whose votes are worth one-tenth of an insider's. The mismatch is not new. It is just larger than it has ever been.

> The only person who can fire Musk from SpaceX is, predictably, Musk.
> — Technology.org, summarizing the S-1, 7 May 2026

## 04. What the valuation requires you to believe

A sober DCF on the disclosed numbers does not get you to $1.75T. To make it pencil, the buyer has to underwrite a combination of the following — none individually crazy, all together demanding:

1. **Starlink hits 30–40M subs by 2028**, holds 60%+ EBITDA margins as ARPU compresses (the international tier and roaming pricing already drove Starlink ARPU down ~18% in 2025 as the base quadrupled[^12]), and survives competitive pressure from Amazon's Kuiper and the Chinese Guowang/Qianfan constellations.
2. **Starship reaches reusable, multi-launch-per-week cadence within 24 months**, drops payload-to-LEO cost to the $100–200/kg range, and unlocks a real market for orbital infrastructure (data centers, defense platforms, in-space manufacturing) — not just a bigger Starlink.
3. **Federal spend on space accelerates** rather than normalizes after the 2025 surge, with SpaceX continuing to capture the majority of new awards in launch, lunar logistics and national-security communications.
4. **xAI becomes economically self-sustaining** rather than a perpetual subsidy from Starlink cash flow. Today it is the latter.

If three of four hit, the price is defensible. If only one or two hit, the equity will reprice — and the lockup expiration around 6–12 months post-listing will land into whatever that new view is.

## 05. The risks the deck is not pricing

:::callout(kind=danger, label="Concentration risk")
Musk's attention is now split across SpaceX, Tesla, xAI, X, Neuralink, the Boring Company and a federal advisory role. Tesla shareholders have already paid for one episode of this. The S-1 contains the customary "key person" disclosure but no enforceable time commitment.
:::

:::callout(kind=warn, label="Political-cycle exposure")
$22B in cumulative federal contracts is a strategic moat that is also a strategic liability. A change in administration or a single high-profile Starship anomaly during a crewed mission could compress the contract pipeline and the multiple simultaneously.
:::

:::callout(kind=info, label="Spectrum and orbital-debris regulation")
Starlink's economics depend on continued FCC and ITU-coordinated spectrum allocations and on permissive deorbit rules. Both are subject to multilateral pressure that has accelerated as the constellation has crossed 8,000+ active satellites.
:::

The risk factor section of the prospectus will list these. The retail investor day will not lead with them. The gap is where mispricing tends to live.

## 07. The honest bottom line

SpaceX is the most operationally impressive private company of its generation, with a real business in Starlink and a credible launch monopoly that competitors are no closer to dislodging than they were two years ago. The IPO is not a question of whether SpaceX is a great company. It is a question of whether $1.75T is a fair entry price for a Class A share that carries one vote per ten of Musk's, into an entity whose consolidated P&L turned negative the moment xAI was bolted on, with execution risk concentrated in a CEO who is running six other companies.

The honest answer is that the market will probably clear, that the first month of trading will probably go up rather than down on scarcity and index demand, and that the longer-run return will depend on whether Starship works as promised by 2028. None of those three claims is the same as "the IPO is correctly priced." They just describe what is likely to happen anyway.

:::callout(kind=info, label="Bottom line")
Retail buyers planning to participate should size positions on the assumption that the governance structure means they have no recourse, that the valuation requires Starship success they cannot verify, and that the index-flow tailwind has an end date. The deal is investable. It is not a free option.
:::
