ABF substrates: the $300 chokepoint inside every AI accelerator

A single Japanese food company's dielectric film, six Asian substrate fabs, and a $10 billion capex race the rest of the world cannot reach.

Every Blackwell GPU, every Trainium2 die, every Granite Rapids socket shipping in 2026 sits on top of a flip-chip BGA substrate whose insulating layers are made of one material, by one company, in volumes the buyer cannot meaningfully second-source. That material is Ajinomoto Build-up Film — ABF — and the company holding more than 95% of the global market is the same Tokyo conglomerate that sells you MSG. The substrate it carries costs $200 to $1,500 in a $25,000 AI package; that asymmetry is exactly why it has become the binding constraint on AI accelerator delivery through 2028.

01The film no one talks about

Every Blackwell GPU, every Trainium2 die, every Granite Rapids socket sitting in a hyperscale rack ships on top of a flip-chip BGA substrate whose insulating layers are made of one material, by one company, in volumes the buyer cannot meaningfully second-source. That material is Ajinomoto Build-up Film — ABF — and the company is the same Tokyo conglomerate that sells you MSG.54

The origin story is the kind that would be dismissed as marketing if Ajinomoto's own IR deck didn't tell it the same way every year. In 1996, Intel's packaging team needed an organic insulator that could survive the architectural shift then under way: from ceramic packages with wire bonds to plastic flip-chip BGAs with copper bumps and laser-drilled microvias.5 The ink-form build-up dielectrics of the day couldn't planarize, off-gassed solvent, and trapped air bubbles under the copper plating. Intel approached Ajinomoto because the food company's polymer arm had been quietly compounding amino-acid–derived epoxies since the 1970s as a by-product stream of glutamate manufacturing. Three years later, in 1999, ABF shipped.1

What followed was a slow chemistry march that the rest of the industry never matched. The GX-series moved from a 46 ppm/°C epoxy in 2005 to a 23 ppm/°C low-CTE film by 2018, and the parallel GZ cyanate-ester line cut dielectric loss roughly in half for high-speed signaling.53 At ECTC 2025, researchers demonstrated 3.5 µm microvias in ABF at aspect ratio 3 using KrF excimer ablation — meaning the dielectric is still scaling with AI substrate density rather than blocking it.7

The result is one of the cleanest moats in the semiconductor stack. Ajinomoto's own ASV Report calls the film the "de facto standard" with an "overwhelming share" of advanced FC-BGA packages, conventionally cited at >95%.4 The financial signature is unmistakable: the Functional Materials sub-segment that houses ABF posted ¥100.7B in FY2025 sales (+31.6% YoY) at a 54.2% business-profit margin, with segment margins climbing from roughly 30% in FY18 to above 50% today as AI-package mix grew.2 Ajinomoto is putting ¥25B of capex behind a 50% capacity expansion by 2030, with a new Kani-Mitake (Gifu) site coming online in 2032.357

The counterpoint is that ABF is not technically irreplaceable. Sekisui Chemical sells a competing build-up film and openly markets itself as "the ONLY alternative mass producing dielectric build-up material" — phrasing that doubles as confirmation of how thin the bench is.6 The real moat isn't the epoxy recipe; it's qualification. Each substrate maker burns 18–36 months requalifying any new film through warpage, peel-strength, drill-yield, and customer-side reliability gates before it can ship on a tier-1 part. So long as Ajinomoto holds the qualified slot at every advanced packaging line, every package built for Blackwell, Rubin, Trainium2, MI400, and Granite Rapids inherits Ajinomoto's pricing power as a non-negotiable line item in the BOM.

02What's in the stack and what it costs

An AI accelerator package is a layered cost stack — and the ABF substrate is the $200–$1,000 part that gates a $25,000+ sale. Logic gets the headlines, HBM gets the bill, but the laminated organic substrate underneath is the structural piece that has to be ordered eighteen months out and that no second source can ship on short notice.

The ABF substrate itself is a sub-line inside that 17%. Each B200-class substrate runs $500–$1,000 a unit — single-digit percentage of total package cost, and well under 5% of NVIDIA's per-unit revenue58. The stack, physically

A modern AI substrate is a laminated sandwich. Most B200-class parts are still core designs — a thin glass-cloth-reinforced epoxy core in the middle — though Ibiden's roadmap and the 2027+ generation are pushing toward coreless construction to hit the flatness and impedance targets at 24+ layers958. Above and below the core sit the build-up layers: alternating sheets of Ajinomoto Build-up Film dielectric and copper redistribution lines patterned by semi-additive process. Layer-to-layer connections are laser-drilled microvias — currently 20 µm and shrinking toward sub-10 µm; a 2025 ECTC paper from the same lineage as the Ibiden roadmap demonstrated 3.5 µm microvias in lab conditions768. The top surface is plated ENEPIG for flip-chip bond pads; the bottom carries thousands of BGA balls for the PCB interface.

Why the cheap part is the binding part

This is the asymmetry that defines the chokepoint. NVIDIA runs 73–78% gross margins on data-center silicon and an H100 ASP north of $25,000; a $300 substrate is roughly 1.2% of revenue and a B200's $500–1,000 substrate is under 4%4546. NVIDIA can absorb almost any substrate price increase a supplier asks for — and has. What NVIDIA cannot absorb is substrate unavailability: every accelerator needs exactly one substrate, and a missing $300 part holds up a $25,000 sale.

Counterpoint

HBM is three times the absolute cost of the substrate — so why obsess about ABF? Because HBM has three qualified suppliers (SK hynix, Samsung, Micron), all of whom are racing capacity. ABF-grade dielectric has effectively one (Ajinomoto), and AI-grade substrate fabrication has six globally — covered in section 03. Cost concentration is not the same as supply concentration, and supply is what fails the build.

The second compounding factor is yield. A naive 99% per-layer yield, compounded over 20 build-up layers, already drops cumulative yield to 81.8%. Industry reporting on real 14-layer AI substrates puts yields in the 75–80% range, and 20+ layer Blackwell-class parts are described as "substantially lower" — every additional layer adds another multiplicative hit from microvia drilling, plating, lamination warpage, and pad registration58. That is the real reason Ibiden's multi-year expansion takes three years from groundbreaking to qualified output: it is not a building, it is a yield curve.

Why this matters. When an analyst models a Blackwell shipment, the binding constraint is rarely the wafer or the HBM auction — both have multiple shots on goal. The binding constraint is whether the substrate fab line that won the ramp can actually turn 20-layer build-up at saleable yield. That single number, hidden inside a 17% line item, sets the ceiling on how many accelerators the industry can build in 2026.

03A six-firm oligopoly with one Western entrant

The FC-BGA substrate industry is one of the most concentrated tier-2 nodes in the entire AI supply chain: five Asian incumbents — Unimicron, Ibiden, Nan Ya PCB, Shinko Electric, Kinsus — plus Korea's Samsung Electro-Mechanics control an estimated 70-85% of global capacity, with Austria's AT&S the only Western producer operating at scale. At the very-top spec required by Blackwell-class GPUs and ASIC accelerators, the field collapses further to a working duopoly of Ibiden and Unimicron.

Geographically the picture is even tighter. Taiwan and Japan together host roughly two-thirds of qualified FC-BGA capacity; Korea adds another sixth via SEMCO; everything else — Chinese mainland output, AT&S Chongqing, AT&S Kulim in Malaysia — is rounding error. There is no US-owned FC-BGA substrate fab of meaningful scale anywhere on the planet.

At the apex of the spec tree — the largest reticle-class bodies, the finest line/space, the highest layer counts required for GPU and AI ASIC packaging — qualification narrows even further. On Ibiden's Q1 FY2025 earnings call CEO Koji Kawashima quantified that niche in unusually direct language:

We estimate our market share to be around 70-80%.

Koji Kawashima, CEO, Ibiden — Q1 FY2025 earnings call, Oct 31 2025

That figure refers specifically to AI-server FC-BGA substrates, where analyst consensus treats Ibiden and Unimicron as the only two qualified suppliers for NVIDIA's top SKUs10.

The individual positioning is worth itemizing. Unimicron is the volume leader on revenue, the primary anchor for NVIDIA and the broader ASIC ecosystem, and is funding parallel expansions at its Yangmei and Guangfu sites in Taiwan to absorb CoWoS-related substrate demand1921. Ibiden sits one rung above on spec — its Gifu/Ogaki sites carry the most demanding NVIDIA Blackwell and successor designs — and the company announced a ¥500B follow-on capex plan in February 2026 explicitly aimed at high-performance IC package substrates8. Nan Ya PCB, the substrate arm of Taiwan's Formosa Plastics Group, reset its leadership in May 2025 and remains a tier-1 supplier for both server and high-end consumer FC-BGA. Shinko Electric was taken private on June 6 2025 by a JIC consortium at ¥5,920/share (a ¥683B deal that required eighteen months of clearance, including China SAMR antitrust approval finalized Dec 27 2024) — meaning a top-five global substrate producer is now invisible to public-market disclosure141516. Samsung Electro-Mechanics is the most aggressive non-Taiwanese entrant, posting +45% YoY growth in its Package Solutions segment in Q1 2026 and committing a fresh $1.2B for a Vietnam plant on top of roughly ₩3T cumulative FC-BGA capex4849. Kinsus — frequently misidentified as an ASE subsidiary — is in fact the substrate arm of Pegatron, which limits the volume ceiling relative to the larger Taiwanese players22. AT&S is the genuine outlier: the only European-headquartered FC-BGA maker at scale began high-volume manufacturing at its new Kulim, Malaysia plant on May 5 2025, having deployed roughly €1B of an envisioned €1.7B Phase 1 capex, with AMD as the publicly confirmed anchor customer25.

One important caveat: the share numbers above should be read as order-of-magnitude. Yole, Prismark, and the various market-research aggregators frequently disagree by several percentage points on the same firm in the same quarter, because the boundary between "FC-BGA," "ABF-only," "all advanced substrate" and "all package substrate" is drawn differently by each. The ranking is robust; the digits after the decimal are not.

Why this matters: when 70-80% of the very capacity an NVIDIA Blackwell rack depends on flows through two Japanese and Taiwanese campuses, concentration becomes pricing power on the way up and a single-point-of-failure on the way down. One fab incident at Ogaki or Yangmei, one earthquake in central Taiwan, one regulatory shock in Korea — and the AI build-out's most expensive accelerators have nowhere to land. The remainder of this report treats that fragility as the central risk, not a tail.

04Boom, bust, boom: the four-year cycle that reshuffled everyone

The substrate industry just lived through a textbook capital-cycle accident: capacity announced into a pandemic-era shortage hit the market exactly as PCs cratered, and by the time the glut cleared, AI had bifurcated demand into a sold-out top tier and a slack commodity tier underneath. Four years, three regimes, one durable lesson about lead-time mismatch.

  1. 2020–H1 2022
    Pandemic boom

    PC and gaming GPU demand collide with crypto mining; Broadcom router-chip lead times stretch from 63 to 70 weeks; Unimicron's ABF capacity is booked through 2025 as of July 2021; Chairman Tseng warns 2022 supply will fall at least 20% short of demand.

  2. H2 2022–2024
    PC collapse, glut deepens

    Unimicron FY23 revenue falls ~26% YoY to NT$104B; net income roughly halves. Nan Ya PCB 1Q23 down 13.6% YoY, FY24 PCB segment NT$31.4B (-23.6%). AT&S issues Jan 2024 profit warning. Ibiden cuts H1 FY2024 guidance from ¥390B to ¥370B in Nov 2024.

  3. 2024–H1 2025
    Bifurcation

    AI-server substrates ramp hard (Ibiden AI-server revenue tripled YoY in FY24); PC/general-server substrates stay soft. Kinsus characterizes the market as split: high-end ABF tight, commodity ABF still adequate. AT&S Kulim begins HVM May 2025.

  4. 2026
    Top-tier sold out

    DIGITIMES reports ABF substrates 'sold out' at Unimicron, Kinsus, Nan Ya PCB by April. SEMCO raises FC-BGA prices. Unimicron Q1 2026 net income +451% YoY; Kinsus FY25 NI +31x. Lead times for 14+ layer ABF stretched to 28+ weeks early 2025.

The first act ran from early 2020 through mid-2022. Locked-down households bought PCs; miners bought GPUs; hyperscalers built. Substrate became the bottleneck inside the bottleneck: Broadcom's router-chip lead times stretched from 63 to 70 weeks specifically because ABF capacity could not be pulled forward64. By July 2021, Unimicron's ABF lines were booked through 202565, and by year-end Chairman Tseng was on record that 2022 industry supply would fall at least 20% short of demand66. Every Tier-1 maker — Ibiden, Shinko, Unimicron, Nan Ya PCB, Kinsus, SEMCO — announced multi-year capex programs into this shortage. Most of that steel and copper would land in 2023 and 2024.

It landed into a collapsing market. PC shipments rolled over in H2 2022, crypto demand evaporated, and the substrate glut that followed was the deepest in a decade. Unimicron's FY23 revenue fell roughly 26% year-over-year to NT$104B, with net income approximately halved. Nan Ya PCB's 1Q23 revenue dropped 13.6% YoY67 and the PCB segment finished FY24 at NT$31.4B, down 23.6%. AT&S issued a profit warning in January 202426, and even in November 2024 — with AI-server demand running hot — Ibiden cut full-year FY2024 revenue guidance from ¥390B to ¥370B and operating profit from ¥85B to ¥70B11. Taiwan substrate-maker share prices crashed in June 202269 and stayed there for eighteen months.

Guidance cut

Ibiden cut FY2024 full-year revenue guidance from ¥390B to ¥370B and operating profit from ¥85B to ¥70B at the 1H earnings call11 — even as AI-server demand ran hot, PC and general-purpose server demand stayed soft and dragged the blended number down.

The third act is bifurcation, and it is the single most important fact in this section. From late 2024 the market split cleanly in two. Ibiden disclosed that AI-server substrate revenue tripled YoY in FY2411; Kinsus and Unimicron described their order books in the same terms — high-layer-count ABF for accelerators and switch ASICs is sold out, while commodity ABF for client CPUs and general-purpose servers still has slack. By April 2026 DIGITIMES was reporting all three Taiwanese majors at full utilization on AI SKUs24, SEMCO had begun raising FC-BGA prices71, and the financial recovery showed up violently in the numbers: Unimicron's Q1 2026 net income rose 451% YoY20, Kinsus FY25 net income grew roughly 31x off a depressed base22.

Notice what the slopegraph shows: even after the AI rebound, FY2025 revenue at all three Taiwanese majors is still below the FY2022 peak. The "sold out" headline is a mix-shift story, not a volume story — high-ASP AI substrates have plugged a hole in the income statement that depressed PC volumes carved out, but the topline has not fully recovered. The counterpoint to every bullish 2026 print is that commodity ABF utilization at the same firms is still well shy of design capacity19.

Why this matters: capex committed in 2026 will produce wafers in 2028–2030, but the inventory cycle that punished everyone in 2023 turned in roughly eighteen months. The industry is being asked to size a four-year build to an AI demand curve that has, twice in five years, surprised its own forecasters in both directions.

Whether 2027–2028 ends in another glut or a sustained shortage depends entirely on how disciplined this build is.

Two structural points follow. First, the customers are co-funding the build. Ibiden's balance sheet carries roughly ¥92.1B of customer advance payments earmarked for substrate capacity, and industry commentary estimates Intel, AMD, and NVIDIA have collectively subsidized something like half of expansion capex at the top suppliers — the same prepay model Ajinomoto itself pioneered with Intel in the 1990s when it needed scale for the original ABF line58. This dramatically lowers the suppliers' downside if AI demand softens, because the silicon vendor has already paid for the tools.

Second, and more importantly, the asymmetric capex insight: ¥25B (~$166M) of Ajinomoto film capex unlocks a 50% film-capacity expansion through 20303, while a single new substrate fab runs $800M–$1.2B55. The film step is roughly an order of magnitude cheaper than the fab step. ABF film is not the binding constraint of the AI substrate supply chain — the substrate fab is. This contradicts the lazy "Ajinomoto is the chokepoint" framing that dominates much of the trade press: Ajinomoto is the price-maker, sole-source and unsubstitutable, but capacity gating lives downstream in Ibiden, Shinko, SEMCO, AT&S, and Unimicron cleanrooms.

Customer demand levels exceed our current production capacity by more than 50%.

Jang Deok-hyun, CEO, Samsung Electro-Mechanics — March 2026 AGM

The counterpoint comes from inside the industry. Ibiden CEO Kawashima has been notably reluctant to celebrate, telling investors on the Q1 FY2025 call: "While supply and demand are tight, competitors may also be expanding capacity, so we are not optimistic about product prices."10 That is the voice of a CEO who has lived through the 2022–2023 inventory bust and is refusing to underwrite the ASP-strength narrative even as he commits ¥500B to new lines. The same call also noted material bottlenecks and that 70–80% of new shipments are AI-related10 — a concentration that is itself a risk.

Why this matters: the ten-figure build now underway is unusually well-funded and unusually concentrated in two or three names per region. If the customer prepays hold and substrate-fab discipline holds, 2027–2028 looks structurally tight. If hyperscaler orders soften by even 20% while every supplier is mid-ramp, the cycle resets — and Kawashima will have been right to hedge his language.

From passive carrier to active component

CoWoS-L is the inflection. Where CoWoS-S used a monolithic silicon interposer and CoWoS-R used a routed organic interposer, CoWoS-L embeds local silicon interconnect (LSI) bridges and deep-trench capacitors directly into the package stack, with the ABF substrate hosting fine-pitch flip-chip attach for that embedded silicon at sub-50 µm bump pitch.42 The substrate is no longer routing power and signal between a chip and a PCB — it is the structural and electrical mounting plane for the interposer-equivalent silicon itself. NVIDIA's product mix tells the story: TheValueist's late-2025 channel checks pegged the 2025 split at CoWoS-S/R 51% → 8% and CoWoS-L 49% → 88% across the year, a near-total inversion in twelve months.51 Layer counts moved in lockstep — H100 ran 16–20 ABF layers, B100/B200 needs 20+, and Ibiden's Kawashima told analysts on the Q1 FY2025 call that high-layer-count material, not square-meter capacity, is the bottleneck.10

Why Rubin Ultra got smaller

The B200 body sits at roughly 110×110 mm, already near the JEDEC 120×120 mm socket ceiling.42 Rubin Ultra's original spec — 120×150 mm carrying four compute dies on a 9.5× reticle interposer — pushed the organic carrier past the point where coefficient-of-thermal-expansion (CTE) mismatch between the silicon bridges and the ABF stack could be managed at yield. SemiAnalysis and industry packaging analysts flagged the same CTE-mismatch failure mode as the cause of Blackwell's 2024 ramp delays, and the larger Rubin Ultra body amplified that physics rather than escaping it.60 NVIDIA's response, per the packaging analyst Markos, was the 2+2 board-level configuration — splitting the four dies across two separate packages connected on the PCB — and a likely move to ABF-GCP (glass-cloth reinforced ABF) for additional through-thickness stiffness.50 The chip was redesigned to fit the substrate, not the other way around.

The hyperscaler ASIC pull-through

The pull-through into hyperscaler silicon is what turns Blackwell's specific yield problem into an industry-wide capacity squeeze. SemiAnalysis's Trainium2 teardown documented a 20-layer ABF carrier over an organic CoWoS-R interposer, with Unimicron holding more than half of AWS's substrate share; Trainium3 stays on the same architectural recipe.4344 Google's TPU v7 Ironwood pairs 8× HBM3e on a Broadcom-routed package that pulls from Ibiden's "AI ASIC" Gama Cell6 line, and Microsoft's Maia 200 launched with 216 GB of HBM3e on what is almost certainly a Unimicron substrate.62 Ibiden's February 2026 capex split made the segmentation explicit: ¥220B for Gama Cell6 ("AI ASIC") and ¥280B for Ono Cell8 ("AI GPU") — two lines for two customer tiers, both top-spec.8

Counterpoint

Not every ASIC needs the top-spec line. Trainium 2/3 deliberately uses CoWoS-R with an organic interposer and four HBM stacks — less substrate-stressing than B200's eight-stack CoWoS-L package, and qualifiable on more suppliers.43 MTIA 3 stays on plain CoWoS-S. The chokepoint is genuine for NVIDIA-class parts and TPU v7-class parts; the volume pressure on Ibiden and Unimicron comes from booking millions of simpler ASIC units alongside NVIDIA's complexity, not from every ASIC being a B200.

Why this matters: the substrate has graduated from BOM line item to architectural gate. When NVIDIA's roadmap respin and four hyperscalers' ASIC plans all land on the same two Japanese and Taiwanese supplier qualifications, ABF capacity at the 20-layer / sub-20 µm-microvia tier becomes the timeline for AI accelerator delivery through 2028 — which is why the Ajinomoto moat in section 07 is worth inspecting at the molecule level.

07The Ajinomoto moat under inspection

Ajinomoto's pricing power is real and growing — Functional Materials business-profit margin climbed from ~30% in FY18 to 54.2% in FY252 — but the moat is not permanent. Three credible disruptors are now visible at 2027–2032 timelines, each chipping at a different layer of the lock-in.

The moat is real and quantifiable

Ajinomoto's IR explicitly discloses that Functional Materials business-profit margin grew from roughly 30% in FY18 to over 50% in FY25, with the segment now contributing 6.4% of group revenue but 30.1% of group business profit2. On the FY25 briefing, management attributed the expansion to a "higher-value product mix" — code for AI-grade ABF, where customers pay a premium for film qualified into Blackwell- and Trainium-class substrates. The company is backing the cash machine with capital: ¥25 billion committed since 2023 for a +50% capacity expansion by 20303, plus a brand-new Kani-Mitake plant in Gifu Prefecture announced for 2032 operations57.

What patents alone never sustained, the customer-co-development model does. Every substrate maker — Unimicron, Ibiden, Shinko, Nan Ya — burns 18–36 months of yield learning to qualify any new dielectric film into a given chemistry stack. That qualification debt is the actual moat: it makes "switching suppliers" a multi-year, multi-million-dollar program rather than a purchase-order decision. Ajinomoto sells time, not chemistry.

Glass-core substrates. The most hyped disruptor and the one with the messiest recent newsflow. Intel announced its glass program in September 2023 with "second half of the decade" HVM31. By mid-2025, IFTLE reported that Intel had stopped funding its internal glass-core development, pivoting to source from Absolics (a $75M CHIPS award recipient) and Samsung Electro-Mechanics3233. SEMCO itself slipped HVM from 2026 to 2027 and, in November 2025, signed a JV with Sumitomo Chemical just to secure glass-core supply3461. Real-world yields run 75–85% versus ABF's 90–95% at 2–3× cost, and Yole forecasts the glass-core substrate market at only "multi-hundred million $" by 2030 against a total advanced IC substrate market of ~$31 billion — low-single-digit share, not the 20–30% commonly assumed in conference decks35.

CoWoP — Chip-on-Wafer-on-PCB. NVIDIA reportedly began CoWoP test vehicles in July 2025, targeting a parallel CoWoS/CoWoP path on Rubin GR150 in late 2026 with 450×450 mm platform boards5478. The architecture is genuinely radical: mount the silicon-interposer module directly onto a reinforced server motherboard and eliminate the ABF substrate entirely. The catch is the PCB itself — CoWoP needs <10/10 µm line/space versus today's 20/35 µm HDI norm, a gating constraint that has nothing to do with Ajinomoto and everything to do with whether PCB fabs can hit substrate-grade lithography. Rubin Ultra reportedly retains ABF, making this a multi-generational displacement rather than a 2026 cliff78.

Sekisui as a real second source. Sekisui Chemical has been quietly shipping competing build-up film since 2014 and self-markets as "the ONLY alternative mass producing dielectric build-up material"6. Estimated share is 5–10% today, with some market-research aggregators forecasting 20–30% post-2026. Sekisui is unlikely to ever take the crown, but its mere existence is what caps Ajinomoto's long-run margin at "above 50%" rather than "above 70%."

Disconfirming evidence

Intel "officially stopped funding its in-house glass substrate development" in mid-2025 per IFTLE 638, redirecting to external sourcing from Absolics and SEMCO3233. SEMCO slipped HVM from 2026 to 2027 and signed a Sumitomo Chemical JV in November 2025 just to secure glass-core supply34. Yole forecasts the glass-core substrate market at "multi-hundred million $" by 2030 against a total advanced IC substrate market of ~$31B35 — i.e., low-single-digit share, not the 20–30% commonly assumed. Separately, SemiAnalysis argues the near-term ABF roadmap is coreless ABF (Cisco demoed a 10-layer 6 µm L/S part), which extends rather than replaces Ajinomoto's runway76.

Why this matters. The 54% margin is not a screen-scrape artifact — it is the most precise read on AI-substrate scarcity available in any public filing. But the three disruptors above are exactly what an investor underwriting "Ajinomoto compounds at 50%+ forever" needs to handicap. The base case for 2027–2030 is that coreless ABF extends the runway, glass-core takes single-digit share, CoWoP ships on one Rubin SKU, and Sekisui caps — rather than collapses — pricing. The moat narrows. It does not drain.

The China substitution gap

The leading edge of China's domestic FC-BGA effort is Shennan Circuits (002916.SZ), which Chinese trade press reports as approaching mass production at 22 layers with 24+ layer parts in R&D — though English-language coverage as recent as 2024 still pegged Shennan at 16-layer mass production, so the spec claim deserves to be treated as a trade-press self-disclosure not an independently audited capability70. Its Guangzhou ABF substrate program is a CNY 6 billion (~$926M) capex commitment targeting more than 200 million units per year at full ramp39. The second-tier domestic player, Shenzhen Fastprint (002436.SZ), remains in small-batch production at sub-20-layer counts. On the film side, the Chinese ABF substitutes — Huazheng's CBF and Tianhe Defense's QBF — are at sample or small-batch qualification stage and are not yet flowing onto FC-BGA substrates above ~16 layers, which is precisely the layer regime where AI accelerators live4155.

State capital is now backing the lane explicitly. Big Fund Phase III injected equity into AKM Meadville in December 2025, the first time China's national semiconductor fund has put direct equity into an IC substrate maker40. One persistent misreading is worth correcting: Wus Printed Circuit is Taiwan-headquartered (the listed subsidiary in Shenzhen is a downstream entity), not a Chinese substrate maker, despite how it is frequently tallied52. Net localization of ABF substrate in China is roughly 4% in 2024, rising to ~5% in 2025E — and the AI front line remains foreign-supplied. Chinese trade press credits SCC and Fastprint as substrate suppliers on Huawei's Ascend 910C, but neither Huawei nor the substrate makers have confirmed this through investor relations41.

The critical regulatory point that often gets lost: ABF and FC-BGA substrates are not on the US BIS Entity List, not covered by a dedicated ECCN, and not subject to a US export-control license requirement4155. The bottleneck on Chinese access to leading-edge substrate is commercial allocation by Ajinomoto and the six-firm substrate oligopoly — not export law. That distinction matters: a tariff or a license rule could be written tomorrow, but the structural shortage is being managed today by private contracts and Japanese capacity sequencing.

Why Western policy is so asymmetric

Compare the four Western blocs and the asymmetry becomes the story. Japan's METI has committed roughly ¥58 billion in direct grants — up to ¥40.5B for Ibiden's Ono plant under the Economic Security Promotion Act12, plus ¥17.8B for Shinko as part of a ¥55B June 2023 substrate basket38. The US CHIPS Act's NAPMP committed $1.4B across three competing substrate technology pillars — organic substrate (ASU-led FOWLP, ~$100M), glass-core (Absolics, $100M NAPMP layer on top of a $75M CHIPS Incentives award), and silicon-core (Applied Materials, ~$100M)36376377. That is bigger than the headline numbers suggest if you count all three pillars together, but it is structurally a hedge across competing technologies, not a capacity build for the incumbent organic-ABF lane.

The EU is the most underweight bloc. AT&S — Europe's only IC substrate maker — received essentially no EU direct funding for its €500M Hinterberg 3 plant in Leoben (opened June 2025) or its €1.7B Kulim, Malaysia build; the only disclosed EU contribution is a ~€8M Chips Act competence center grant75. Korea relies almost entirely on the K-Chips Act's 15–25% investment tax credit, with no SEMCO-specific direct grant on the public record75.

Read the policy revealed-preferences directly: Japan treats substrates as a national-security commodity worth protecting the incumbent on; the US is hedging across three substrate technology bets because it has no incumbent to protect; the EU has revealed-preferenced its industrial policy elsewhere (fabs, not substrates); China is funding a domestic catch-up at scale but cannot close the AI-layer-count gap on a 2026–2028 horizon.

Why this matters: the policy asymmetry, combined with the absence of US export controls on ABF, means the AI-accelerator substrate supply is being rationed today by Ajinomoto and six Japan/Korea/Taiwan/Austria firms acting on commercial logic — and no Western government has bought enough optionality to change that allocation if it tightens further.

09What could break the thesis

The base case in this report is that ABF substrate scarcity is the binding capacity gate on AI accelerators through 2028, with Ajinomoto extracting >50% margins on a +50% capacity ramp and Ibiden plus Unimicron splitting most NVIDIA and hyperscaler-ASIC substrate orders. Five things would falsify that base case, and each is worth naming explicitly.

1. AI accelerator orders soften 20% in 2027. The capex tsunami of section 05 is sized for an AI demand curve that keeps doubling. If GPU and ASIC orders flatten — not collapse, just flatten — the substrate makers will be mid-ramp into oversupply for the second time in five years. Ibiden's CEO Kawashima has already said he is "not optimistic about product prices" precisely because every competitor is expanding in parallel10. A repeat of 2022–2023 is the single largest tail risk, and it is not particularly small.

2. CoWoP succeeds on Rubin GR150 in late 2026 and scales. NVIDIA's chip-on-wafer-on-PCB program is the only roadmap item that eliminates the ABF substrate entirely5478. The gating constraint is PCB lithography rather than substrate chemistry, so a breakthrough at a Taiwanese or Japanese PCB fab on <10/10 µm L/S would matter more than any Ajinomoto announcement. If two NVIDIA SKUs and one hyperscaler ASIC ship on CoWoP by 2028, the AI-grade ABF TAM grows much more slowly than this report assumes.

3. Glass-core substrates ship earlier than Yole expects. Yole's forecast — glass-core at multi-hundred-million-dollar revenue by 2030 against $15B AICS — is the most bearish-on-glass take from a credible analyst35. If Absolics' Georgia line, SEMCO's Sejong pilot, or an Intel-Absolics tie-up clears qualification ahead of plan, glass could take 8–12% share by 2030 instead of 3–5%. That is still a minority, but it punctures the "Ajinomoto compounds at 50% forever" thesis at the margins.

4. A Taiwan or Japan supply shock. With ~64% of qualified FC-BGA capacity in two earthquake-prone island nations, the realized worst-case scenario is not market-driven at all. A single seismic event in central Taiwan or Gifu Prefecture could remove 20–30% of global supply overnight, with no Western backup. This is not a thesis-breaker so much as a thesis-amplifier — it would make ABF supply more of a chokepoint, not less — but it would also force the policy hand of every government covered in section 08.

5. Sekisui or a Chinese film maker qualifies onto a tier-1 line. Sekisui has been at 5–10% share for a decade without breaking through to NVIDIA-class substrates. Huazheng CBF and Tianhe QBF are at sample stage. If any of them clears qualification at Unimicron or Ibiden for an AI-grade part — which would take 18–36 months of yield learning — Ajinomoto's >95% film share starts to look more like 85%, and the margin trajectory bends. Watch substrate-maker IR commentary on "supplier qualification programs" as the leading indicator; Ibiden's Q1 FY2025 transcript already mentioned dual-sourcing activity10.

The honest summary is that ABF substrates sit at the intersection of an unusually durable industrial-chemistry moat, an unusually concentrated manufacturing oligopoly, and an unusually fast-moving end market. Three things have to go right for the base case to hold through 2028: AI demand stays strong, substrate fab capacity ramps on schedule, and no displacement technology ships at production volumes ahead of plan. Any two of three would still make ABF the most under-appreciated chokepoint in the AI supply chain. All three failing simultaneously is the only scenario in which substrates stop being the binding gate — and there is no public roadmap on which all three fail by 2028.
