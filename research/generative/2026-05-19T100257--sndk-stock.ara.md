## 01. The 28-fold tape: spin-off to S&P 500

SanDisk went from a Western Digital divestiture nobody wanted in February 2025 to the best-performing S&P 500 component of the AI cycle by May 2026 — a {accent}28-fold re-rating{/} from a $46.85 February-month close to $1,333.01 in May, compounded across three sequential catalysts that the sell side missed in order: forced selling into the spin, index-flow mechanics, and the Q3 FY26 NAND-pricing print. (The first regular-way day's close, February 24, 2025, was $48.60 per WDC's 8-K.)

The spin closed on February 21, 2025, with WDC distributing one SNDK share for every three WDC shares held, retaining a 19.9% stub (28,827,787 shares) and extracting a $1.5 billion dividend on the way out the door.[^1] [^2] Regular-way trading began February 24 at a $48.60 close[^1] — a clean number to anchor the tape against, and one that valued the standalone NAND business at roughly the implied stub multiple WDC sell-side analysts had been carrying as a sum-of-parts placeholder. The first quarter post-spin did what forced-distribution spin-offs typically do: SNDK drifted from the high-$40s to a $32.11 April-2025 trough as index funds that didn't want a pure-play NAND name sold mechanically into a market with no natural buyer.

The second catalyst was index inclusion. S&P 500 addition became effective November 28, 2025, with the announcement on November 24 sending the stock up more than 13% on heavy volume as passive vehicles rebuilt a position they'd been short of since the spin.[^3] That re-rating arc — $52 in August to $223 by month-end November — was substantially mechanical, and a fair counterpoint to any pure-fundamentals reading of the tape: a non-trivial fraction of the early move was index flow meeting a thin float, not earnings revisions.

The third and largest leg — $237 at year-end 2025 to $1,333 by mid-May 2026, a 5.6x in twenty-one weeks — is harder to dismiss as mechanical. WDC itself monetized 5.82 million shares at $545 on February 17, 2026 for roughly $3.17 billion, leaving only a 1.69-million-share residual.[^4] That print, in hindsight, looks cheap: SNDK traded through $1,000 within ten weeks of the offering, meaning WDC left approximately ==$4.6 billion== of mark-to-market value on the table on the secondary alone. The fact that a fully-informed insider with a 19.9% stake cleared the float at $545 is the cleanest available bear datapoint — either WDC's risk committee was wrong about the cycle, or the buyer base post-secondary was different in character (momentum, then squeeze) from the fundamental cycle call that followed.

The Q3 FY26 print on April 30 did the work the secondary did not anticipate. Within four trading days, the sell side rebased: Morgan Stanley took its target to $1,100 from $690 on April 27 (modeling CY26 EPS of $127.92 against a $77.55 Street, i.e., 65% above consensus),[^5] Goldman to $1,200 from $700 on May 1,[^6] and Bernstein to a Street-high $1,700 from $1,250 on May 4 on the NAND pricing trajectory.[^7] Short interest had already moved against the tape — from 4% of float in November 2025 to 7.5% by January 2026, with an S3 squeeze score of 82.5 ("extreme") and roughly $3 billion of mark-to-market losses on the short book before the April print landed.[^8]

> We just took our targets up on Micron and SanDisk and Intel the most in our group. They are signing these long-term agreements that make them more predictable and I think they get recognized for it.
> — Ben Reitzes, Melius Research, on CNBC Squawk Box, May 18, 2026 [^9]

Why this matters: the 28x is the headline, but the structure of the move — forced selling into the spin, then index flow, then a fundamental EPS rebase against a heavily-shorted float — is what the rest of this report has to underwrite. If the cycle thesis in sections 03-05 is wrong, the squeeze and index components do not hold the stock up alone. If it is right, the $1,333 tape is not the ceiling.

## 02. The Q3 print that broke the model

SanDisk's Q3 FY2026, reported April 30, 2026 for the quarter ended April 3, was the rare release in which {accent}every line on every analyst model was wrong by a factor{/} — and wrong in the same direction.

Revenue of $5.95B printed +97% sequentially and +251% year-over-year, against a guide of $4.4-4.8B and a Street consensus that clustered tightly below the low end of management's own range.[^10] Non-GAAP gross margin came in at 78.4%, more than 1,100 basis points above the high end of the 65-67% guide and roughly double the 51.1% the company posted just one quarter earlier.[^10] [^15] Non-GAAP EPS of $23.41 cleared the $14.66 consensus by 60%.[^10] Datacenter revenue, the segment the bull case rests on, hit $1.467B — up 233% sequentially and 645% year-over-year off a $213M comp in Q4 FY25 when the spin completed.[^10] [^16] By any framework, this was a print that re-rated the franchise in a single afternoon.

The slope of the four-quarter trajectory is the part that broke models built on smoothed cyclical recoveries. Revenue traveled from $1.90B in the stub Q4 FY25 quarter immediately post-spin, to $2.31B in Q1, to $3.03B in Q2, to $5.95B in Q3 — and management then guided Q4 to $7.75-8.25B, implying another 30%-plus sequential step.[^10] [^14] [^15] [^16]

The segment mix shift is where the equity story actually lives. Datacenter went from a $213M curiosity at spin to a $1.47B run-rate engine in three quarters; over the same span, Edge — the embedded and enterprise SSD bucket — expanded from $1.10B to $3.66B, while Consumer (cards, USB, retail SSD) actually contracted 10% sequentially.[^10] [^16] [^17] [^18] The implication is straightforward and important: the revenue beat is being delivered by the two end-markets directly exposed to AI training and inference build-out, not by a broad NAND-cycle tide.

:::quote(attr="David Goeckeler, CEO, SanDisk, Q3 FY2026 earnings call")
This quarter marks a fundamental inflection point for Sandisk. We are advancing to a new business model built on multi-year customer engagements backed by firm financial commitments.
:::

On the call, CFO Luis Visoso disclosed that the three new-business-model (NBM) contracts signed in the quarter carry minimum contractual revenue of approximately $42B, and that five NBMs to date are now backed by over $11B in financial guarantees from customers.[^13] Capex was $240M, just 4% of revenue, against $2.955B of adjusted free cash flow — a 49.7% FCF margin in a memory business that historically struggled to clear 10%.[^13] Goeckeler also raised the CY2026 datacenter NAND growth outlook from "the 60s" to "mid-70s" percent.[^13]

The counterpoint, and the one bears managed to ignore on the day: bit shipments were flat year-over-year and down high-teens sequentially.[^13] In other words, none of the 97% sequential revenue jump came from volume. It came entirely from ASP and mix — datacenter and high-capacity enterprise SKUs priced at multi-x premia to consumer NAND, sold under take-or-pay NBM contracts that lock the price in. That is a structural reset if it holds, and a giant working-capital trap if it doesn't: the 10-Q shows the offsetting accounts-receivable build, and any reversion in spot pricing into a backlog of high-priced WIP would compress margin in both directions at once.[^12]

It matters because the print mechanically forced every sell-side model to mark earnings power higher. At guided Q4 revenue and a 75-78% gross-margin run-rate, SanDisk is now annualizing somewhere north of $100 in non-GAAP EPS — a number that, six months ago, was outside the 95th-percentile bull case. The $1,333 tape is the market's first attempt to price what that earnings stream is worth across a cycle, and the next four sections take apart whether it can.

## 03. Why now: AI memory's structural rewiring

NAND was never on the AI bingo card — HBM was. The structural rewiring of 2025-2026 is that hyperscaler capex inflected from ~$400B to a run-rate near $725B with explicit memory call-outs in every Q1 2026 print, enterprise SSD lead times stretched past 16-20 weeks, and the workload itself moved bits: inference checkpointing, KV-cache spillover, and HDD-to-eSSD substitution at the rack converted datacenter NAND from a forecasted high-teens grower into a {accent}mid-70s percent bit-demand year{/}.[^13]

Each of those numbers carried a memory asterisk. Microsoft CFO Amy Hood attributed {accent}$25B of the $190B FY26 capex line{/} directly to rising memory and component costs, and warned the company expects to remain capacity-constrained on GPUs, CPUs, and storage through at least 2026.[^20] [^26] Alphabet's Sundar Pichai said cloud revenue "would have been higher if you were able to meet the demand," and CFO Anat Ashkenazi lifted FY26 capex to $180-190B from $175-185B in the same call.[^22] Amazon printed $43.2B of cash capex in Q1 2026 alone — an implied ~$200B annualized — with Andy Jassy saying the constraint is now physical, not financial.[^21]

:::quote(attr="Andy Jassy, CEO, Amazon, Q1 2026 earnings call")
On memory and storage, and the supply chain... the cost of these components, particularly memory, has skyrocketed.
:::

Meta was the cleanest read-through. Susan Li raised FY26 capex to $125-145B from $120-135B and explained the delta in one sentence:

:::quote(attr="Susan Li, CFO, Meta, Q1 2026 earnings call")
We have continued to underestimate our compute needs even as we have been ramping capacity significantly.
:::

The OpenAI / Oracle Stargate buildout is the marginal demand vector. Combined planned capacity has reached ~7 GW with over $400B committed across three years; the Abilene site is already live on OCI at 1.2 GW.[^24] Oracle's Q3 FY26 RPO swelled to $553B (+325% YoY), anchored by a single $300B / five-year OpenAI contract — a backlog whose storage layer has to be filled with something, and that something is overwhelmingly TLC and QLC eSSD at the 60-122 TB capacity points.[^25] Pure Storage, a useful downstream tell, exceeded its full-year 1-2 EB hyperscaler shipment plan by the end of Q3 FY26 — a quarter early.[^23]

The translation into SanDisk's order book is now visible in the tape, not the model. The Datacenter segment trajectory tells the story without commentary: {sparkline:213,269,440,1467} — $213M in Q4 FY25 to $1,467M in Q3 FY26, a ~7x ramp in three quarters.[^13] On that print, CEO David Goeckeler raised the CY26 datacenter NAND bit-growth view from "the 60s" to {accent}the mid-70s percent{/}, and described enterprise SSD lead times at 16-20+ weeks with no near-term path to easing.[^13]

:::callout(kind=warn, label=Counterpoint)
Hyperscaler capex digestion has triggered every prior memory crash — Q4 2018 → Q1 2019 saw a >40% spot NAND drawdown after a nearly identical capex-led inflection. Microsoft's $25B "memory" line item also bundles DRAM with NAND, so the cleanest read-through to SanDisk's specific socket is indirect; the company benefits because the entire memory complex is tight, not because hyperscalers have line-itemed NAND.[^20]
:::

Why this matters: AI capex is the demand vector that converted SanDisk from a cyclical castoff into a critical supplier. The prior cycle's bull case required a consumer-PC and smartphone-unit recovery that never came; this one requires only that hyperscalers spend the capex they have already guided to — and Jassy, Pichai, Hood, and Li have all said, on the record, that they intend to.[^19] [^20] [^21] [^22]

## 04. The NAND market in three numbers

The 2026 NAND market is a textbook shortage: bit supply growth is capped in the high teens by cleanroom and capex discipline, contract ASPs rose 55-60% QoQ in Q1 with another 70-75% projected for Q2, and channel inventories have collapsed from roughly 12 weeks to 2-4 weeks. The result is a top-five oligopoly whose Q4 2025 revenue clustered at $21.2B, with Samsung and SK Group taking exactly half the pie and the next three names — Kioxia, Micron, SanDisk — separated by less than 150 basis points of share.

Q4 2025 top-five NAND revenue printed $21.17B, +23.8% QoQ, with Samsung at $6.6B, SK Group at $5.21B, and Kioxia / Micron / SanDisk bunched between $3.0B and $3.3B.[^29] The compression of the back half of the leaderboard is the structurally novel feature — three producers within a rounding error of each other means none of them individually can swing supply without immediate share consequences, which is precisely the condition under which oligopoly discipline holds.

TrendForce upgraded Q1 2026 contract pricing to +55-60% QoQ across every product category — client SSD, enterprise SSD, eMMC / UFS, and wafer — with all categories posting record sequential gains.[^28] The Q2 print is projected at +70-75% QoQ on contract NAND.[^30] Compounding two consecutive quarters in that range implies contract ASPs more than doubling inside six months, against a bit-supply backdrop the industry itself describes as fixed. ==Counterpoint Research's parallel forecast for Q2 2026 is more conservative — 15-20% server and PC DRAM increases — so the +70-75% NAND figure is the upper end of credible estimates, not a consensus number.==

> NAND demand significantly in excess of our available supply for the foreseeable future.
> — Sanjay Mehrotra, CEO, Micron, fiscal Q2 2026 earnings call (March 2026)

Mehrotra's framing is corroborated by the print behind it: Micron's NAND segment did $5.0B in revenue (+169% YoY) with blended NAND ASPs up in the high-70s percent QoQ and data center NAND revenue more than doubling sequentially.[^31] SK Hynix's Q1 2026 came in at KRW 52.58T (+198% YoY) with operating profit of KRW 37.61T, on bit shipments up roughly 10% QoQ and NAND ASPs up in the low-30s percent QoQ — a number that already looks stale next to the Q1 contract data.[^32] The capex side is the binding constraint: 2026 industry NAND capex is projected at $22.2B, up only 5% YoY from $21.1B in 2025, and TrendForce explicitly notes the dollars are flowing into "process upgrades and hybrid bonding rather than capacity expansion."[^27] Samsung cut 2025 wafer output to 4.72M from 5.07M (-12.8%) and SK Hynix trimmed NAND output ~10% in 2H25; Samsung is reportedly weighing 20-30% or more in 2026 price hikes on top of the contract trajectory above.[^34] Kioxia, on its Q3 FY25 print, told the market that its entire 2026 NAND production is sold out and pegged industry bit growth at high-teens in CY26 versus mid-teens in CY25.[^35] Channel inventory has done the rest of the work: enterprise SSD contract prices rose 40-50% in Q4 2025 alone as channel weeks-of-supply collapsed from ~12 in 2024 to 2-4 by October 2025.[^33]

The bear counter is the one that always wins eventually. Supplier "discipline" in NAND has historically broken precisely at peak ASPs — that is the regime in which the marginal dollar of capex earns its highest expected return, and the cleanroom-constrained framing relies on every one of five producers staying in formation. Samsung's mooted 20-30% 2026 hike[^34] is the kind of price signal that, in prior cycles, has pulled forward investment decisions at the very moment buyers were assuming supply was fixed; the more credibly the industry telegraphs a shortage, the stronger the incentive for any one producer to defect on capacity. The Kioxia "fully booked" claim[^35] is itself a forward commitment that constrains 2026 but says nothing about 2027 wafer starts.

Why this matters for SanDisk: at 14.3% share the company is a price-taker, not a price-setter — every basis point of contract ASP flows through a fixed wafer base. In a regime where two consecutive quarters of +55-75% QoQ pricing are stacking on top of a bit-shipment line that is, by the industry's own admission, capped in the high teens, SanDisk's earnings power is a leveraged bet on the oligopoly holding its line through 2026.

## 05. Kioxia is the other side of the trade

SanDisk does not own a single wafer fab. Every bit it sells is etched in a Japanese cleanroom it co-funds with Kioxia, and the public-market re-rating of that partner since December 2024 is, mechanically, the same trade as SNDK with different financing on top.

The structure is unusual for a $200B-market-cap semiconductor name. SanDisk procures substantially all of its flash-based memory wafers from three joint ventures with Kioxia — Flash Partners, Flash Alliance, and Flash Forward, collectively "Flash Ventures" — operating across seven manufacturing facilities in Japan.[^41] SanDisk is contractually obligated to fund 49.9-50.0% of each entity's capital investments whenever JV operating cash flow is insufficient.[^41] That is not a supply contract; it is a co-ownership of the bit supply, with the upstream P&L of memory manufacturing showing up as a take-or-pay obligation on SanDisk's balance sheet rather than as depreciation.

The other side of that 50/50 was for years a private Bain-controlled entity. That changed on December 18, 2024, when Kioxia listed on the Tokyo Stock Exchange at ¥1,455/share, raising ¥120B at a ¥784B (~$5.2B) market capitalization.[^37] Post-IPO ownership: Bain Capital ~52%, Toshiba ~32%, SK Hynix ~14% via convertibles with voting capped at 15% until 2028.[^37] Eighteen months later, Kioxia (TSE 285A) traded at ¥49,770 and closed May 18, 2026 at a ¥28.1T market cap — a +3,155% gain since IPO, with an all-time high of ¥53,490 on May 14.[^38]

The two re-ratings are not coincident — they are co-determined. Both companies sell the same bits off the same Yokkaichi and Kitakami lines, split roughly down the middle, and both reprice when the marginal AI-server SSD bid shows up at the JV's door.

> Kioxia Corporation and SanDisk Corporation today announced that they have entered into an agreement to extend the term of the Yokkaichi Joint Venture Agreement through December 31, 2034.
> — Kioxia / SanDisk joint statement, January 29, 2026 [^36]

That January 2026 extension is the most important governance event in the SNDK story since the spin. Two things were settled in one document. First, the bilateral economics of the largest NAND fab cluster on earth were locked in for another full decade — through the next two technology nodes, BiCS10 and BiCS11.[^36] Second, SanDisk agreed to pay Kioxia $1.165B over 2026-2029 in connection with the extension, effectively a true-up of past Yokkaichi capex that had been carried asymmetrically.[^36] The long-rumored full merger path is now formally off the table; the partners chose to keep separate equity stories and a permanent supply joint venture instead.

The roadmap they extended is competitive but not dominant. BiCS8 (218L) entered production at Kitakami Fab2 in September 2025 with CMOS-bonded-to-Array integration.[^39] BiCS9 — also 218L hybrid CBA — added Toggle DDR6.0 at 4.8 Gbps (+33% IO speed) and remains a Yokkaichi product.[^42] BiCS10 (332L, +59% bit density) was originally slated for 2H27 mass production but has been pulled into 2026 at Kitakami specifically to chase AI data-center SSD demand.[^40]

{accent}The counterpoint is the same as the architecture{/}: SanDisk's lack of owned fabs is a structural vulnerability that compounds in any downcycle. There is no manufacturing alternative — no idle Texas or Singapore line to redirect — and SK Hynix's August 2025 mass production of industry-first 321-layer QLC NAND already puts the Kioxia / SanDisk alliance one technology generation behind on layer count, even as BiCS10 closes the gap.[^43] The asymmetric dependency runs the other way too: Kioxia is contractually obliged to sell SanDisk roughly half of whatever it makes, at JV cost.

Why this matters: anyone shorting SNDK against the cycle is implicitly long the same NAND complex that Kioxia rode 31x in seventeen months — and the bilateral economics that drove that re-rating now extend, by signed contract, to 2034.

## 06. Balance sheet: zero debt, $6B buyback, and the working-capital tell

In nine months, SanDisk flipped from a levered spin-out into a debt-free cash compounder — and then immediately announced a $6B buyback authorization that, against ~158M shares at ~$1,333, retires only ~2.8% of the float.[^44] [^46] The cleanest forward risk indicator now sits on the asset side: a $1.66B build in accounts receivable that mirrors the same cash generation funding the deleveraging.[^44]

:::

The cash-flow build is the part that demands attention. Nine-month operating cash flow swung to +$4,545M from -$10M in the prior-year period, with Q3 alone producing $2,993M of free cash flow on $134M of nine-month capex (just $45M in Q3).[^44] CFO Luis Visoso flagged the structural delta on the call: adjusted FCF margin of 49.7%, with capex running at roughly 4% of revenue versus a historical mid-teens norm — a function of the joint-venture model with Kioxia, where SanDisk pays its share of fab capex through wafer prepayments rather than capitalized PP&E.[^13] Those prepayments are real cash, just routed through a different line: $856M of unamortized prepayments to Kioxia for the Y7, K1, and K2 buildings sit on the balance sheet today, with an additional $373M of contractual prepayment commitments scheduled through FY29.[^44]

The counterpoint sits in working capital. Of the $4,545M nine-month OCF, the operating line absorbed a $1,658M use from accounts receivable growth and a further $159M from inventory build — partially offset by a $486M source from contract liabilities and $640M from income taxes payable.[^44] A/R of $2.73B against an annualized run-rate of roughly $9-10B works out to roughly one quarter of revenue on the books — entirely normal for a memory company in an up-cycle, but mechanically asymmetric to the downside.

:::callout(kind=warn, label="Working-capital risk")
The $1.66B nine-month A/R build is the cleanest forward indicator on the balance sheet. SanDisk is shipping more product, at higher prices, to customers extending payment cycles into a tight-supply environment — the same conditions that produced the cash generation also stretched the receivable. If contract NAND pricing rolls before that A/R clears, customers can renegotiate or push out, and the inventory build (+$159M nine-month) becomes carry on falling-value bits. The same dynamic operates symmetrically on contract liabilities (+$486M): customer prepayments that look like a free funding source in tight markets unwind quickly when supply loosens.
:::

Against that backdrop, the April 30 buyback authorization is a deliberate but measured signal. Visoso framed the timing precisely: the authorization "reflects the company's attainment of a net cash position following full repayment of its TLB."[^13] But the math is what it is — $6B divided by ~158M shares outstanding at the post-print ~$1,333 reference price equals roughly 4.5M shares, or ~2.8% of the float.[^46] That is a credible commitment to floor the equity, not a transformational reduction in share count.

Why this matters: a zero-debt balance sheet generating 49.7% FCF margins gives CEO David Goeckeler an enormous degree of capital-allocation freedom — the kind of optionality that lets a company fund High Bandwidth Flash development, write Kioxia checks for the next fab building, and buy back equity simultaneously without touching the revolver.[^13] [^44] The unresolved question is whether $6B of buybacks executed at ~$1,333 — a price that already reflects a 28-fold run — will look brilliant or disastrous in two years. The A/R line will tell that story first.

## 07. High Bandwidth Flash: the HBM analog SanDisk is building with SK Hynix

SanDisk's High Bandwidth Flash (HBF) is a credible attempt to insert NAND into the in-package AI memory bill of materials, but it will be a positioning story until a GPU vendor commits silicon to a reference platform. The MOU with SK Hynix dated August 6 2025 standardizes the interface, the spec lands at HBM4-compatible footprints, and first samples are targeted for 2H 2026 — but no announced Nvidia or AMD design win yet anchors the roadmap.[^47] [^53]

:::kv
- {term: First sample, def: "2H 2026"}
- {term: First AI-inference device, def: "Early 2027"}
- {term: Gen1 bandwidth, def: "1.6 TB/s read"}
- {term: Gen1 capacity, def: "512 GB per 16-die stack (256 Gb die)"}
- {term: Capacity vs HBM, def: "8-16x at similar cost"}
- {term: NAND base, def: "BiCS8 218L with CBA bonding"}
:::

Gen1 HBF targets {accent}1.6 TB/s read bandwidth{/} from a 16-high stack of 256 Gb BiCS8 die, yielding 512 GB per package while closely matching the {accent}physical footprint, power profile, and stack height of HBM4{/}.[^52] SanDisk's own fact sheet projects a Gen2 step to >2 TB/s and 1 TB stack capacity at 0.8x Gen1 power, and a Gen3 step to >3.2 TB/s and 1.5 TB at 0.64x Gen1 power — aggressive scaling that assumes both BiCS9-class die and a continued cadence on the CBA hybrid-bonding line.[^48] The MOU with SK Hynix is the load-bearing piece: it standardizes the spec across the two largest NAND-and-HBM combined footprints in the industry, which is what GPU controllers would need to design around a non-proprietary interface.[^47]

The supporting governance moves are deliberate. The HBF Technical Advisory Board, announced July 24 2025, is chaired by {accent}David Patterson{/} (RISC co-creator, UC Berkeley) and includes {accent}Raja Koduri{/} (ex-AMD SVP, ex-Intel EVP) — the kind of names that read as a stalking horse for GPU-side engagement rather than a technical body.[^49] In parallel, SanDisk's UltraQLC platform gives HBF a manufacturing flywheel: the SN670 128 TB and UltraQLC 256 TB U.2 SKUs disclosed at FMS 2025 ship in 1H 2026 with a 512 TB SKU in 2027 and a 1 PB stretch target, all running on the same BiCS8 / CBA base wafer that feeds HBF.[^50] [^54]

:::timeline
- {date: 2025-02, headline: "Investor Day — UltraQLC roadmap", body: "SanDisk lays out 128 TB to 1 PB SSD progression as the post-spin growth story."}
- {date: 2025-07-24, headline: "HBF Technical Advisory Board formed", body: "Chaired by David Patterson; Raja Koduri joins."}
- {date: 2025-08-05, headline: "UltraQLC SN670 SKUs disclosed", body: "128 TB + 256 TB NVMe in U.2; 1H 2026 availability."}
- {date: 2025-08-06, headline: "SanDisk + SK Hynix MOU on HBF", body: "Two largest combined NAND/HBM footprints align on a single spec."}
- {date: 2026-2H, headline: "First HBF samples", body: "Gen1 16-high BiCS8 stack at 1.6 TB/s, 512 GB per package."}
- {date: 2027-Q1, headline: "First HBF-equipped AI inference devices", body: "Target window per SanDisk guidance."}
- {date: 2027, headline: "512 TB UltraQLC eSSD", body: "Same BiCS8/CBA base wafer as HBF — shared manufacturing flywheel."}
:::

> HBF is being driven by the growing memory requirements of large language models in inference. In particular, the key-value cache, which caches context information, scales strongly with sequence lengths and batch sizes.
> — paraphrased from Blocks and Files, Aug 7 2025 [^51]

The technical thesis hangs on one workload: KV-cache-resident inference. As context windows extend past 1M tokens and batch sizes scale to amortize prefill, the KV cache outgrows the HBM stack on a single accelerator, and serving systems either page to host DRAM (slow, bandwidth-starved) or shard across more GPUs (expensive). HBF's pitch is {accent}8-16x the capacity of HBM at similar cost{/} in the same package — enough headroom to keep the cache resident for far longer contexts, with bandwidth in the same order of magnitude as HBM3.[^47] [^52] That is a real architectural gap, and it is qualitatively different from "use more SSDs," because the latency floor of an in-package stack is set by package traces, not PCIe.

The counterpoint is unavoidable: {accent}NAND read latency is still roughly 100-1000x DRAM{/}, even with CBA bonding shortening the I/O path. Any claim that HBF "accelerates KV cache" depends on prefetch heuristics and tiering policies the GPU vendor has to implement in the memory controller and the runtime — and neither Nvidia nor AMD has disclosed such a path. The TAB is advisors, not committed customers. The MOU is between memory vendors, not between a memory vendor and a compute vendor. Until a GPU reference design lists HBF in its BOM, every spec slide is a proposal, not a product.

Why this matters: if HBF lands in a single Nvidia or AMD reference platform by 2027, SanDisk's addressable surface shifts from {accent}datacenter SSDs at ~$70-100B TAM{/} to a slice of the in-package AI memory market — and the multiple the equity has historically earned on NAND cycles is the wrong frame for that business.

## 08. Valuation: cheap on spot, expensive on the cycle

At $1,333 SanDisk trades at a ~$209B market cap and a ~$205B enterprise value, putting the Q4-annualized forward P/E at ~10.6x — the highest in the memory complex but still inside the historical 5-10x band that NAND and DRAM names compress into at the top of every cycle.[^61]

The math is mechanical. ~158M diluted shares times $1,333 ≈ $209B equity value. Net the ~$4B of net cash that fell out of the Q3 balance sheet and you get a ~$205B enterprise value.[^10] Capitalize the Q4 FY26 guide midpoint of $31.50 in non-GAAP EPS at four quarters and you get ~$126 of run-rate earnings; $1,333 / $126 = {accent}10.6x{/}.[^10] That is the multiple the bulls underwrite. Stockanalysis publishes a tighter 8.31x forward P/E using sell-side consensus that already partially rolls forward CY27,[^55] but for a top-of-cycle stock the four-quarter anchor is the conservative read.

The real fight is over the denominator, not the numerator. Morgan Stanley models CY2026 EPS at $127.92, ~65% above the $77.55 Street consensus and almost exactly on top of the Q4-annualized $126.[^60] In other words: the bull case, the sell-side outlier, and the company's own near-term run-rate all converge on roughly the same earnings power. Consensus is the outlier — it is still half-pricing a soft landing in CY27.

Every prior NAND peak has resolved the cheap-or-expensive paradox the same way: the multiple compresses because the earnings number is recognized as unsustainable. Micron at the FY18 peak printed ~$11.95 of EPS and traded at roughly 3.5-5x forward on that number before the cycle rolled — and the stock still lost half its value as EPS collapsed back to a loss.[^61] A 10.6x multiple on $126 of peak-quarter-annualized EPS already implicitly assumes that SNDK earns more through the cycle than the bear-case base rate has historically allowed.
:::

That is the counterpoint sitting underneath the screen. Run the bear math: if CY27 EPS halves to ~$63 as bit pricing normalizes and HBF ramps slip a quarter, and the multiple compresses to the historical 6x trough — call it the Hynix print today — you land at $63 × 6 = $378 a share, a {accent}~72% drawdown{/} from $1,333 without anything actually "breaking." That is not a forecast; it is the arithmetic of a normal mean-reversion. Conversely, if MS is right that $128 of CY26 EPS is durable into CY27 and the market re-rates SNDK toward HBM-economics multiples (call it 14-16x), the same shares clear $1,800-$2,050.

Why this matters: the SNDK valuation argument is not really an argument about what multiple to apply. The peer set narrows the multiple to a 5-10x band and the spread inside that band is mostly noise. The argument is about which earnings number to capitalize — the $77 consensus, the $126 run-rate, or some normalized through-cycle figure between $40 and $60 that nobody on the sell-side is willing to publish. Pick the EPS, the price prints itself.

## 09. What could break the thesis

NAND is the most violently cyclical commodity in semiconductors, and the same five-producer oligopoly that is "disciplined" today defected in every prior cycle. The bull case for SNDK at $1,333 requires believing the 2026 upcycle is structurally different — that AI memory demand has rewired the supply curve permanently. History says that bet has paid off exactly zero times. Below: the cycle-top stack, the YMTC ramp the bulls dismiss, the insider tape, and the math on a peer-median reversion.

Those are not tail-risk scenarios. They are the modal outcome of every memory cycle since the industry consolidated to five producers. The {accent}-68% DRAM per-GB collapse{/} of 2018-19 happened with a tighter oligopoly than exists today — and it followed a capex inflection identical in shape to what 2026 has telegraphed. Micron's FY23 10-K is the document the bulls should be made to read aloud: an investment-grade producer with one of the lowest cost curves in the industry generated {accent}negative 9.1% gross margin{/} for a full fiscal year.[^62] The same firm, the same fabs, the same management — one cycle later.

### The YMTC encroachment the bulls hand-wave

The disciplined-oligopoly thesis assumes the supply side stays at five. It is becoming six.

YMTC went from ~10% global NAND shipment share in Q1 2025 to {accent}13% by Q3 2025{/}, and is publicly targeting 15% by end-2026.[^70] [^67] The $3B Wuhan Phase III venture — 100k wpm per phase, mass production 2H 2026 — is engineered to be sanctions-proof, with >50% of equipment from Chinese suppliers.[^68] [^67] TechInsights' teardown confirms YMTC's 512 Gb die hits 12.66 Gb/mm² — competitive with Western nodes.[^69] On the DRAM side, CXMT captured 8% of global Q3 2025 share with DDR5-8000 and HBM samples in hand.[^72] Chinese memory is not a 2030 problem. It is a 2026 problem, arriving precisely when SanDisk's cleanroom-constrained capex hits.

### The cycle-top stack

:::callout(kind=danger, label="Cycle-top stack")
Six signals firing simultaneously, any one of which would historically have been a sell trigger: +55-75% QoQ NAND contract increases printed back-to-back in Q1 and Q2 2026[^28] [^30] — the kind of multi-quarter ASP melt-up that has never not been followed by mean reversion; suppliers publicly claiming "fully booked" 2026 capacity[^35] — the standard cycle-top tell; Samsung weighing 20-30% price hikes in 2026[^34]; SK Hynix cutting NAND output ~10% in 2H 2025 to engineer the squeeze[^34]; a 2026 capex inflection across all five producers (cleanroom-constrained but real)[^27]; and SanDisk's own Q3 print where bit shipments were flat YoY — every dollar of the revenue beat was ASP, not volume.[^13]
:::

That last point is the one nobody wants to discuss. The bull case is "AI demand is structural," but SanDisk shipped roughly the same number of bits in Q3 FY26 as it did a year earlier.[^13] The 28-fold rerating since spinoff is, mechanically, a pure ASP rerating multiplied by multiple expansion. If ASPs round-trip — even partially — both legs of that compound unwind.

### Insiders are heading for the door

Stanley Druckenmiller's Duquesne Family Office liquidated its entire 166,235-share SNDK position in Q4 2025 after a single quarter of holding, having ridden roughly a +400% move.[^71] ==Druckenmiller did re-open a smaller 38,155-share position in Q1 2026, which complicates the "heading for the door" read — but the round-trip out of the original ~5x position is itself the signal.== Druckenmiller does not, as a rule, sell early in structural stories. The 13F is not a thesis-killer on its own, but it is the kind of signal that, in retrospect, marks the top of memory cycles with uncomfortable frequency.

### The bull rebuttal and why it isn't enough

The bull rebuttal is that this time AI demand is structural and supply is cleanroom-constrained through 2027. Both claims are partially true. HBM and HBF demand are real, hyperscaler capex is real, and wafer-fab equipment lead times are long. But the historical record is that every NAND downturn arrived with a similar "structural demand" narrative attached — datacenter NAND in 2018, enterprise SSD in 2022 — and prices fell ~50% anyway because supply discipline broke at the margin, demand elasticity bit, or both. The marginal Chinese wafer does not need to be cost-competitive to crater pricing; it needs to be merely additive into an inelastic short-term demand curve.

### The drawdown math

Run a historical mid-cycle compression: EPS halves from ~$120 forward to ~$60 normalized; the multiple compresses to the peer median (~6x forward P/E, where Micron and SK Hynix have spent most of their lives). That implies $60 × 6x ≈ $360/share — a {accent}~73% drawdown from $1,333{/}. This is not a worst-case. The worst case is the FY23 Micron precedent: negative gross margin, EPS goes negative, and the multiple becomes irrelevant because the denominator does not exist. A 28-fold gain in 15 months is precisely the kind of move that creates the conditions for a 70%+ drawdown — and historically in this industry, both have been true of the same security within a 36-month window.

## 10. Verdict: which earnings number is the article about?

After eight sections of evidence, the SanDisk debate collapses to one question — which line of the income statement gets capitalized into the equity. Everything else is a side argument. The peer set narrows the forward P/E to a 5-10x band that the equity already lives inside; sell-side targets cluster $1,100-$1,700 because every desk picked a slightly different EPS to multiply. The cycle history says peak EPS does not deserve a peak multiple. The bull case says this is not a peak.

:::kv
- {term: What kills the bull case, def: "Samsung announces materially higher 2027 NAND wafer adds; hyperscaler CY27 capex prints flat or down; SanDisk Q4 FY26 misses the $7.75-8.25B revenue guide; bit shipments grow while ASPs roll over (the classic supply-response signature)."}
- {term: What kills the bear case, def: "HBF lands in an Nvidia or AMD reference platform with disclosed BOM allocation; two more producers commit to 2027 contract increases; CY27 hyperscaler capex prints above $750B; the NBM contracted backlog grows materially beyond the $42B from the three Q3 deals."}
- {term: What both sides agree on, def: "The Q3 FY26 print was real; the $42B NBM backlog is real; the JV with Kioxia is locked through 2034; SanDisk has zero debt and 49.7% FCF margin at peak. The disagreement is only about how long that peak holds."}
:::

Two strikingly different earnings paths sit inside the same data. Capitalize the bull-case run-rate (Morgan Stanley's $127.92 CY26 EPS, almost exactly Q4-annualized) at a memory-leader multiple of 12-14x and you get $1,500-$1,800 — a stock that is fairly priced today, slightly cheap on the upside. Capitalize a normalized through-cycle EPS of $50-60 at the peer-median 6x and you get $300-$360 — a 70-77% drawdown without anything actually "breaking," just the next NAND cycle landing on schedule.[^61] The probability mass between those outcomes is wider than the implied distribution in either the sell-side targets or the options skew.

A practical frame for what to watch over the next four quarters:

- {flag:green} Q4 FY26 (reports July/August 2026): does revenue hit the $7.75-8.25B guide and does Visoso re-anchor the FCF margin at >40%? Does the A/R line continue to balloon or stabilize?
- {flag:yellow} CY2026 hyperscaler capex prints in October-November: do MSFT, GOOG, META, AMZN sustain their guides into CY27, or quietly trim?
- {flag:yellow} TrendForce Q3 2026 NAND contract data: do prices accelerate, stabilize, or roll? A flattening of the +70-75% QoQ trajectory[^30] is the first quantitative signal that supply discipline is bending.
- {flag:red} A single hyperscaler reduces an LTA, or a sixth producer (YMTC, Solidigm, even Samsung) signals materially higher 2027 NAND wafer adds. That is the cycle-top inflection memory has flagged five times since 2007.[^62] [^64]

The article's title called this "the anatomy of a 28-fold tape." Anatomy is the right word: the gain is real, the cash flow is real, the joint venture and the technology roadmap are real, and so is the historical base rate that NAND mean-reverts. Position sizing — not the long/short call — is the trade. The next four quarters will tell whether $1,333 is a midpoint or a memorial.
