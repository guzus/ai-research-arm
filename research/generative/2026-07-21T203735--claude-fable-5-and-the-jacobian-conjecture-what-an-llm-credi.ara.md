## 1. The claim, plainly

On July 20, 2026, an Anthropic researcher told the internet that an 87-year-old open problem in algebraic geometry was dead, and that an AI model had helped kill it.

:::quote(attr="Levent Alpöge, X, July 20 2026")
hello there the jacobian conjecture is false thanx to my close friend akhil for asking about it and my other close friend fable for working during the world cup final
:::

Stripped of the deadpan, the post is a specific, falsifiable mathematical claim. Levent Alpöge — an Anthropic researcher — announced on X that the Jacobian conjecture is false, crediting mathematician Akhil Mathew for posing the question that reopened the search and Claude Fable 5, Anthropic's model, for the construction work done "during the World Cup final" [^1]. The conjecture in question, formulated in 1939 by Ott-Heinrich Keller, says that any polynomial map from complex n-space to itself whose Jacobian determinant is a nonzero constant must be invertible — one of the cleaner statements in mathematics, and one that had resisted every attempted proof or disproof for generations, landing as Problem 16 on Stephen Smale's 1998 list of problems for the 21st century [^10].

What makes this claim unusually easy to check, rather than merely unusually bold, is its form. Alpöge did not assert a proof of anything; he supplied an object. The map is explicit: F: **C**³ → **C**³, built from degree-7 polynomials, with Jacobian determinant identically equal to −2 — a nonzero constant, exactly satisfying the conjecture's hypothesis [^2][^3]. And yet three distinct points feed into the same output: F(0, 0, −1/4), F(1, −3/2, 13/2), and F(−1, 3/2, 13/2) all map to (−1/4, 0, 0) [^2][^3]. A map that collapses three different inputs to one output is not injective, and a map that isn't injective cannot be invertible — full stop, regardless of what its Jacobian determinant says. If the arithmetic checks out, that single map is a counterexample, and the conjecture is false as originally stated. No induction, no long chain of lemmas, no appeal to a deep structural theorem — just polynomials that anyone with a computer algebra system can plug in and evaluate.

- {label: "Jacobian determinant", value: "−2"}

That checkability is precisely why the claim moved from a single tweet to something resembling scientific consensus within a day. Mathematician David Speyer reconstructed the map from the posted coordinates and verified the constant Jacobian and the map's generic non-injectivity on his own blog within hours of the announcement [^2]; a companion write-up pinned down the exact three colliding points [^3], and other mathematicians ran independent verification by counting points over finite fields, all arriving at the same answer [^2]. None of this required trusting Alpöge's word or Anthropic's model; it required trusting arithmetic, which is a much lower bar. Within less than a day of the original post, the core computational claim — this specific polynomial map is non-injective despite a constant nonzero Jacobian — had been reproduced by people with no stake in whether an AI model gets credit for it [^2].

It also matters who is making the claim. Alpöge is not an anonymous account or a corporate PR handle testing a talking point; he holds a 2020 Princeton PhD supervised by Fields Medalist Manjul Bhargava [^8], was a Junior Fellow at Harvard's Society of Fellows, and won the 2015 Morgan Prize, the top undergraduate mathematics award in the United States [^25]. That pedigree does not make the counterexample correct — mathematics does not defer to credentials — but it does mean the claim arrived already filtered through someone whose professional incentives run toward getting this right, not toward a viral moment. The combination of a credentialed author, an explicit checkable object, and same-day third-party reproduction is a meaningfully different epistemic situation than a bare AI-generated claim with no way to verify it.

None of that, however, is the same as the conjecture being settled. What has actually been checked so far is arithmetic on one specific map: that the Jacobian determinant really is a nonzero constant and that three named points really do collide. It has not yet passed through formal peer review, and "independently reproduced by mathematicians on their blogs within a day" is a strong signal, not a publication. The counterexample as posted also only addresses dimension three and above — the classical n = 2 case, which is arguably the version of the conjecture mathematicians cared most about, remains open, a distinction the next section takes up directly. Readers should treat this as "announced and independently checked," not "proven" or "published," until a refereed writeup exists.

Why this matters is not just that a decades-old problem may have fallen — plenty of open problems eventually do — but that the falling happened in public, in under a day, in a form ordinary working mathematicians could pick up and test themselves, with an AI model named as a collaborator in the process rather than as the sole author of an unverifiable black box.

## 2. What's actually false — and what's still open

Reading the July 2026 headlines, it is easy to come away thinking "the Jacobian conjecture" — one thing, singular — has been resolved. It has not: the new result is a genuine disproof, but it is a disproof of the general-dimension statement, and it leaves the specific case mathematicians have cared about most for 87 years completely untouched.

The conjecture, stated precisely, concerns a polynomial map F over a field of characteristic zero: if the Jacobian determinant det(J_F) is a nonzero *constant*, F is conjectured to have a polynomial inverse [^2]. The logical hinge is the direction of the implication. By the chain rule and the inverse function theorem, a constant nonzero Jacobian is *necessary* for a polynomial map to be invertible with a polynomial inverse — that half has never been in doubt. What has been open since Ott-Heinrich Keller first posed it in 1939 is whether the condition is also *sufficient*: does every polynomial map with constant nonzero Jacobian have to be invertible? "Sufficient" is exactly the direction a counterexample attacks, and that is exactly what the July 2026 construction supplies — a map with constant nonzero Jacobian that provably is **not** invertible [^2].

But sufficiency fails only in some dimensions, not all of them, and the dimension-by-dimension picture is the whole story:

:::kv
- {term: "n = 1 (single variable)", def: "Trivially true — solved from the start"}
- {term: "n = 2 (the plane, Keller's original 1939 case)", def: "STILL OPEN"}
- {term: "n = 3", def: "Disproved, July 2026"}
- {term: "n ≥ 4", def: "Disproved by padding with identity coordinates"}
:::

The n=1 case was never a mystery: if f'(x) equals a nonzero constant c, integrating forces f(x) = cx + b, which is invertible by construction — there is no room for a counterexample in one variable, and there never was [^2]. The new work is a specific, degree-7 construction in **three** variables (n=3) [^3]. From there, extending it to every n≥3 is a routine, undisputed padding trick: append extra coordinates that map identically (z₄ = w₄, z₅ = w₅, and so on), which preserves the constant-Jacobian property while leaving the original three-variable failure embedded inside [^3]. That is why a single n=3 example is enough to kill the conjecture for the entire infinite family n≥3 — five extra lines of algebra, not five more independent discoveries.

None of that machinery touches n=2. This is the counterpoint that matters most: **"disproved" is not the same as "solved."** Keller's original planar formulation — arguably still the more famous version of the problem, and the one every graduate algebraic-geometry course states first — remains genuinely, completely open. It is not merely unproven in the sense that nobody has gotten around to it; n=2 has spent nearly nine decades resisting *both* a proof and a disproof, which is a qualitatively different kind of stuck than n≥3 turned out to be. Once mathematicians had the right degree-7, three-variable construction, n≥3 fell in one stroke and then generalized upward for free [^3]. The plane has offered no comparable foothold in either direction, which is precisely why it was always considered the hardest core of the problem, not a lesser corollary of it.

It's also worth being precise about how the general-n case was previously understood to reduce. The Bass–Connell–Wright reduction of 1982, building on Yagzhev's 1980 work, showed that the full conjecture (for any n) follows from a special "cubic-homogeneous" sub-case — but that reduction runs *upward* in dimension, trading a general map in n variables for a cubic-homogeneous map in some larger number of variables, and it says nothing about n=2 [^11]. The new degree-7, fixed-at-n=3 counterexample does not sit inside that classical cubic-homogeneous reduced form in any direct way — it is a separate, lower-dimensional construction that happens to falsify the general statement without engaging the reduction machinery that mathematicians had spent decades building around it [^11].

A field this stubborn has also attracted a well-documented graveyard of wrong turns, which is worth sizing up before the next section examines why a counterexample is easier to trust than a proof:

:::iso
- {label: "Confirmed flawed proofs/disproofs pre-2026", glyph: "✗", count: 5}
:::

MathWorld and other survey sources count at least five published incorrect proofs of the Jacobian conjecture, plus many more incorrect attempts that never reached print [^6][^12][^13]. The pattern repeats with names attached: in 2004, Carolyn Dean circulated a proof that was initially endorsed by commutative-algebra expert Mel Hochster, only for a gap to surface and the endorsement to be retracted [^6][^12]. In 2009, Dang Vu Giang posted a purported proof to arXiv and did not dispute the flaw once it was pointed out [^6][^13]. Five confirmed failures is not a large number in absolute terms, but for a problem this narrowly stated — one determinant condition, one polynomial map — it is a striking density of expert-vetted, expert-retracted claims, and it is the backdrop against which any new claim, disproof or proof, should be read skeptically until independently checked.

Why this distinction matters: treating "the Jacobian conjecture is false" as a blanket headline erases the fact that mathematics' actual open problem — the n=2 case — is untouched, and conflating the two risks both overstating what July 2026 accomplished and underrating how much of Keller's original question is still standing.

## 3. Why a counterexample is not a proof — and why that's good news for confidence

A claimed **proof** of the Jacobian conjecture is a universally-quantified statement that dies the instant a single hidden case anywhere in an unbounded argument turns out to be wrong, while a claimed **counterexample** is an existential statement that lives or dies on finitely many exact, mechanically checkable arithmetic facts — which is exactly why a disproof can plausibly be nailed down (or shot down) within days, while a proof of the same conjecture has a decades-long track record of being announced and then quietly withdrawn once a referee finds the one case nobody closed [^6][^12][^13].

The asymmetry is a matter of quantifier logic, not mathematical taste. "Every polynomial map with a nonzero constant Jacobian is globally invertible" is a **∀** claim: it ranges over every dimension, every degree, every coefficient field, unboundedly. A proof has to close *every* one of those cases in a single unbroken chain of reasoning, and the failure mode is silent — a hidden degree bound smuggled in halfway through, an implicit smoothness assumption, a step that divides by an expression that can secretly vanish. Finding that one gap requires auditing the entire argument end to end, which is slow, adversarial, and exactly the kind of work formal peer review exists to do. That is precisely the graveyard the Jacobian conjecture has produced for almost ninety years: proof attempt after proof attempt, each retracted once someone located the buried flaw, a pattern documented across decades of survey literature [^6][^12][^13]. A disproof, by contrast, is an **∃** claim: "there exists at least one map for which this fails." You do not need to reason about every map in the universe — you need to produce *one* witness and check it. Checking a single finite object is bounded, mechanical work; checking an unbounded universal argument is not.

That is what this claim actually reduces to. Stripped of its narrative, it is two exact computations: first, that the determinant of the Jacobian, det(J_F), equals the nonzero constant −2 identically across the entire domain — a polynomial identity that can be verified by symbolic expansion and term cancellation, not by numerical approximation or a floating-point solver that could be hiding rounding error [^2]. Second, that two specific, named points genuinely map to the same output under F — an exact substitution of coordinates into a polynomial map, arithmetic anyone with a computer algebra system can rerun byte-for-byte and get the identical answer, with no tolerance band and no room for "close enough" [^3]. Neither check requires interpretive judgment about whether an argument's structure is sound; both are yes/no questions with a single correct answer, which is why independent parties who never saw each other's work were able to reproduce them within roughly two days [^4].

None of this makes counterexample claims immune to being wrong. The residual risk is real, just differently shaped: a miscounted dimension, a map that turns out not to actually be polynomial once its construction is unpacked, a "constant" determinant that is secretly non-constant on some lower-dimensional subvariety that a quick spot-check missed, or a global hypothesis quietly assumed away rather than proven. The failure modes for an existential claim are narrow and locatable — they live inside a finite, nameable object — but they are not zero. The correct response to that residual risk isn't reading the construction and judging whether it "feels" right; it's literal, independent recomputation of the exact identities by people who didn't see the original derivation, which is the substance of what happened in the 48 hours this story broke (see section 4) and is a categorically faster and more decisive form of scrutiny than waiting for a journal referee to spend a year hunting for a gap in a universal argument.

:::callout(kind=info, label="Local vs global")
A genuine counterexample to the Jacobian conjecture has to fail some condition beyond pointwise non-singularity — that's the entire point of the conjecture. The inverse function theorem already guarantees *local* invertibility everywhere the Jacobian determinant is a nonzero constant; the open question is whether that forces *global* invertibility. One way local-but-not-global shows up concretely: along certain input sequences, the input's magnitude can grow without bound while the output converges toward a fixed value — a failure of properness. Properness was never a hypothesis of the conjecture, so a counterexample failing to be proper doesn't weaken the claim; it's exactly the failure mode a genuine counterexample is expected to exhibit. The map looks perfectly invertible under a microscope at every single point, yet sends far-apart inputs to the same destination.
:::

This is why mathematicians with real technical standing were willing to engage with genuine scrutiny — recomputing determinants, rechecking substitutions, probing for a hidden global assumption — within a single day rather than shelving judgment for years, and why "not yet formally peer-reviewed" means something structurally different here than it would attached to a claimed proof of the same conjecture: for a proof, that caveat means the one fatal gap simply hasn't been found *yet*; for a finite, exactly-checkable counterexample, it increasingly means the checking has already been done, repeatedly, by people with every incentive to find an error and none has surfaced.

## 4. Forty-eight hours of crowdsourced peer review

With no journal referee anywhere in sight, the mathematical community assembled its own real-time verification pipeline — independent reproduction, finite-field sanity checks, and a competing Lean formalization effort — and that pipeline caught and corrected a genuine discrepancy within about a day.

The clock started at {accent}~2:19 AM UTC on July 20, 2026{/}, when Levent Alpöge posted the counterexample on X, crediting Akhil Mathew for posing the question and Claude Fable 5 for the construction itself [^1]. That is the entire initial evidentiary basis: a social-media post, not a preprint. What happened next is the substance of this section.

:::timeline
- {date: "2026-07-20, ~02:19 UTC", headline: "Alpöge posts the counterexample on X", body: "Credits Akhil Mathew for the question and Claude Fable 5 for the construction."}
- {date: "2026-07-20", headline: "David Speyer reproduces the map", body: "Confirms determinant −2 and the three-point collision on Secret Blogging Seminar; commenters run independent finite-field checks."}
- {date: "2026-07-20", headline: "Lean formalization race begins", body: "Kevin Buzzard documents a push to formalize the disproof; pull request #4474 opened against DeepMind's Formal Conjectures repo."}
- {date: "2026-07-20/21", headline: "Determinant discrepancy caught and corrected", body: "Reviewers flag that the Lean-formalized version uses a rescaled determinant of 1, not the original −2; attribution is corrected mid-review."}
- {date: "2026-07-21/22", headline: "Still no formal peer review", body: "The pull request remains open; Alpöge has not posted a full technical writeup or arXiv preprint."}
:::

Within hours of the original post, mathematician David Speyer reproduced the exact polynomial map on his "Secret Blogging Seminar" blog and confirmed the Jacobian determinant is {accent}−2{/} [^2]. That reproduction was not a solitary act of trust — Speyer's comment section became an ad hoc referee report, with other mathematicians running independent finite-field point-counting checks and separately implemented "exact verifiers" that all agreed with the original claim [^2]. This is worth pausing on: {mark}multiple people, using different tools and different code, arrived at the same numerical answer{/} before any institution had touched the claim. A companion expository write-up further pinned down the specific collision at the heart of the disproof — the three points F(0,0,−1/4) = F(1,−3/2,13/2) = F(−1,3/2,13/2) = (−1/4,0,0) — giving other researchers an exact, checkable target rather than a vague description [^3]. That write-up also confirmed the extension to every dimension n≥3 by adjoining identity coordinates, matching the padding argument described in Section 2 [^3].

In parallel, a second and more demanding form of scrutiny began: formal verification. Kevin Buzzard blogged about a "race" already underway to encode the disproof in the Lean proof assistant, framing the episode as "human mathematicians being out-counterexampled" [^4]. A pull request — #4474 — was opened against Google DeepMind's Formal Conjectures GitHub repository to add a machine-checked Lean version of the disproof [^4][^5]. Formalization is a much higher bar than a blog reproduction: Lean does not accept hand-waving, so getting the counterexample to typecheck forces every algebraic step into the open [^5].

:::callout(kind=info, label="Real scrutiny, not credulity")
It was precisely this higher-friction process — not the faster, looser blog checks — that surfaced the one actual error in the public record.
:::

That error is the crux of this section. The Lean-formalized version circulating in the pull request used a {accent}rescaled{/} counterexample with Jacobian determinant {accent}1{/}, not Alpöge's original {accent}−2{/} [^5]. Kevin Buzzard flagged the mismatch directly during review, and contributor "deancureton" acknowledged that the attribution "isn't exactly correct," explaining that Alpöge had given a hint that a particular rescaling would produce determinant 1 "to work in all characteristics" [^5]. In other words: the underlying disproof was never in doubt, but the credit trail and the exact object being formalized needed a correction, and the correction happened because a reviewer was reading closely enough to notice a mismatched constant — the kind of thing a rubber-stamp amplification process would have waved through.

Two counterpoints keep this chronology honest. First, informal, rapid, crowd-verification is not the same as a completed referee report — the pull request was still open as of the most recent check, with no indication of a merge date, and Alpöge himself has not published a full technical writeup or arXiv preprint laying out the construction end to end [^4][^5]. Second, all of the checking described here — the blog reproduction, the finite-field spot checks, the Lean formalization attempt — was performed by a small, self-selected group of mathematicians who happened to be paying attention on a given weekend, not a systematic, independent audit; convergence among a handful of quick checks is strong evidence but not proof of the kind a journal, or a fully merged and reviewed formalization, would eventually provide.

Why this matters: the speed and informality of this process is often mistaken for its absence — "no peer review yet" gets read as "no scrutiny yet." The 48-hour record here shows the opposite. Independent reproduction, independent numerical verification, and an independent, much stricter formal-verification effort converged on the same underlying disproof, and that same process caught and fixed a real discrepancy in attribution and in which determinant value was actually being formalized — evidence that the mathematical community treated an AI-assisted claim with the same skepticism it would apply to any surprising result, arguably more so, precisely because of its unusual provenance.

## 5. Human insight or machine leap?

No one directing the July 2026 Jacobian counterexample has published the process behind it, so crediting the mathematician or the model is still an open question — but this claim sits closer to genuine open-ended discovery than most of the AI-math announcements that came before it, several of which did not survive scrutiny.

Start with what is actually on the record, which is thin. The only public account of how the counterexample was produced is Levent Alpöge's own casual tweet, crediting Akhil Mathew with posing the question and Claude Fable 5 with "working" on it during a World Cup final — no published prompt, no transcript, no tool-call log, from either Alpöge or Anthropic, describing exactly how the model was used [^1]. That absence is the whole problem this section has to sit with: it means the central question — how much of the intellectual work belongs to the model versus the mathematician directing it — cannot be resolved from outside, only bounded.

Three distinct scenarios are all consistent with the public record. The model could have autonomously generated a novel construction via open-ended search of its own initiative. It could have served as a fast symbolic-algebra assistant, executing a targeted search strategy that Alpöge or Mathew had already designed. Or it could have mostly verified and formatted a result a human had essentially already found by hand. One circumstantial detail leans against the least interesting of these readings: the counterexample lands at degree 7, far below the degree-200-plus territory that older brute-force literature on this style of construction might lead one to expect [^2][^3]. A solution that compact argues for either an efficiently targeted search or genuine model-contributed insight over blind brute force — but that is an inference from the shape of the output, not a confirmed fact, since no process detail has been published by either party.

One data point cuts, in a small way, against the "the AI just got lucky once" reading. Aaron Lou, a researcher at OpenAI, has stated that an internal, non-web-search version of an OpenAI reasoning model independently derived "essentially the same" counterexample without first having seen Alpöge's announcement [^24]. That is worth taking seriously as a directional signal — it suggests the construction may be more reachable by current-generation models than a one-off fluke would imply. But it should not be over-weighted: it is a single, unverified, self-reported claim from a rival lab with an obvious incentive to stake a claim on the same result, not an independently reproduced or documented finding.

:::compare
- {role: "WEAKEST — RETRACTED", name: "OpenAI Erdős claim, Oct 2025", value: "Retracted in days"}
- {role: "STRONGEST — VERIFIED", name: "AlphaProof/AlphaGeometry, 2024 IMO", value: "Lean-checked, Nature-published"}
- {role: "SUBJECT", name: "Jacobian counterexample, Jul 2026", value: "Informally verified, unreviewed", subject: true}
:::

Placing the Jacobian claim on this spectrum requires walking the recent history of "AI solves famous math problem" announcements, because that history runs from embarrassing to rigorous, and the differences are instructive. The low end is October 2025, when an OpenAI VP publicly claimed GPT-5 had "solved" ten previously unsolved Erdős problems; the claim collapsed within days after the Erdős-problems curator showed the model had merely surfaced already-published solutions rather than produced novel proofs [^20] — a cautionary precedent for exactly the kind of fast, credit-claiming announcement this article is examining. The high end, by contrast, is Google DeepMind's AlphaProof and AlphaGeometry 2, which scored a silver-medal-equivalent 28 out of 42 at the 2024 International Mathematical Olympiad using Lean formal proof-checking, peer-reviewed and published in *Nature* [^18] — though that result required expert humans to manually translate problems into Lean and ran for two to three days of compute against a human 4.5-hour limit, so it is bounded, formal-space proof search rather than open-ended discovery. DeepMind's own Gemini Deep Think pushed further along the autonomy axis a year later, reaching full gold-medal-standard IMO performance in 2025 — 35 out of 42, five of six problems solved — entirely in natural language, with no Lean formalization step at all [^19]; that is a meaningfully more autonomous mode of operation than AlphaProof's, even though it remains a bounded competition-problem setting rather than genuine research.

The most direct precedent for how an announcement like this *should* be handled came in May 2026, when OpenAI announced — with three named mathematician endorsements — that a model had disproved an approximately 80-year-old planar unit-distance conjecture [^20]. OpenAI explicitly framed that release as a course-correction after the October 2025 Erdős retraction: this time with a published prompt and chain-of-thought, and third-party validation completed *before* the announcement went out. The Alpöge/Fable 5 story has notably not done either of those things, which is precisely why the credit question in this section stays open rather than resolved. And on pure mathematical substance, the strongest pre-Jacobian case for a model contributing real creativity — not just fast execution of known technique — is an April 2026 GPT-5.4-Pro-assisted closure of a 1968 Erdős problem: mathematician Jared Lichtman judged it genuine, "Book-proof"-quality insight, while Terence Tao separately described it as revealing a previously undocumented connection between the anatomy of integers and Markov process theory [^21]; that verdict, from two of the most credible living judges of mathematical taste, is the bar the Jacobian claim is implicitly being measured against.

Set against that spectrum, the Jacobian counterexample sits well clear of the Erdős-problems floor — independent mathematicians informally verified the specific degree-7 construction within 48 hours (see Section 4) rather than debunking a repackaged known result — but it has not yet gone through the peer review, Lean formalization, or pre-announcement third-party validation that anchor the strongest precedents. It is, in short, unresolved by design: a result whose mathematical content looks real but whose process is undocumented. That gap matters beyond this one story, because it sets the evidentiary bar the field will keep re-litigating — every future "AI solved X" claim will be read against how quickly, and how completely, this one either gets a published process or fails to.

## 6. An "87-year-old" problem? Not exactly

Nearly every headline covering the Fable 5 claim dated the Jacobian conjecture to 1939 — and a peer-reviewed 2026 paper argues that number is 55 years too young, a small but pointed reminder that the "how old is this problem" framing raced across the internet faster than the actual history of mathematics did.

The near-universal shorthand traces the conjecture to Ott-Heinrich Keller, who stated it in his 1939 paper "Ganze Cremona-Transformationen" [^9] — subtract from 2026 and you get the "87-year-old problem" line that ran everywhere. But a 2026 historical study by Lázaro O. Rodríguez Díaz, published in the peer-reviewed journal *Comptes Rendus Mathématique* (submitted to arXiv in December 2025), argues that the precise two-variable statement now called the Jacobian conjecture actually appears first in an 1884 paper on functional determinants by German mathematician Ludwig Kraus, in the Vienna Academy of Sciences proceedings — 55 years before Keller [^7]. That is not a trivial rounding difference; it is more than half a century of missing attribution baked into a number reporters treated as precise.

:::kv
- {term: "If dated from Keller (1939) — the popular framing", def: "87 years"}
- {term: "If dated from Kraus (1884), per a Dec. 2025/2026 Comptes Rendus paper", def: "142 years"}
:::

The Rodríguez Díaz paper doesn't just move the birthdate — it also finds a specific flaw in Kraus's own 1884 attempted proof: an unjustified claim that a derivative does not vanish at a particular point [^7]. That makes Kraus, on this reading, the first person on record to both state and unsuccessfully try to prove the exact conjecture now attached to Keller's name — one more entry on the list of documented failed proofs already covered in Section 2, just relocated to a century earlier than anyone had been citing.

Worth flagging as a real counterpoint: this is a single, very recent peer-reviewed paper, not yet a settled consensus. ==unverified: as of this writing, other standard math-history references have not yet incorporated the Kraus attribution==. A well-sourced correction is not the same thing as an absorbed one, and older reference works take time to catch up.

The irony writes itself. In the same 48-hour window that Wikipedia and MathWorld both updated their Jacobian-conjecture pages within about a day of the Fable 5 story breaking (Section 4/7), nobody paused to check whether the "87-year-old" figure those very updates repeated was itself correct. The number that traveled fastest was the one nobody vetted; the number that would have corrected it sat in a December 2025 journal submission most newsrooms never opened. This matters beyond trivia: it's a compact case study in how a viral, informally-verified framing — even one dressed up with a specific, credible-sounding figure — can outrun the slower, peer-reviewed scholarship that actually settles it, which is precisely the dynamic this article's other sections trace in the mathematical claim itself.

## 7. Anthropic's calculated silence

Anthropic has not issued a single official blog post, press release, or corporate statement about the Jacobian conjecture counterexample, and that silence sits in sharp contrast to how quickly and directly the company spoke when the same model, Claude Fable 5, became the subject of a government export-control order weeks earlier.

Start with what Anthropic did say, on the record, about Fable 5. When the model launched on June 9, 2026, the company's own materials framed it around software engineering — a Stripe migration case study and a top score on Cognition's "FrontierCode" eval — agentic knowledge work (a leading result on Hebbia's Finance Benchmark), computer vision, and, through the more access-restricted sibling Mythos 5, life-sciences research [^14]. Mathematics research appeared nowhere in that positioning [^14]. Three days later, on June 12, 2026, the US government ordered Anthropic to suspend all Fable 5 and Mythos 5 access for any foreign national, anywhere, including the company's own foreign-national employees — and Anthropic's official X account posted about the suspension directly and promptly [^15]. When access was partially restored on July 1, 2026, Anthropic again communicated in its own voice: Fable 5 came back globally, but Mythos 5 was restored only for a vetted set of US organizations the government had approved on June 26, a tiered and conditional rollback tied to future cooperation commitments rather than an unconditional reversal [^16]. In both directions — bad news and good news — the company's public-affairs machinery moved fast and spoke plainly.

None of that speed or voice has shown up for the math claim. As of July 22, 2026 — more than 48 hours after the Jacobian conjecture story went viral — extensive searching turns up no official Anthropic acknowledgment of the result at all [^17]. The same model, the same company, weeks apart, producing two very different communication postures.

:::timeline
- {date: "2026-06-09", headline: "Fable 5 and Mythos 5 launch", body: "Positioned around coding, agentic knowledge work, vision, and life sciences — no mention of mathematics research."}
- {date: "2026-06-12", headline: "Export-control suspension", body: "US directive blocks all foreign-national access; Anthropic posts about it directly and promptly."}
- {date: "2026-07-01", headline: "Tiered restoration", body: "Fable 5 restored globally; Mythos 5 restored only for vetted US organizations."}
- {date: "2026-07-20", headline: "Jacobian conjecture claim goes viral — no corporate statement", body: "More than 48 hours later, Anthropic has issued no official statement on the math result."}
:::

What should a reader make of the gap? Two readings are both plausible, and the facts above only establish the first half of each — that a directive existed, that Anthropic spoke about it, and that no equivalent statement exists for the math claim — not which motive, if any, is driving the silence.

:::callout(kind=info, label="Two readings")
**Caution.** An unreviewed, informally-verified research result is a different risk profile than a government directive. Amplifying a counterexample before independent verification is complete would expose Anthropic to exactly the kind of reversal seen elsewhere in AI-assisted mathematics, where other labs' claims have publicly collapsed under scrutiny (Section 5). Staying quiet until the community finishes checking is the conservative, arguably responsible choice.

**Comms lag, not strategy.** The Jacobian result reads as a personal achievement — a mathematician's own account of using the model, posted informally — not a planned product moment that corporate communications had queued up. A suspension order forces an immediate, mandatory response; an employee's math breakthrough does not carry the same institutional trigger, so the absence of a statement may simply mean nobody has decided whether or how to respond yet, not that anyone has decided to stay silent on purpose.
:::

A third, more speculative reading worth naming — because some outside commentary has floated it — is that the quiet, informal, employee-driven version of this story is arguably better publicity for Anthropic than a deliberate campaign would have been, and that its timing, arriving while the company's public narrative had been dominated by the export-control fight rather than by a research or product win, works in Anthropic's favor regardless of intent. That is an analyst's inference about how the story lands, not a strategy Anthropic has confirmed, and it should be weighted accordingly.

Why this matters: the size of the gap between "spoke immediately" and "has said nothing for 48-plus hours" is itself a data point about how Anthropic is choosing — deliberately or not — to let an unverified, employee-driven claim about its own model stand or fall on the mathematics community's judgment rather than the company's.

## 8. The bitcoin non-sequitur

A cluster of crypto-adjacent outlets covered a pure-mathematics result — an explicit counterexample to the Jacobian conjecture — as a bitcoin story, but the actual argument on offer is an unquantified capital-rotation narrative that neither supplies a technical mechanism nor survives contact with the same day's market data.

CoinDesk ran a piece amounting to "Claude's Fable 5 just solved an 87-year-old math problem, and it matters for bitcoin"; stripped of the headline, its case is that continued AI capability progress "strengthens the case" for speculative capital rotating out of crypto and into AI/compute infrastructure — hedged language, not a quantified flow estimate or event-study finding, and it never identifies a transmission mechanism linking *this specific math result* (as opposed to AI progress in general) to bitcoin [^22]. That gap matters because the coincidence doing the rhetorical work is a name, not a shared structure: "Jacobian" also names a real object in elliptic-curve and hyperelliptic-curve cryptography (the Jacobian of a curve), but bitcoin's actual security assumption is elliptic-curve discrete-log hardness under ECDSA over secp256k1, and there is no established technical link between that and the Jacobian-*determinant* invertibility question this conjecture is actually about [^22].

:::callout(kind=warn, label="No technical link")
"Jacobian conjecture" (polynomial-map invertibility over C^n) and "Jacobian of a curve" (the abelian variety used in elliptic-curve cryptography) share a name and a 19th-century namesake, not a mathematical structure. Bitcoin's cryptography does not depend on either.
:::

The rotation story also fails the most basic test available: what bitcoin actually did the next day. On July 21, 2026 — the day after the announcement — bitcoin rallied to roughly $66,000–$66,500, a ~5-week high and roughly a 2% move in 24 hours [^23]. Financial press attributed that rally to five consecutive days of spot-ETF inflows exceeding $700M and optimism around pending crypto-market-structure legislation, and noted it moved together with the Nasdaq-100 — a risk-on co-movement, not the "capital fleeing crypto for AI" direction the rotation narrative would predict [^23]. If a math-driven AI-progress rotation out of crypto were actually operative that week, the observable move ran the opposite way.

Not every outlet even tried to make the argument: several outlets in the same coverage cluster simply republished the pure AI/math story under a crypto-news masthead with no bitcoin-relevance claim at all [^22], which looks less like a considered editorial judgment that this is a crypto story and more like topical and audience-capture placement — a viral AI story gets a crypto-flavored headline because it will travel on a crypto feed.

None of this means crypto-adjacent coverage of AI news is inherently illegitimate — AI capex and chip-demand narratives genuinely do intersect with crypto-adjacent trading psychology often enough that "AI progress broadly affects risk appetite" is a defensible, if weak and diffuse, claim. What doesn't hold up is the much stronger and more specific one the headlines actually sold: that *this* counterexample, about polynomial-map invertibility, "matters for bitcoin." The gap between those two claims is the whole story, and it's a useful illustration of how a viral science headline gets an unrelated hook bolted onto it purely because the hook trades better on a different audience's feed.

## 9. What would break this story

The mathematics has survived forty-eight hours of informal, high-quality scrutiny with no error found. That is meaningfully strong evidence — and it is not the same thing as the case being closed. Several concrete, checkable developments could still overturn or substantially deflate today's confidence, and readers should track them rather than treat the story as settled.

The most direct risk is procedural: no formal peer review has occurred, and the Lean formalization pull request remains open rather than merged [^5]. That process has already caught one real discrepancy — the rescaled determinant-1 version versus Alpöge's original −2 — and corrected it without touching the core result [^5]. It is reasonable to expect more small corrections of this kind as review continues; it would be a different and more serious matter if a future correction turned out to touch the actual collision or determinant computation rather than a matter of rescaling and attribution.

The base rate argues for real, if moderate, caution. The Jacobian conjecture has a documented history of at least five retracted proofs and disproofs before this one, several from credentialed experts whose initial confidence did not survive scrutiny [^6][^12][^13]. The AI-for-mathematics genre specifically has its own recent, high-profile retraction: an October 2025 claim that a model had "solved" ten Erdős problems collapsed within days once independently checked [^20]. Nothing currently distinguishes this claim's risk profile from that reference class except that, so far, the independent checking has gone the other way — confirming rather than debunking. That is genuinely different from the Erdős episode, but the pattern of "initial excitement, later walk-back" is common enough in this exact problem's history that it remains the single most important thing to watch for going forward, not something already ruled out by 48 hours of goodwill.

A second live gap is procedural rather than mathematical: no prompt, transcript, or process description has been published by Alpöge or Anthropic (Section 5). If a fuller writeup later shows Fable 5's actual contribution was more cosmetic than substantive relative to human-directed work, the "AI helped disprove a famous conjecture" framing would need real revision — though that would not make the underlying mathematics wrong, only the popular narrative around it overstated. Relatedly, Aaron Lou's claim that an internal OpenAI model independently found "essentially the same" counterexample is a single, self-reported, unverified data point from a rival lab [^24]; if it turns out to be exaggerated or unsubstantiated, that removes one piece of corroborating context without affecting the core Alpöge/Fable 5 result itself.

Finally, watch the framing, not just the facts. The n=2 case remains completely untouched by any of this (Section 2), and continued headline conflation of "the Jacobian conjecture is false" without that caveat would simply repeat, at a larger scale, the same hype-outrunning-scholarship pattern already visible in the contested "87-year-old" framing (Section 6). And Anthropic's current silence is only unremarkable while the result is unreviewed; if the company still says nothing even after the Lean formalization is merged and any referee process concludes, that would be a more notable signal worth a separate look, not the same quiet caution it reads as today.

None of these risks currently has evidence behind it beyond base rates and open process questions — which is exactly why they belong in a "what would break this" section rather than the body of the article. The honest state of play, as of July 22, 2026: a real mathematician, using an AI model in a role nobody has fully documented, produced an explicit and so far unbroken counterexample to a genuine open problem, for three variables and above, that the wider mathematical community has not yet finished checking.
