A SEK 3 small-cap with two decades of losses re-rated 18× in twelve weeks on a CPO/AI-photonics narrative and a US dual-listing announcement; the disclosure perimeter from management is dramatically narrower than the price implies.

What management has actually said is narrower than that move suggests. Confirmed on the record: the ELS partnership with O-Net and Enablence [^3]; the Jabil 1.6T LRO collaboration [^4]; a LiDAR ramp framed as a $28–53M revenue contribution across 2026–2030 in the Q4 2025 interim report [^1]; and the *evaluation* — not the decision — of a US dual listing [^22]. What management has *not* confirmed is at least as material: there is no named hyperscaler customer for the ELS product line, no announced Form F-1 or 20-F filing date, no bookrunner, and no target listing year. The Q1 2026 report was postponed from 2026-05-20 to 2026-05-29 to allow more time to close [^28]; separately, the 2025 annual report was restated under PCAOB standards as part of the US-listing readiness work [^2]. Both are the kind of housekeeping a dual-listing candidate has to do, but they also widen the gap between the current SEK 55 print and the audited numbers backing it.

A further flag — and this one is on the record. On 2026-04-16, the same day the dual-listing evaluation was announced, Ekobrottsmyndigheten (Sweden's Economic Crime Authority) prosecutor Jonas Myrdal told EFN that confidential details about the planned US listing "must have leaked" and that Nasdaq Stockholm itself, as marketplace operator, should examine the communications [^36]. That is an open inquiry, not a finding — no wrongdoing has been established and no parties have been charged — but it is now a primary-source-attributed fact the chart should be read against, not separately from.

What would weaken this read of the tape: if the 2026-05-29 Q1 report shows accelerating Photonics product revenue and the LiDAR ramp picks up a named customer inside the $28–53M envelope [^1], the fundamentals begin to catch up to the chart and the multiple-compression argument loses force. If it does not — if Q1 is a soft print, the F-1 timeline slips, and ELS remains a partnership announcement without a named hyperscaler order — then the 18× is a sentiment trade priced against a still-narrow disclosure perimeter, and the asymmetry runs the other way. This matters because SIVE is now the most visible European pure-play wager on the CPO/AI-photonics thesis, and the gap between what the stock implies and what the company has formally confirmed is the entire trade.

### Wireless: a US-resident mmWave business with a Swedish HQ

The Wireless segment sells beamforming RFICs and antenna modules in two flavours. The {accent}TRX BF02{/} family is a 28 GHz front-end targeting licensed 5G FWA, SATCOM user terminals, and high-resolution radar — the chip behind the aiRadar imaging-radar reference design [^14] and the Tachyon Networks fixed-wireless deployment [^15]. The {accent}TRX BF01{/} plus the BFM06010 transceiver address unlicensed 57–71 GHz WiGig and V-band backhaul, where Sivers has been the dominant merchant supplier since the chip arrived.

The footprint that makes any of this credible is American. Sivers paid {accent}$22.5M cash plus 39.2M new shares{/} for MixComm in February 2022 [^24], buying the Columbia University spin-out team led by Harish Krishnaswamy, the silicon-germanium and CMOS know-how, and the Chatham NJ design center that now runs the global Wireless business. Without that deal, Sivers would still be a Kista-only mmWave shop selling reference designs into European telco trials; with it, the Wireless segment grew +34% in FY2025 [^1].

### Photonics: a 4-inch InP line running indium phosphide DFB lasers

The Photonics segment is the asset the AI-CPO narrative actually attaches to. Sivers Photonics in Hamilton, Glasgow operates a {accent}4-inch indium phosphide wafer line{/} — the InP100 platform — covering the 1270–1650 nm range and shipping DFB-laser building blocks for telecom, datacom, and co-packaged optics external light sources [^11]. The standard product menu spans the 4λ FR4 CWDM DFB chipset for 400G/800G short reach, a DR8-style 1310 nm 70 mW DFB for high-power single-wavelength links, and the 8λ O-band CW DFB chipset built to the CW-WDM MSA spec that several CPO architectures have adopted [^11].

This is not a clean-room someone slapped together for the hype cycle. The Glasgow site is the former CST Global, acquired in 2017, and at its 2016 peak as an independent it was shipping {accent}~1 million DFB lasers per month{/} into the optical-comms supply chain [^12]. The capacity exists; the question is whether AI buyers want what comes off it.

### The NRE problem hiding inside the growth rate

Sit with the segment data for one more beat. In Q4 2025, Wireless revenue {accent}fell 11% YoY to SEK 50.4M{/} while Photonics {accent}grew 52% YoY to SEK 30.3M{/} [^1]. The internal pivot from telco-mmWave to AI-photonics is no longer just management commentary — it is showing up in the segment lines. Adjusted EBITDA for the full year was negative SEK 10.8M (a 31% improvement on FY2024), and Q4 adj EBITDA turned {accent}positive at +SEK 10.8M{/} [^1], the first positive quarter since the MixComm deal closed.

But 72% NRE is still 72% NRE. Development billings can roll off as fast as they roll on — they are milestone-recognised against project plans, not annuity revenue tied to ship volumes. SEK 85.7M of true product run-rate is a fraction of what the disclosed Wireless customer wins imply, and a fraction of what a credible CPO-ELS franchise would need to clear. The question for the next four quarters is not whether Sivers makes real semiconductors. It does. The question is whether product revenue can catch up to the chart before the next equity raise resets the cap table — the topic of section six.

## 03. Why co-packaged optics needs external light sources

The architectural move that creates the ELS slot is co-packaged optics: putting the photonic engines on the same substrate as the switch ASIC, displacing the pluggable transceiver. That move forces a second, less-discussed change — it puts a continuous-wave laser source {accent}outside{/} the package, as a field-replaceable module, for the first time at AI-rack scale.

:::stats
- {label: Optical transceiver TAM (2026), value: "$26B", note: "+60% YoY (LightCounting)"}
- {label: CPO TAM by 2036, value: "$20B+", note: "37% CAGR (IDTechEx)"}
- {label: ELS share of CPO BOM, value: "~10%", note: "implies ~$2B by 2036"}
- {label: 1.6T pluggable chipset sales (2026), value: ">$2B"}
:::

The physics is what makes the migration non-optional. At 200G-per-lane signalling — the Rubin-generation baseline — copper DAC cables top out at roughly one to three metres of reach before the channel loss budget collapses [^30]. AI back-end fabrics already span more than that inside a single rack, and the leaf-spine hop between racks is far worse. Optics has to take over the short reach, and pluggable optics at the faceplate are running into their own wall: each 1.6T port burns about 30 watts at the switch interface, and a 144-port platform spends a kilowatt on transceiver power alone. Co-packaging the engines drops the per-interface number by roughly 70 percent.

:::compare
- {role: PLUGGABLE, name: "Switch interface", value: "~30W"}
- {role: CPO, name: "Co-packaged interface", value: "~9W"}
- {role: SUBJECT, name: "≈70% energy reduction (NVIDIA)", value: "−21W per port"}
:::

That power number is why hyperscalers are willing to tolerate the manufacturing pain of CPO [^32]. Broadcom claims roughly the same — about 70 percent lower optical interconnect power on Tomahawk 6 Davisson [^9]. Once you commit to bringing the optics inside the package, though, you inherit a thermal problem: the switch ASIC sits at 100 °C or hotter under load, and a directly-modulated or even continuous-wave InP laser parked next to it will drift in wavelength and age out fast. Wavelength stability is what makes WDM and coherent links work; lose it and the link budget evaporates.

The industry's answer is to physically separate the light source. Pull the laser off the package, put it in a pluggable cassette at the front of the chassis, run polarisation-maintaining fibre from the cassette to the photonic engine, and field-replace the cassette on its own MTBF cycle. That cassette is the External Light Source. Sivers Photonics MD Alex McCann put the case bluntly in March:

:::quote(attr="Alex McCann, MD, Sivers Photonics")
ELS is a crucial companion to CPO because it separates temperature-sensitive lasers from the extreme heat of high-power processors, ensuring wavelength stability. [^3]
:::

The architectural detail that turns this from an interesting accessory into a real BOM line is the count. NVIDIA's Spectrum-X Photonics Ethernet platform — due 2H 2026 — uses External Light Sources at the chassis fan-out, with module counts in the 16-to-64 range depending on the switch variant and each housing multiple CW lasers feeding the photonic engines on substrate [^7]. ==Apply the same architectural pattern (with variant-specific module counts) to Quantum-X InfiniBand and to Broadcom's TH6 Davisson== and the slot the bull case targets is not "one laser per transceiver" but tens of field-replaceable laser cassettes per switch. The roadmap is publicly staked out.

:::timeline
- {date: 2025-03, headline: "NVIDIA unveils Quantum-X / Spectrum-X Photonics", body: "Lumentum named for CW lasers; Coherent named for SiPh integration; Sivers not named."}
- {date: 2025-10, headline: "Broadcom announces Tomahawk 6 Davisson (102.4T CPO)", body: "First 200G/lane CPO ASIC with field-replaceable lasers; ~70% lower optical power vs pluggables."}
- {date: 2026-Q1, headline: "NVIDIA Quantum-X InfiniBand CPO ships", body: "115 Tb/s, 144×800G ports, 3 COUPE engines and 16-64 ELS modules per switch (variant-dependent)."}
- {date: 2026-03-17, headline: "Sivers + O-Net + Enablence announce ELS module", body: "Sivers CW laser arrays + Enablence NxN Star Coupler + O-Net packaging."}
- {date: 2026-2H, headline: "NVIDIA Spectrum-X Photonics (Ethernet) ships", body: "Hyperscaler procurement window for 2027 buildouts opens."}
:::

Sizing the slot honestly requires acknowledging that the analyst range is wide. LightCounting's April 2026 newsletter projects strong growth across optical transceivers and 1.6T pluggable chipsets as the front-end AI fabric continues to absorb spend [^19]. IDTechEx, looking specifically at CPO, projects the segment past $20 billion by 2036 at a 37 percent CAGR [^10]. ==Apply an industry-typical low-double-digit BOM share for the ELS subsystem== and the addressable line for outside laser vendors is roughly $1–2 billion by the mid-2030s, with low-hundreds of millions in play near-term as Quantum-X / Spectrum-X and Tomahawk 6 deployments ramp. The TAM range across analyst houses is wide enough that anyone modelling this should carry the spread rather than picking the friendliest line.

The slot, then, is real, structural, and architecturally locked-in for the next decade of AI fabric scaling. That is the entire bull case for an outside laser vendor. The hard question — which Section 5 takes up — is whether {accent}addressable{/} survives contact with {accent}addressed{/}. NVIDIA's own developer post names Lumentum as the CW laser supplier for Quantum-X [^7], and Coherent is openly signalling InP capacity expansion at its Sherman, TX 6-inch fab to lock in hyperscaler ELS contracts [^17,18]. The thesis for Sivers is that the EML and CW supply gap at 2026–2028 volumes is wide enough to need a credible third source; the bear thesis is that "third source" is a slot Coherent intends to fill itself. The question for Sivers is not whether CPO becomes a real market — it will — but whether the ELS slot stays open long enough for a 130-person fab-light outsider with SEK 43M of cash on the balance sheet to step into it.

The two pieces doing the most narrative work are the LiDAR ramp and the Jabil LRO collaboration. On LiDAR, {accent}Sivers has not named the customer{/}; sell-side desks have widely attributed it to Aeva, whose own NVIDIA DRIVE Hyperion integration was announced at CES 2026 [^29]. The attribution is *plausible* — the timing, the SOA content, and the automotive-grade FMCW profile all fit — but it is not confirmed by either company, and any model that hard-codes Aeva as the anchor is doing inference, not accounting. Worth flagging: Aeva announced its own vertically-integrated high-power semiconductor optical amplifier (>28 dBm at >20% wall-plug efficiency) for AI data center and FMCW LiDAR applications on 2026-01-15 [^37], which over a multi-year horizon is a real integration risk to the SOA half of Sivers' content if Aeva moves it in-house. The contracted Q4 2026 ramp [^1,5] is what gets the program from "design win" to "product line".

The Jabil 1.6T LRO collaboration, announced 15 April 2026 [^4], puts Sivers DFB lasers into Jabil's energy-efficient 1.6T pluggable transceiver. The TAM math the bulls reach for is real — LightCounting projects 800G-and-above pluggables will be roughly 80% of the pluggable market by 2030 [^4] — but the honest framing is that {accent}LRO is a stepping-stone to CPO, not CPO itself{/}. The packaging is still pluggable; the external-laser architectural shift is what gets you to the much larger CPO opportunity, and Sivers' position there runs through the separate O-Net / Enablence ELS work [^3].

On the wireless side, the Intelsat digitizer program [^13] and the aiRadar 28 GHz antenna module [^14] are real programs with multi-year revenue tails, but their mass-production POs are still ahead of them. The ESA IRIS² BFIC contract [^16] is the clearest example of the NRE-to-product gap on this list: it is *technically* a contract, but it is a {mark}$0.9M, 18-month development engagement{/}, not a unit-shipping order.

The pipeline math is where calibration is hardest. The {accent}$453M opportunity pipeline{/} is a company-defined, non-audited KPI [^1]: Sivers does not disclose the probability weighting, the average historical pipeline-to-PO conversion rate, or the time-to-revenue distribution. That means the headline number cannot be triangulated against any forward product-revenue forecast — it is a *direction-of-travel* metric, not a bookable one. The contracted LiDAR ramp is the sanity check: $28-53M cumulative across 2026-2030 [^5] is roughly $5.6-10.6M of average annual contribution, which is, on its own, about the size of a single one of the 2025 wireless design-win buckets. Even a real, ramping anchor customer is not, by itself, a re-rating event at current valuation.

Design wins are necessary but not sufficient. The next twelve months — Q4 2026 LiDAR start of production, the Tachyon ramp turning into follow-on POs, Intelsat moving from program award to volume shipment, the first Jabil LRO production order landing — are what tell us which of these programs convert and which stay parked as slides in next quarter's deck.

## 05. Lumentum, Coherent and the credible competitive squeeze

The ELS slot is real, Sivers has a credible architectural answer via O-Net and Enablence, and the slot is largely pre-spoken-for. That is the honest one-line read of Section 4 against the supply landscape.

:::compare
- {role: INCUMBENT, name: "Lumentum (LITE)", value: "$808M FQ3'26 rev"}
- {role: INCUMBENT, name: "Coherent (COHR)", value: "$1.6B+ FY25 Cloud/Networking"}
- {role: SUBJECT, name: "Sivers (SIVE)", value: "SEK 306.6M (~$29M) FY25"}
:::

Sivers is two orders of magnitude smaller than the incumbents it would need to displace, or — more plausibly — slot in alongside. Lumentum reported Q3 FY26 revenue of $808.4 million, +90% year-over-year, on the back of accelerating datacom-laser demand for AI infrastructure [^17,41]. Coherent demonstrated a 400 mW 1311 nm BH-DFB CW laser specifically targeted at CPO co-packaged photonics at OFC 2026, with volume production targeted Q3 2026 [^18]. Sivers' entire FY25 group revenue was SEK 306.6M [^2] — roughly the size of a single quarter's *growth* at Lumentum.

The incumbent stack is already inside NVIDIA's tent. Lumentum is publicly named in NVIDIA's Spectrum-X / Quantum-X Photonics ecosystem disclosures [^6,7]. On 2026-03-02 NVIDIA announced a $2.0 billion equity investment plus a multi-year purchase commitment in Lumentum for advanced optics technology [^39]; the same day NVIDIA announced a parallel $2.0 billion equity investment plus a multi-year purchase commitment in Coherent for advanced laser and optical-networking products [^40]. Sivers is conspicuously absent from that capital stack.

:::callout(kind=warn, label=Concentration)
NVIDIA put $2.0B + $2.0B of fresh equity into Coherent and Lumentum on 2026-03-02 paired with multi-year purchase commitments [^39,40]; Sivers is not in that capital stack.
:::

Coherent's competitive answer to a third source is published technology, not capacity-disclosure: at OFC 2026 the firm demonstrated a 1311 nm BH-DFB CW laser at 400 mW (55 °C, linewidth <200 kHz, RIN <–145 dB/Hz), with volume production targeted Q3 2026 [^18]. Lumentum's published UHP CW laser for CPO already runs 350 mW at 50 °C / 235 mW at 70 °C at 1311 nm with linewidth <500 kHz [^41]. ==Sivers Photonics' published DR8 1310 nm DFB on the product page is rated at 70 mW== [^11]; the headline-power gap to Coherent's sampling spec is ~5.7× and to Lumentum's frontier 3.4–5.0× depending on operating temperature. The bull's "Sivers is the credible third source" claim is structurally true on geography and political-neutrality grounds; it is structurally weaker on the published spec sheet.

Japan adds a third pillar through Mitsubishi and Sumitomo Electric as significant InP merchant suppliers — Sumitomo is named in TSMC's COUPE laser-materials disclosures [^7]. The structural fact the bull case for Sivers rests on is that incumbent capacity expansion through 2026–2027 is publicly disclosed by both Coherent and Lumentum to lag near-term hyperscaler demand for CPO-grade CW lasers; whether that gap is open wide enough for a 130-person fab-light outsider to slot in before the incumbents close it is the entire debate.

To be explicit: that grid is an editorial reading of the partnerships named in [^6,7,9,17,18], not audited revenue shares — no public numbers parse the incumbents' CPO exposure cleanly by hyperscaler. The directional point survives the precision caveat: Sivers' disclosed hyperscaler exposure is, today, zero.

Geopolitics cuts both ways. The O-Net relationship gives Sivers an Asia-Pacific ODM partner and process-engineering muscle Sivers cannot match alone [^3], but O-Net is China-headquartered and a tightening of US export controls on advanced photonics could turn that partnership from an asset into a liability for US hyperscaler procurement. The counter is that Sivers' Glasgow wafer fab and Chatham, NJ design center make for a cleaner "Western supply" story than fully-Chinese alternatives — potentially material under CHIPS-era preferences. Honest framing: it cuts both ways, and which edge cuts harder depends on a 2026-2027 political variable nobody controls.

The timing argument is the entire bull case. LightCounting forecasts CPO volume inflection in 2027-2028 [^19]; the supply gap matters for roughly 18-24 months until incumbents finish their capacity ramps. Bull: Sivers slots in during this window before Coherent and Lumentum catch up. Bear: Sherman's 6-inch InP line and Lumentum's 2H 2026 CW ramp close the window before Glasgow scales. The most generous reading of the bull case actually *quotes the incumbents back to themselves* — both Lumentum and Coherent are publicly signaling structural InP shortages, and that admission is itself the strongest non-Sivers argument for a third source.

The repricing in Section 1 implicitly assumes Sivers wins a hyperscaler ELS socket. As of writing, none is publicly disclosed, and the incumbent capacity arriving in 2026-2027 is structured exactly to make sure none has to be.

The result is a register that has roughly tripled since 2020, a balance sheet that just absorbed a SEK 127M restatement haircut, and a cash position that — without the February facility and April placement — would have run out inside FY2026.

## 07. The dual listing and the reflexivity trap

The dual-listing thesis is reflexive: the announcement is doing the work the listing is supposed to do, and the share price is now compounding the gap between what management has said and what the market has priced.

The loop, in plain English. US-listed AI-photonics incumbents trade at multiples Stockholm has no comparable for — Coherent at roughly nine times sales, Lumentum at roughly seven (Section 5). A credible Nasdaq cross-listing for a Swedish CPO-adjacent name therefore implies a step-change rerating. The {accent}announcement{/} of evaluating such a listing pulls a slice of that rerating forward. The higher share price improves the terms of any capital raise. A better-funded operating story justifies more of the multiple. The loop only breaks two ways: real product revenue lands (Section 4), or hard listing-readiness milestones convert the evaluation into a commitment.

:::callout(kind=warn, label="Disclosure gap")
As of 2026-05-17, there is no SEC EDGAR filing, no Form F-1 draft, no announced bookrunner, no named US listing advisor, and no target listing year. The dual-listing announcement is an *evaluation*, not a *commitment*. [^22,28]
:::

What management has actually said. The Apr 16 release frames the rationale as {mark}"enhance access to the significant US tech-centric capital markets, support long-term growth, and broaden its international investor base."{/} That sentence is the entirety of the stated case [^22]. There is no language about photonics multiples, named hyperscaler customer visibility, or US capital-pool depth. The market has supplied that subtext on its own.

The calendar. Each slip is small in isolation; cumulatively they describe a finance organisation working through real PCAOB-uplift workload while the equity reprices 18× around it.

The asymmetry. A Swedish issuer cross-listing into Nasdaq under Foreign Private Issuer status typically runs 6–12 months of SEC registration work after the first F-1 draft hits EDGAR. ## 08. What could break the thesis

The bull case is the chain of arguments built in sections three through five: CPO is happening at hyperscaler scale, ELS is a structurally required and growing BOM line, the incumbents' own filings concede an InP supply gap, and Sivers — with Glasgow capacity, an MSA-aligned product menu, and an O-Net/Enablence go-to-market — is the credible third source. Here are the disconfirming questions and the evidence that would actually move the priors.

:::callout(kind=danger, label="Falsifiers")
Each item below is something you could observe in 2026–2027 that would substantively weaken the thesis. If two or more land together, the trade unwinds.
:::

**1. Coherent or Lumentum announces a multi-year, named-hyperscaler ELS supply contract that closes the supply gap.** The supply-gap argument depends on incumbent capacity *not* meeting demand through 2027. Coherent has guided InP capacity doubling by end-2026 on a 6-inch line at roughly half the unit cost of 3-inch [^18]; Lumentum has guided the 200G EML mix rising toward 25% by end-2026 [^17]. If those ramps land on schedule and yield curves cooperate, the slot Sivers is aiming at narrows by 2027. The single most damaging news for SIVE would be a Coherent or Lumentum 8-K naming a multi-year ELS supply contract for an NVIDIA or Broadcom rack platform.

**2. The strategic LiDAR customer slips, scales below the low end, or in-sources the SOA.** The $28–53M cumulative 2026–2030 range disclosed by Sivers [^5] is the most concrete near-term product-revenue anchor in the file. Aeva (the widely-attributed but unconfirmed customer) has already announced its own high-power SOA in January 2026, which over the lifecycle range [^5] could halve the per-unit content Sivers ships. A Q4 2026 ramp that slips into 2027 — or a Q4 2026 ramp at the low end of the range that also loses the SOA content — would knock out the most visible 2026-2028 product-revenue catalyst.

**3. The Q1 2026 print on 2026-05-29 shows decelerating Photonics product revenue.** Q4 2025 Photonics grew 52% YoY on segment product sales [^1]. The market is extrapolating that into a multi-year inflection. A Q1 2026 segment print that decelerates back toward FY2025's full-year +8% [^1] reframes the Q4 acceleration as lumpy NRE timing rather than a CPO ramp. This is a 12-day-out catalyst at the time of writing.

**4. The F-1 timeline slips into 2027 without an EDGAR draft.** Section 7's reflexive loop holds only as long as the listing pathway looks credible. No F-1 draft is on file as of 2026-05-17 [^28,34]; the AGM vote is 2026-06-15 [^28]. If the F-1 has not been filed by year-end and management has not named a bookrunner or target year, the dual-listing premium compresses regardless of the operating story.

**5. The Swedish Economic Crime Authority probe escalates into a formal enforcement action.** As of the bird/X coverage cited above, the probe is at the inquiry stage and no parties have been charged. A formal enforcement action — particularly one that touches the April directed share issue or any insider transactions around the Mar 17 / Apr 15 / Apr 16 catalyst sequence — would compound the disclosure-perimeter problem and likely accelerate the rerating in reverse.

**6. Another SEK 100M+ directed issue inside the next six months.** The ts2.tech read-through suggests the board is seeking authority for up to ~15% further dilution [^34]. Exercised near current levels, near-term EPS dilution is meaningful but absorbable. Exercised on weakness — i.e., after a soft Q1 print or a missed listing milestone — it tells the market the SEK 125M April raise was not the runway-fixing event the bulls modelled, and resets the dilution clock again.

**Where I would change my mind in the bull's direction.** A 2026-05-29 print with Photonics product revenue at +30% YoY or better, the LiDAR ramp confirming a named customer inside the high end of the $28–53M envelope [^5], and an F-1 draft on EDGAR before AGM would together close most of the disclosure-perimeter gap. None of those three is implausible. None is the base case from the public record today.

The honest closing read is this: Sivers is in the room where the AI-photonics conversation is happening, but it is not in the room where the hyperscaler ELS contracts are being signed. Whether it moves from one room to the other inside the 18–24-month window before incumbent capacity closes is the entire trade — and the 2026-05-29 Q1 print is the next data point that meaningfully changes the priors.
