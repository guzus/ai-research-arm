Cerebras goes public on a dinner-plate chip and a UAE customer book

A $5.5B IPO, a 108% first-day pop, and a $20B OpenAI deal that pays the company partly in its own stock. The bull case is real. So are three load-bearing dependencies the prospectus puts in writing.

On 14 May 2026, Cerebras Systems priced its IPO at $185 a share — above its already-raised $150–$160 range — sold 30 million shares for $5.55B in proceeds, opened the next morning at $385, and closed its first session at $311.07.1 The fully-diluted market cap at pricing was $56.4B; intraday it cleared $95B before settling near $66B.2 It is the largest U.S. tech IPO since Snowflake, and the second time Cerebras has tried this — the first attempt, filed in September 2024, collapsed when CFIUS opened a national-security review of the company's largest shareholder.3

The market clearly believes something. The interesting question is what, exactly, the buyer of a CBRS share is underwriting. Strip the press release away and three claims sit underneath the price: that wafer-scale silicon is now the right architecture for inference-era AI, that a $20B contractual relationship with OpenAI is real revenue and not a circular financing arrangement, and that 86% of 2025 revenue concentrated in two Emirati institutions is a one-time scaling artifact rather than the business itself. Each is defensible. None is settled.

The swing from a half-billion loss to $238M of GAAP profit on $510M of revenue is the headline number. A 47% net margin on hardware is unusual for a company on its first profitable year, and it is the strongest single argument the bull case has. The footnote, which the prospectus does disclose, is that the profit was generated against a customer base where two related Emirati entities provided $0.86 of every revenue dollar.4 CEO Andrew Feldman's stake is worth roughly $1.9B at the IPO price; CTO Sean Lie's, ~$1B.2

02The chip: real engineering, not a slogan

Cerebras's argument is structural, not stylistic. A GPU cluster builds large models by stitching together small dies with high-bandwidth interconnect; every cross-die hop costs energy and latency. Cerebras etches the entire compute fabric onto one continuous silicon wafer, then sells the wafer as a single addressable accelerator. The WSE-3, the current generation, measures 46,225 mm², holds 4 trillion transistors across 900,000 cores, carries 44 GB of on-die SRAM, and reaches 21 PB/s of memory bandwidth at TSMC's 5 nm node.5 Peak performance is 125 petaflops of 16-bit AI compute.

The architecture matters most where it matters most — autoregressive inference, where each generated token requires re-reading the model's weights. GPU clusters bottleneck on memory bandwidth at the 70B+ parameter scale; the WSE-3 keeps weights on-die. Artificial Analysis, the independent benchmark consortium, has measured Cerebras sustaining 969 tokens/second on Llama 3.1 405B and 2,600+ tokens/second on Llama 4 Scout, against GPU-class numbers in the 10–15 t/s range for the same 405B workload.6 Those are not marketing numbers from Cerebras; they are third-party measurements that competitors have not credibly disputed.

The counter-take a sober reader has to take seriously: throughput is one axis of inference economics, not the whole graph. Cost-per-token, batch utilization, and time-to-first-token on cold prompts are equally load-bearing — and on those axes the gap narrows materially. Cerebras's economic edge depends on workloads where end-to-end latency genuinely matters (agentic loops, multi-turn reasoning chains, code generation) rather than on bulk batch inference, where GPU price/perf is still hard to beat. AWS's March 2026 disaggregated-inference deal, which puts Trainium on prefill and CS-3 on decode inside Bedrock, is an explicit acknowledgement of this split: each side of the inference loop has different hardware needs, and the WSE-3 wins on decode specifically.7

03The OpenAI deal, read as a financial instrument

The OpenAI relationship is the single most-cited line item in coverage of the IPO, and it is also the most carefully structured. The Master Relationship Agreement obligates OpenAI to purchase 750 MW of inference capacity, with a contractual option to scale to 2 GW by 2030.8 The headline value cited in coverage ranges from "$10B+" to "$20B+" depending on whether you count the base contract or the expanded option. The structure underneath the headline is more revealing than the number.

Read straight, that is a customer agreement. Read against the broader 2026 financing pattern across the AI stack — Nvidia investing in OpenAI, OpenAI buying from Oracle, Oracle leasing Nvidia GPUs, Microsoft underwriting OpenAI compute, OpenAI now both lending to and taking equity in Cerebras — it is one node in what Bloomberg has called the AI "circular deal" graph.9 The economic substance is that Cerebras has, in effect, agreed to deliver roughly half its prospective gross profit on this contract back to OpenAI as equity. That is fine if OpenAI executes the capacity drawdown as planned. It is structurally fragile if OpenAI's own revenue trajectory slips and it elects to slow-walk the option.

The honest read

A $20B contractual headline that pays the buyer back in 10% of the seller's equity and a billion-dollar working-capital loan is not the same instrument as a $20B purchase order from a diversified institutional customer. The terms protect both sides — but they also mean the deal's value to Cerebras shareholders is contingent on milestones neither party fully controls.

04The UAE problem the S-1 cannot dissolve

The original 2024 S-1 collapsed because 87% of 2024 revenue came from G42, the Abu Dhabi-headquartered AI conglomerate, and CFIUS opened a review of G42's minority stake on concerns about historical ties to Huawei and downstream technology transfer to China.3 The 2026 refile clears that review — G42's stake was restructured into non-voting shares — but it does not solve the underlying concentration. The current revenue split:

MBZUAI (Mohamed bin Zayed University of AI) 62%
G42 24%
All other customers (AWS, OpenAI base, US labs, federal) 14%

The prospectus itself notes that MBZUAI and G42 are "related parties with respect to each other," which is the regulatory way of conceding that the headline diversification — G42 falling from 85% to 24% YoY — is a reallocation between affiliated Emirati institutions, not the arrival of independent demand.4 The risk-factor section lists "geopolitical tensions in the Middle East and potential escalation of U.S. export controls" as a material risk — the standard language for "if the policy environment around chip exports to the Gulf hardens, a majority of our 2025 revenue is exposed."

Concentration risk, restated honestly

The 2024 IPO was pulled because one customer was 87% of revenue. The 2026 IPO cleared at 86% of revenue from two customers in the same country with disclosed related-party links. This is not a diversification story; it is the same exposure with a new chart.

05The competitive frame: inference is no longer a niche

Cerebras went public into a market that has decided, in the last twelve months, that fast inference is the bottleneck. The evidence:

  • Nvidia acquired Groq for ~$20B in December 2025 and announced custom Groq-derived Language Processing Units at GTC in March 2026 — a tacit admission that even Nvidia's own GPU roadmap was leaving inference latency on the table.10
  • SambaNova unveiled the SN50 chip in February 2026 (claiming 5× max speed vs. competitors, 3× lower TCO for agentic workloads) and closed a $350M round led by Intel.10
  • Cerebras reports being "sold out into 2027" on inference capacity, with OpenAI as anchor and AWS as distribution.1

Three things follow from this competitive picture. First, the dedicated-inference thesis is no longer a contrarian bet — it is a consensus three of the largest infrastructure buyers have already paid for. Second, Cerebras is now the only publicly tradable pure-play in the category; Groq is inside Nvidia, SambaNova is still private. Scarcity is a non-trivial part of the day-one repricing. Third, the moat is narrower than the share price implies: if Nvidia's Groq-branded LPUs hit the cadence Jensen has promised, the wafer-scale architecture's latency advantage compresses to a band where price/perf and ecosystem integration become the deciding factors. That is a fight Nvidia historically wins.

The inference economy is large enough that letting any specialist build escape velocity in it was strategically unacceptable.

06What the valuation requires you to believe

A $56–66B valuation on $510M of 2025 revenue is roughly 110–130× trailing sales. To make that pencil over a five-year horizon, the buyer has to underwrite a combination of the following:

  1. The OpenAI 750 MW deployment ramps on schedule through 2026–2028, and OpenAI exercises the 2 GW expansion option by 2030 — even though doing so deepens Cerebras's circular-financing exposure to a single counterparty.
  2. The UAE revenue base remains intact, despite escalating U.S. export-control scrutiny and the prospectus's own admission that MBZUAI and G42 are related parties. The most plausible bull resolution here is the AWS/Bedrock distribution channel scaling fast enough that 2026 revenue is no longer UAE-dominated by year end.
  3. WSE-4 ships on cadence in 2026–2027, materially raises SRAM capacity (likely via 3D stacking), and either holds or extends the throughput-per-dollar gap versus Nvidia's post-Groq inference stack.5
  4. The 47% net margin survives competition. The current margin reflects scarce supply, premium pricing on inference, and a customer book that has not yet had to negotiate against alternative vendors. None of those three conditions persists if Nvidia, AWS Trainium, and SambaNova all reach the same throughput tier in 2027.

If three of those four hit, the price is justifiable. If only two hit, the equity reprices materially — and the standard 180-day lockup will deliver employee and pre-IPO investor float into whatever that new view is.

07What to watch in the next twelve months

  1. 2026 Q2
    OpenAI capacity milestone #1.

    The first contracted MW delivered under the MRA. A clean delivery starts the warrant vesting clock and validates the production ramp; a slip starts the loan-clawback conversation.

  2. 2026 Q3
    AWS Bedrock general availability.

    Cerebras-backed inference at hyperscaler-distribution scale is the cleanest answer to the customer-concentration risk. If GA slips into 2027, the UAE concentration narrative dominates the next earnings cycle.

  3. 2026 Q4
    First public earnings as a listed company.

    The metrics to track: (a) revenue % from non-UAE customers, (b) gross margin trajectory as supply unlocks, (c) any disclosure on WSE-4 timing.

  4. ~November 2026
    180-day lockup expiration.

    Insider supply hits the market on whatever the prevailing thesis is by then. A weak narrative + concentrated employee selling is the textbook recipe for an overhang.

  5. 2027 H1
    WSE-4 launch and Nvidia GPU/LPU response.

    The single biggest determinant of the five-year thesis. If WSE-4 holds the latency lead and competitive inference pricing has compressed, Cerebras has a durable franchise. If not, it is a niche provider with a great quarter.

08The honest bottom line

Cerebras built the technically harder thing, and the technically harder thing turns out to matter exactly when the industry needs it. A wafer-scale accelerator was a contrarian bet in 2015 and looks like a structurally correct one in 2026, at the moment that inference — not training — has become the load-bearing economic activity in AI. The first-day price action is, on the merits, defensible.

What the price action is not a verdict on is whether the three dependencies in the prospectus — circular financing with OpenAI, 86% UAE concentration, and a margin profile built on scarce supply — resolve favorably over a holding period that lasts longer than the lockup. The bull case is "two of three break right and the company grows into the multiple." The bear case is that any one of them slips and a 130× sales multiple has nowhere to go but down. Both are honest readings of the same S-1.

Bottom line

Cerebras is the most credible non-Nvidia inference story currently public, and the deal cleared on real engineering rather than narrative alone. It is also a company whose top-line, profit, and warrant economics all hinge on a small number of counterparties whose own trajectories are not under Cerebras's control. The technology deserves the rerating; the share price has front-loaded it.
