CXMT prices the biggest IPO of 2026 this week — on the best quarter the DRAM cycle ever paid for

Six months after its prospectus turned China's biggest DRAM project into a numbered company, CXMT returns to the Shanghai market with numbers no one would have dared file in December — and a pricing announcement due July 15 that will produce the first market-cleared valuation of Chinese memory.

ChangXin Memory Technologies — listing as 长鑫科技, ticker 688825 — opens subscription for its RMB 29.5 billion STAR Market IPO on July 16, 2026, the largest A-share listing of the year and the second-largest in the exchange's history261. The company pricing this week is not the company that filed on December 30, 2025: Q1 2026 revenue hit RMB 50.8 billion, up 719.13% year over year, with attributable net profit up 1,688.3%9; Omdia now ranks it the world's #4 DRAM maker at ~7.6% of global revenue76; and the DDR4 chip it sells for $38.50 on the spot market traded at $1.75 fourteen months ago3632. This report examines what the pricing window actually values — a structural champion of Chinese memory self-sufficiency, or the best quarter of the biggest shortage in DRAM history, annualized.

01The deal — six months from filing to the largest float of 2026

CXMT's RMB 29.5 billion STAR Market listing is engineered to be a controlled event, not a market test: the calendar was compressed by regulatory pilot, half the deal is pre-placed with strategic holders, a greenshoe cushions the aftermarket, and the founder is locked up for a decade. The raise — 6.688 billion new shares, 10.00% of post-issue capital — is the largest A-share IPO of 2026 and the second-largest in STAR Market history, behind only SMIC's RMB 53.2 billion float in 2020 2461.

The speed is the tell. The Shanghai Stock Exchange accepted the prospectus on 2025-12-30 — the first filing processed under the STAR Market's pre-review pilot — and the listing committee approved it unanimously on 2026-05-27, with registration submitted the same day 5. The CSRC granted registration permit 证监许可〔2026〕1344号 on 2026-06-12 6. Filing to approval took under six months against a STAR Market average of roughly 358 days 5. The structure removes discretion from the order book at every layer. Fully half the offering is reserved for strategic investors: the sponsors' own investment arms (中金财富 and 中信建投投资) take 2.00% each under 24-month locks, 377 core employees subscribe up to RMB 1.593 billion under a 36-month lock, and as-yet-unnamed "large cooperating enterprises, large insurers, and national-level investment funds" absorb up to RMB 10.844 billion — the names surface only in the July 15 pricing announcement 88. On the inquiry side, the standard 3% highest-bid cull trims exuberance from price discovery 13. Founder-chairman Zhu Yiming has committed to zero share transfers for ten years after listing — and at most 20% of his remaining locked shares annually thereafter — while pledging 768 million shares, half his interest in the Hefei Jixin No.41 partnership, to employee incentives beginning 36 months post-listing 96.

Why it matters: this deal is the template for how Beijing intends to capitalize its strategic-technology champions — fast-tracked, state-anchored, and insulated from the very price discovery an IPO nominally exists to perform.

02The windfall — from RMB 40 billion of losses to a RMB 33 billion quarter

The numbers CXMT will price on this week are unrecognizable from the ones it filed on: between the December 2025 prospectus and the July 2026 subscription, a cyclical price shock converted a barely-profitable company into one guiding to more attributable profit in a single half-year than its cumulative revenue across 2022 and 2023 combined.

Start with what the December filing actually showed. CXMT booked attributable net losses of ¥8.328 billion in 2022, ¥16.340 billion in 2023, and ¥7.145 billion in 2024 — roughly ¥32 billion over three years, with an accumulated deficit of ¥36.65 billion still on the balance sheet at end-2025 58. Revenue over the same stretch was ¥8.287 billion, ¥9.087 billion, and ¥24.178 billion 57. The main-business gross margin was negative 2.19% as recently as 2023 7. This was a company selling DRAM below cost while depreciating one of the largest capital programs in Chinese semiconductor history.

Then the cycle turned (Section 03 covers why), and every line of the P&L repriced at once. FY2025 revenue reached ¥61.799 billion, up 155.6%, with main-business gross margin swinging from 5.00% to 41.02% in a single year 7. Attributable net income crossed zero for the first time — ¥1.875 billion for FY2025 8. Ex-non-recurring attributable profit was ¥5.316 billion, notably above the headline figure, an unusual inversion indicating net-negative non-recurring items rather than one-off gains propping up the result 58.

Q1 2026 is where the filing-era numbers stop being a useful guide. Revenue of ¥50.8 billion — 82% of all of FY2025 in thirteen weeks — with attributable net income of ¥24.76 billion, up 1,688.3% year over year 9. The H1 2026 forecast extends the run: ¥110–120 billion of revenue, ¥66–75 billion of consolidated net income, and ¥50–57 billion attributable, implying attributable growth of +2,244% to +2,544% 986.

Two accounting details deserve more attention than the headline swing. First, depreciation: CXMT charged ¥5.24 billion, ¥10.56 billion, and ¥14.88 billion in 2022–24, then ¥24.68 billion in FY2025 — thirteen times the year's attributable profit, and nearly the whole of gross profit 6295. Fixed assets grew from ¥68.0 billion in 2022 to ¥171.6 billion by H1 2025, 59.19% of total assets 62. Second, R&D capitalization: the ratio fell from 40.73% in 2022 to 0% by H1 2025 after two rounds of SSE audit inquiry — a conservative shift into the IPO, but also a reminder that earlier "losses" were softened by capitalizing R&D that now runs through the income statement 62.

Consolidation catch

Most of the consolidated profit isn't the parent's. CXMT holds only 30.68% of the Xinqiao fab entity (长鑫新桥) and 31.72% of the Beijing fab entity (长鑫集电), consolidating them via acting-in-concert agreements controlling roughly 73–75% of votes — while Big Fund II directly holds 26.99% and 24.67% of those entities 89. In FY2025, consolidated net income was ¥7.14 billion but only ¥1.875 billion was attributable: ~74% leaked to minority holders 898. By Q1 2026 the leak had narrowed to ~25% (¥33.0 billion consolidated vs. ¥24.76 billion attributable) as the parent-level Hefei fab drove the upcycle P&L 89 — but any "net income" figure quoted for CXMT needs a consolidated-or-attributable label before it means anything.

The counterpoint is about assurance, not arithmetic: the FY2025 figures are audited, the Q1 2026 figures are only reviewed (审阅), and the H1 2026 guidance is an unaudited management forecast — three different assurance levels that coverage routinely blends into one growth story, and the prospectus itself warns the H1 2026 surge carries the risk of being unsustainable 995. Why this matters: investors subscribing on 2026-07-16 are underwriting a valuation built on the least-verified tier of these numbers, booked at what the company's own filing concedes may be the peak of the cycle 95.

03The cycle that built the windfall — the DDR4 price event and the HBM crowd-out

The 20x DDR4 price move that turned CXMT profitable was manufactured by the incumbents themselves: Samsung, SK Hynix, and Micron announced the end of DDR4 while AI demand pulled their wafers into HBM, and the resulting squeeze repriced exactly the commodity products CXMT ships in volume.

The raw price event is the single most important fact in this prospectus. A DDR4 8Gb (1Gx8 3200) chip that traded around $1.75 on the TrendForce spot market in mid-May 2025 averaged $38.50 in the July 13, 2026 session — a roughly 22x move in fourteen months, and still rising (+1.85% day-over-day as of 2026-07-13) 3236.

NoteSparse checkpoints, not a continuous series: mid-May 2025, end-May 2025, Dec 30 2025, and the Jul 13 2026 session average. Intermediate prints (e.g. $17.06 on Dec 10 2025) sit between these anchors 3236.

The proximate trigger was end-of-life paperwork, not demand. Samsung stopped taking DDR4 orders in June 2025 and Micron issued its EOL notice the same month; spot jumped +56% in the following two weeks, and server DDR4 contract prices rose 18–23% QoQ in 2Q25 before the product had physically left the market 33. What the sellers framed as an orderly sunset became, in TrendForce's phrase, an "unexpected cash cow" — by September 2025 Samsung and SK Hynix had delayed their phase-outs into 2026, with SK Hynix boosting DDR4 output at Wuxi while Micron alone held to its exit schedule 33.

The incumbents misread their own squeeze. On its July 24, 2025 earnings call, SK Hynix characterized the DDR4 rally as "a short-term demand surge driven by supply concerns" (as relayed by TrendForce) 33 — the rally then ran another twelve months. By 1Q26 conventional DRAM contract prices were up a record 90–95% QoQ; a 64GB DDR5 RDIMM that cost roughly $450 in 4Q25 crossed $900 in 1Q26. The pace is now decelerating — +58–63% in 2Q26, a forecast +13–18% for server DRAM in 3Q26 — partly because long-term-agreement price ceilings cap how fast contracts can reprice, not because supply has loosened 34. Samsung's own Q1 2026 call confirmed the inversion: "It is true that conventional DRAMs have higher margins versus HBM," EVP Jaejune Kim told investors, adding that "the supply shortage has actually become more intense" 40.

The structural engine underneath is the HBM crowd-out. TrendForce puts HBM's share of top-3 DRAM wafer input at ~18% at end-2025, rising to ~22% by end-2026 and ~30% by end-2027 — while HBM's share of bits runs only 8%, 9%, and 13% over the same span. That wafer-to-bit gap is the crowd-out: each HBM wafer yields far fewer sellable bits than the conventional wafer it displaces 35.

Where does CXMT sit in this causal chain? The counterpoint to the "CXMT caused the spike" narrative is that its role is real but one step upstream: its 2023–24 undercutting — selling 20–30% below incumbent prices — is part of what made DDR4 unprofitable enough for the incumbents to announce exit. The spike's proximate triggers were those EOL announcements, the HBM wafer diversion, and tariff-driven pull-forward — not any withdrawal of CXMT supply. Nor did CXMT's output relieve the squeeze: its discount narrowed from 20–30% to roughly 5–10% blended by Q1 2026, and module vendors reported near price parity at Computex in June 2026 37. That is rational price-taker behavior in a shortage — parity pricing is evidence of market tightness, not of a lost cost advantage; CXMT's edge became availability, not price 37.

Micron's framing is the tell: asked about Chinese competition on a call reporting 84.9% gross margins, the answer was about geography, not technology 39. This matters for the IPO because it defines what investors are actually buying: CXMT's record quarter is the product of a price event its competitors engineered and its own supply barely touched — a windfall rented from the incumbents' capital-allocation choices, not earned through pricing power of its own.

04How big is CXMT really — one company, three different market shares

"CXMT is the world's No. 4 DRAM maker" is true on every metric that matters — but it means a different number on each one: roughly 7.6% of revenue, ~9% of bits, and ~13% of wafer capacity, and the gaps between those three figures are precisely the measure of the company's node disadvantage and its price-cycle windfall.

Start with the metric that headlines the IPO coverage. On Omdia's Q1 2026 cut, CXMT holds 7.6% of global DRAM revenue, fourth behind the three incumbents 76:

  1. 1Samsung38.6%
  2. 2SK Hynix28.8%
  3. 3Micron22.4%
  4. 4CXMT7.6%

The trajectory behind that rank is steep but the sourcing needs honest handling. One Omdia-derived series has CXMT at 3.97% of revenue in 2Q25 rising to 7.67% by 4Q25 96; a second Omdia-derived cut, published by Seoul Economic Daily on July 11, puts 4Q25 at 4.7% and Q1 2026 at 7.6% — a quarter-on-quarter near-doubling 76. Both series trace to Omdia; the 4Q25 discrepancy (7.67% vs 4.7%) most plausibly reflects different revenue definitions rather than a factual dispute, and both land in the same ~7.6–8% zone for Q1 2026. Either way, the composition of the jump is the tell: SemiAnalysis decomposes CXMT's 1Q26 revenue surge into bit shipments up roughly 11% while blended ASPs rose about 57% 22. Five-sixths of the share gain was price, not volume — the revenue-share metric most quoted in IPO marketing is also the most cycle-inflated one, and it will compress mechanically when DRAM pricing normalizes.

The physical footprint tells a bigger story than the revenue line. Nikkei-sourced reporting puts CXMT at roughly 300k wafer starts per month across Hefei (~180k) and Beijing (~120k) as of early 2026, running at full capacity 27 — SemiAnalysis's more conservative model has ~265k installed at end-2025, growing to ~350k by end-2026, within sight of Micron's ~385k 2211. On wafers, CXMT is nearly the world's #3. The prospectus confirms there is no slack left in the installed base: utilization climbed from 87.06% (2023) to 92.46% (2024) to 95.73% in 2025 28, so every increment of 2026 output must come from newly installed tools, not sweating existing ones. And in one legacy segment CXMT is already the incumbent: after the Big Three's exits, TrendForce ranks it the largest LPDDR4X supplier in the world and projects it to lead global output-bit growth in 2026 — even as CXMT itself has announced phased DDR4 termination by 2026, deliberately shrinking that legacy base 30.

The counterpoint to the more breathless framing deserves stating plainly: the "25–30% of global capacity by 2027" narratives circulating around the IPO are backed by zero installed tools. The Shanghai fab — sized at 2–3x the Hefei site, with a hypothetical full-ramp potential above 400k wpm — begins equipment move-in only in H2 2026, with first production in 2027 29; announcements are not installations, and even the aggressive SemiAnalysis build-out (adding roughly 85k/70k/80k wpm per year toward ~420k end-2027 and ~500k end-2028) reaches only about 17% of global capacity by end-2027 2211.

Why this matters: the three shares are one company viewed through three lenses — the 13% is what China's capital has already bought, the 9% is what CXMT's technology can currently extract from it, and the 7.6% (as of Q1 2026) is what a once-in-a-decade price cycle is temporarily paying for it — so any valuation built on the revenue number is capitalizing the cycle, while the durable claim is the wafer base and the durable discount is the node gap.

05The technology tax — G4, DDR5 yield, and the die-size penalty

CXMT's DDR5 is real, shipping, and physically verifiable — and the same teardown that proves it exists also prices the tax every wafer pays: a node the incumbents ran five years ago, a die roughly 40% larger than Samsung's equivalent, and a cost-per-bit more than 30% above the Big 3, while the one number that would show the tax shrinking — yield — is exactly the one nobody independent can confirm.

How far behind depends on the yardstick, and the flattering-to-incumbents answer is not the only honest one. Shim Dae-yong, a 26-year SK Hynix veteran, puts the gap at "more than five" years — benchmarking against the Big 3's first 16nm-class production in 2018–19 77. TechInsights' own product benchmark — the same 16Gb DDR5 part on D1z, mass-produced in 2021 — gives roughly three years 20, and analysts quoted in the IPO-week prospectus coverage land at "about two to three" 11. Both framings are true against different yardsticks; neither erases the arithmetic that a ~40% larger die than Samsung's equivalent means structurally fewer chips per wafer 21 — though that comparison partly conflates node gap with design efficiency, since Samsung's part sits on a newer node.

Whatever the true rung, the P&L consequence is not in dispute: SemiAnalysis estimates CXMT's cost-per-bit runs more than 30% above the Big 3 while its DRAM ASP sits only 5–10% below — meaning the >70% gross margin printed in 1Q26 is a shortage-pricing artifact, not proof the technology tax has been paid down 22.

Nor is the product line standing still. LPDDR5X at 8533 and 9600 Mbps entered mass production in May 2025, with 10667 Mbps in customer trials by October — an inline speed ladder of Mbps — though spec parity is not node or cost parity: Samsung has run 12nm-package LPDDR5X since August 2024 24. That mobile part now sits in over 30% of Chinese Android-brand phones ex-Huawei, with LPDDR above 40% of shipments 25 — the customer story belongs to section 08.

The escape route is G5, a 15nm-class 1a-equivalent: R&D targeted complete in late 2025, samples in 2H2026, mass production by late 2026 — explicitly without EUV, "similar to Micron's 1a" 20. But there is no mid-2026 confirmation of G5 risk production, and SemiAnalysis expects the majority of 2026 output to stay on G4 22. First-principles litho analysis says the EUV denial is not the wall it looks like: DUV self-aligned multi-patterning can carry planar DRAM through 0b/0a, and X-SAQP actually beats EUV on CD uniformity for storage-node arrays. The cost is process-step complexity — and CXMT's specific trade, relaxing minimum metal pitch to stretch cheaper LELE patterning, is the die-size penalty observed in the teardown. The binding constraint may be restricted high-aspect-ratio etch and metrology tools, not exposure wavelength 26. Shim's counterview — "from 1b onward, EUV is effectively unavoidable" 77 — puts the tipping point almost exactly at the node after the one G5 targets.

Why this matters: the IPO prices CXMT as if the technology tax is a temporary toll, but every independently checkable number says it is structural at G4 — so the bull case rests on a G5 transition and a yield curve that, as of the prospectus itself, no one outside Hefei has seen 11.

06HBM — the gap the prospectus won't fund

The single most revealing disclosure in CXMT's IPO documents is an absence: the company built its trillion-yuan narrative on AI memory, yet the prospectus allocates zero dedicated capital to HBM capacity — consistent with a product that is still samples and a pilot line, roughly two product generations and ~4 years behind SK Hynix, and slipping.

Read the filing's own language. HBM2 is described as "small-scale production," HBM3 as "sample delivery," and ~98.3% of revenue comes from LPDDR (66.43%) and DDR (31.87%) — yet the use-of-proceeds table contains no HBM capacity-expansion line at all 11. The only HBM-adjacent money is a RMB 9B "frontier memory R&D" bucket, an R&D allocation, not a fab. For a company whose IPO story leans on China's AI-memory self-sufficiency, that is a load-bearing silence: you do not omit funding for a product you expect to ship at volume inside the fundraising horizon.

CXMT's regulatory status as of July 2026 is the strangest in the export-control regime: an interagency committee has approved putting it on the Entity List, Commerce refuses to publish the addition, Apple is lobbying for a guarantee it never happens — and the tools CXMT needed are already bolted to its cleanroom floors, so the control system is arguing about a door the equipment already walked through.

The omission was a choice, not an oversight — three times over. In March 2024, Biden administration officials pushed to list CXMT and were overruled 42. In December 2024, BIS listed 140 entities and skipped it again; CSIS's Gregory Allen wrote that CXMT's equipment purchases were simply "too attractive to turn down" for the US toolmakers, with Japanese pressure on Tokyo Electron's behalf reportedly reinforcing the carve-out, and Applied Materials and Lam reportedly holding licenses to supply the fabs 42. And since October 2025, Commerce under Undersecretary Kessler has published zero Entity List additions of any kind while trade talks proceed — an approved CXMT listing sitting in a drawer 41.

The freeze matters less than it appears, because CXMT front-ran it. Allen's summary is the operative fact: the US "cannot effectively take back the equipment" 42. What 'sanctioned' actually means here

CXMT is not on the Commerce Entity List — the binding one that cuts off US-origin exports 41. It is on the DoD 1260H list, which carries no export prohibition: a federal-procurement ban plus investment and reputational effects only 48. What actually constrains its fabs are tool-category rules — the 18nm half-pitch threshold, replaced January 2025 by measurable cell-geometry criteria — which apply regardless of any listing 45, and Dutch/Japanese licensing, which governs lithography shipments and servicing case-by-case 47.

The localization claims circulating around the IPO deserve heavy discounting. The "40–50% domestic tools, etch >60%" figures are unit counts from unnamed industry-chain sources 50; Naura's own assessment (via Kerrisdale) puts DRAM wafer-fab-equipment localization at roughly 10% by value, rising toward 20–30% 50. Metrology is ~5% localized, overlay effectively 0% 55; ArF photoresist is under 1% domestic against a 40% national target 56; and no domestic immersion scanner is in volume production — SMEE's verified ceiling is 90nm mass production 54, while Yuliangsheng's 28nm-class scanner is only in testing at SMIC, with sub-10nm domestic litho unlikely before 2030 53. ASML's China collapse from 41% of system sales to 19% is thus best read not as decoupling achieved but as the buying spree ending: CXMT now runs on its installed NXT-class base 52. Meanwhile the January 2026 Section 232 proclamation tariffed only advanced logic at 25%, leaving commodity DRAM untouched pending a Phase 2 review 49.

The counterpoint: this equilibrium is a bargaining posture, not a settlement. The freeze could reverse in a single Federal Register notice; the suspended affiliates rule snaps back on November 10, 2026 46; and Moolenaar publicly opposes any Apple-style guarantee 48. But note the asymmetry — listing CXMT post-IPO would strand US suppliers' service licenses and revenue, not CXMT's installed base, which is precisely why Commerce keeps not doing it. Why this matters: investors pricing the IPO are being asked to value a company whose worst-case sanction — the one already approved and sitting unpublished — would hurt its American suppliers more than its own production through at least 2027.

08Customers — Tencent's RMB 20 billion contract, Apple's audition, and the 2.79% problem

CXMT's demand book is mid-transformation: what was a phone-memory story eighteen months ago is becoming a state-adjacent AI-server story — server DRAM tripled to 26.51% of revenue on Beijing's localization mandates, Tencent has reportedly signed a multi-year RMB 20 billion supply deal, and Apple is auditioning its chips — yet the same prospectus describes a company that sells almost nothing outside Greater China and routes 68% of revenue through five distributors.

Start with the mix. LPDDR — the mobile line that fed Xiaomi, Transsion, OPPO, and vivo — still dominated FY2025 revenue at 66.43%, but the DDR series behind it jumped from 13.26% to 31.87% in a single year 11.

The sharper cut is the end-market one: server DRAM went from 8.39% of revenue in 2024 to 26.51% in 2025 11.

That tripling did not happen in a vacuum. Beijing required data centers to source at least 50% of chips locally from August 2025, then barred foreign accelerators from state-funded projects in November 2025 70. Both rules chiefly target accelerators — the memory pull-through is analyst inference, not statutory text — but a hyperscaler forced to buy domestic AI silicon has every procurement incentive to pair it with domestic DRAM.

The marquee proof point is Tencent: a long-term server-DRAM supply agreement worth more than RMB 20 billion (~$2.94 billion) covering several years, per Reuters 63. Handle it with care. The sourcing is anonymous, the term conflicts across Reuters' own sources (two say up to three years, one says five), HBM inclusion is "not immediately clear," neither company commented, and it appears in no filing 63. What is corroborated separately: CXMT is taking DDR5 server orders from both Tencent and ByteDance after completing customer validation — though DDR5 yields are reportedly still low, so order intake is not yet proven high-volume delivery 64.

The rest of the book is auditions, not orders. Apple approached Commerce in late May and has since begun technical validation of CXMT DRAM — the step preceding supplier approval — for China-market devices only, with no commercial commitment; CXMT parts are priced 10–30% below incumbents 6566. The cautionary precedent is YMTC, whose 2022 NAND qualification reached a similar stage before Apple shelved it under congressional pressure 66. Qualcomm's CFO, asked directly about CXMT, said the company is "probably among the first to be qualified with every memory provider" — a generic, hedged CFO line, not a Snapdragon design win 68. HP and Dell have started qualifying CXMT DRAM (HP for non-US markets only) and Acer/ASUS are sourcing local memory via Chinese partners, all anonymously sourced with no confirmed shipping win as of July 2026 69. For the Big 3's customers, CXMT qualification is optionality and negotiating leverage as much as intended volume.

Now the structure underneath. Top-five customers took 74.12%, 67.30%, and 68.08% of main-business revenue in 2023–2025 — and those direct customers are distributors, which obscures per-hyperscaler revenue; the prospectus names Alibaba Cloud, ByteDance, Tencent, Lenovo, Xiaomi, Transsion, Honor, OPPO, and vivo only as end customers 67.

The 2.79% problem

The prospectus geography: mainland China 42.79% of FY2025 revenue, Hong Kong-booked 57.21%, genuinely overseas roughly 2.79% 11. The Hong Kong line is distributor domicile, not end demand — those chips flow back into Chinese devices and data centers. Strip the booking artifact and CXMT is a domestic-demand company whose "globalization" is, so far, a rounding error.

The Korean-industry counterargument writes itself: the server-mix tripling reflects mandate-driven procurement, not head-to-head competitive wins — remove the localization rules and the hyperscalers revert to Samsung and SK Hynix. True, but the flip side matters for an IPO investor: mandate-driven demand is precisely the kind that does not churn on price or on a Micron discount. Why this matters: CXMT's revenue quality question is not whether the demand is real — it is whether a captive, five-distributor, one-country book deserves a multiple built for a global memory franchise.

The annualization trap

The ¥2–3 trillion scenarios are 20x P/E on ¥150–200 billion of annualized 2026 attributable profit — a run-rate built by extrapolating the best quarter of the biggest memory shortage on record. The prospectus itself flags the H1 2026 surge as carrying "存在不可持续的风险" — a risk of being unsustainable — and FY2025 depreciation of ¥24.68 billion nearly consumed gross profit 95. And per-share claims on consolidated profit overstate the owner's take: the parent holds 30.68%/31.72% of the two fab entities, so ~74% of FY2025 consolidated net income leaked to minorities, and even in the blowout Q1 2026 (~¥33.0B consolidated vs. ¥24.76B attributable) roughly 25% still did 89. H1 2026 guidance of ¥50–57 billion attributable is enormous — but it is a peak-cycle number, not a through-cycle one 9.

The structural bull case survives the annualization critique, and it deserves stating plainly: SK Hynix and Samsung trade at ~21x trailing earnings in the same cycle, CXMT is the only listed pure-play on Chinese memory self-sufficiency, and scarcity value in a mandate-protected home market is a real, durable asset — the honest debate is the size of the multiple, not the existence of a premium 91. But the SMIC precedent shows the premium is a local phenomenon: the day its STAR shares popped +201.97% at debut, its Hong Kong line fell 25.23% 93 — the same company, two markets, two prices; CXMT's 50% strategic lockup and greenshoe are engineered to damp exactly that pop. As of July 14, with pricing due July 15, this matters because the number that prints this week will be the first market-cleared valuation of Chinese memory — and every rung above it is still, for now, a story 3.

10What could break the thesis — the bear case, with dates on it

Every input to CXMT's windfall — price, policy, and patience — is mean-reverting, and unlike most IPO-week bear cases, this one comes with quantified forecasts and a calendar.

The question for the subscription window is not whether CXMT is real — the wafers, the customers, and the state's commitment are. It is whether the buyer is paying a peak-cycle price for a company whose own filing history proves it loses money at mid-cycle prices.

ARA Research

The cycle turns, on schedule. Every prior memory supercycle ended in a 45–65% price collapse over four to eight quarters: 1996–97 saw −51% then −65% after ~50 announced fab projects; 2019 DRAM ASPs fell 47.4%; the 2022 bust cut spot by more than half 85. This one has a specific supply wave attached: Samsung is converting P4 to 1c DRAM and restarted P5 (~$100B+ combined, production 2028–29), SK Hynix's M15X ramps up to 90k wspm from 2026–27 with the Yongin Y1 cleanroom opening February 2027, and Micron's Idaho ID1 delivers first wafers mid-2027 with ID2 in late 2028 8180. CXMT's own Shanghai site — the supply leg the incumbents cannot discipline — starts production in 2027 22. Bernstein has put numbers on the collision: DRAM prices +43.2% in 2027 to $2.23/Gb, then −52.9% in 2028 to $1.05/Gb 82. Even Micron's bullish "tight beyond calendar 2027" contains its own expiry date — the same call conceded "gradual improvement in 2028," and "no line of sight to oversupply" is what incumbents said in mid-2018, two quarters before that crash 80. Morgan Stanley — whose 2024 "winter" call was wrong and retracted — now flags memory at "peak rate of change," warning a small demand miss flips the market "instantly" into oversupply 83.

CXMT bleeds first in a downcycle. The prospectus quantifies the exposure better than any short-seller could: at 2023–24 prices, CXMT's gross margin was −4.03% ex-writedown-reversals in 2024 86, and 2023 product-level gross margins ran as deep as −108.76% on the DDR series and −121.37% on the LPDDR series per prospectus-derived press summaries 95. With cost-per-bit more than 30% above the Big 3 22, Jim Handy's mechanism applies with force: at today's ~80% incumbent margins even a 2x-cost producer earns ~60% gross margin, but "today's AI-driven shortage will reverse at some point... the majors will see prices drop to cost" — a level below CXMT's cost, at which it would "hemorrhage money" 38. The counterpoint is that CXMT has already survived one price war on state capital, and a mandate-protected home market blunts the exit pressure a commercial firm would face 38. But survival is not a valuation thesis at 16x book.

Demand is cracking at the consumer edge already. As of July 2026, conventional DRAM contract growth has decelerated from ~90–95% QoQ (1Q26) to a forecast +13–18% (3Q26); DDR4 16Gb spot logged its first ~5% decline in roughly a year in early April 2026 (the 8Gb line has since resumed climbing — see Section 03); consumer SSD spot fell 30–40%; phone OEMs are cutting memory content (flagships to 12GB) and unit targets (Transsion −40%, OPPO −20%, Vivo −15%); and IDC sees 2026 PC units down 11.3% — demand destruction, not new supply, is doing the rebalancing 84. The AI leg still holds, but it rests on ~$660–690 billion of 2026 hyperscaler capex running at 45–57% of revenue and funded increasingly by debt, with memory's share of that capex tripling from ~8% to ~30% and >40-week DRAM lead times showing the classic double-ordering signature of every prior bust 87.

Policy can reprice the story in one notice. The approved-but-unpublished Entity List addition (Section 07) is a standing option Washington can exercise after the float; the suspended affiliates rule snaps back November 10, 2026 4146. In Seoul, the legal record is hardening: prosecutors indicted 10 people in December 2025 for leaking Samsung's 10nm-class DRAM process to CXMT, a former Samsung researcher drew a seven-year sentence in April 2026, and Korea's Supreme Court widened criminal exposure for tech-leak rings in February 717273. None of it binds CXMT directly — but it feeds the political case for listing, and it undercuts the "independent innovation" narrative the IPO leans on. Korea's response is capital as well as courts: a ₩33 trillion support package plus a Semiconductor Special Act with state-funded fab infrastructure from 2027 74, while Samsung and SK Hynix pulled HBM4 mass production forward to February 2026 and are expanding 1c capacity roughly 8–9x — though SK Hynix notably slowed its HBM4 ramp in June 2026 to chase commodity-DRAM margins, evidence the incumbents currently see more profit in the shortage than threat in Hefei 7597.

What the bears still get wrong. Korea's June 2026 semiconductor exports hit $44.82 billion, up 199.5% — the supercycle is, for now, swamping any displacement effect, and record incumbent margins are the opposite of a CXMT-damaged market 79. The technology-gap estimates span an unhelpfully wide range — "two to three years" (mainstream analysts) to "more than five" (an ex-SK Hynix VP) 7877 — and a company that went from 70k wafer starts in 2022 to roughly 300k in 2026 has repeatedly beaten the conservative end of such ranges 4227. The red-team pass on this report's three strongest claims (the July 16 subscription schedule, the Q1 2026 financials, and CXMT's absence from the Entity List) found no contradicting primary evidence as of 2026-07-14.

What would confirm the bear case, in order of arrival: a final IPO price near the ¥4.41 floor (institutional inquiry refusing the growth story); DDR4 spot breaking decisively lower in 2H26; US CSP server-DRAM inventory builds converting to order cuts; a 2027 Shanghai-fab ramp landing into falling prices; and an Entity List publication after the trade truce lapses. Why this matters: the subscription closes July 16, and every one of those falsifiers has a date attached that lands after the money is committed — which is, perhaps, the point of the schedule.
