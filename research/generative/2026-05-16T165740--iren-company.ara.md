## 01. The pivot, in three years

In thirty-six months {accent}IREN{/} went from defaulting on $107.8 million of NYDIG-funded miner debt to a counterparty Microsoft trusted with a $9.7-billion compute contract [^3,10]. The through-line that connects those two endpoints is the bet two ex-Macquarie infrastructure bankers placed in 2018: that vertically-integrated, low-cost-power data centers built first for Bitcoin would convert into AI compute faster than greenfield neocloud rivals could pour concrete.

:::stats
- {label: NYDIG SPV default, value: $107.8M, note: "Nov 2022 [^3]"}
- {label: Stock low, value: $1.02, note: "Dec 2022 (96% drawdown)"}
- {label: Microsoft contract, value: $9.7B, note: "Nov 2025 [^10]"}
- {label: NVIDIA contract + warrant, value: $5.5B, note: "May 2026 [^11,12]"}
:::

Daniel and Will Roberts incorporated Iris Energy in New South Wales in November 2018 and listed on Nasdaq three years later at $28.00, raising capital against 8.27 million ordinary shares with JPMorgan, Canaccord, and Citi as bookrunners [^1,2]. The brothers walked into the IPO with roughly 78% of the voting power and a playbook borrowed from their infrastructure-finance day jobs: secure long-duration grid connections in jurisdictions with structurally cheap power, build the substations and the buildings, then sit on the asset and decide later what load to put on top of it [^1]. The first load was bitcoin miners. The thesis was always that the building was the moat.

That thesis nearly broke the company in 2022. Two limited-recourse SPVs holding $107.8 million of NYDIG-financed miners were generating roughly $2 million a month against $7 million of monthly debt service after Bitcoin's drawdown; the SPVs received default notices in November and the equity walked away [^3]. By December the stock printed $1.02, down 96% from the IPO, and the British Columbia government froze new BC Hydro connections for crypto miners with an 18-month moratorium that cut off the company's pipeline in its founding province [^23]. The default was ==contractually clean but reputationally costly==: the ringfencing held, but a three-year litigation tail with NYDIG only closed in August 2025 when IREN paid $20 million to settle — a number small enough to confirm the legal structure had worked, and large enough to confirm NYDIG had a colorable claim worth paying to make go away [^18].

The pivot itself began quietly. On February 5, 2024 IREN commissioned 248 NVIDIA H100s for Poolside AI — its first AI Cloud customer and the first revenue dollar that did not depend on the bitcoin price [^4]. Ten days later the company shortened its name from Iris Energy to IREN, signaling to the market that the bitcoin-miner identity was no longer the whole story [^5]. From there the slope steepens fast: a $9.7 billion, five-year GB300 contract with Microsoft anchored to the Childress site in November 2025 [^10]; then in May 2026 a $3.4 billion NVIDIA cloud contract paired with a five-year warrant entitling NVIDIA to buy 30 million IREN shares at a $70 strike, signed the same week the Sweetwater 1 substation energized [^11,12,13]. Two contracts, eighteen months apart, worth more than fifteen times the company's IPO valuation.

The bear case on this trajectory is not that the contracts are fake; it is that insiders saw what was coming and traded around it. In September 2025 — between the Microsoft negotiations and the announcement — co-CEOs Daniel and Will Roberts each sold one million shares above $33, taking roughly $66 million off the table at what were then multi-year highs [^19]. A serious section says so. The disclosure does not impeach the operational pivot, but it does sharpen the question every later section has to answer: are the Roberts brothers building a thirty-year power-and-compute infrastructure business, or monetizing a story.

Every subsequent section depends on whether the answer is the first one.

## 02. The portfolio: five sites, five gigawatts

On paper, IREN's portfolio looks like the most diversified power book of any AI-pivoting bitcoin miner — six operating or in-build sites totaling roughly {accent}2.94 GW{/} of grid-connected and contracted capacity [^13,14]. In practice, ninety percent of the gigawattage sits in two adjacent Texas counties, and the largest single tranche — Sweetwater — still has no anchor tenant as of mid-May 2026 [^13].

:::rank-list
- {label: "Sweetwater 1, TX (substation live)", value: "1,400 MW", pct: 100, highlight: true}
- {label: "Childress, TX", value: "750 MW", pct: 54}
- {label: "Sweetwater 2, TX (in build)", value: "600 MW", pct: 43}
- {label: "Mackenzie, BC", value: "80 MW", pct: 6}
- {label: "Prince George, BC", value: "50 MW", pct: 4}
- {label: "Canal Flats, BC", value: "30 MW", pct: 2}
:::

The Sweetwater complex is the franchise bet. On {accent}May 1, 2026{/}, IREN energized the 345 kV / 138 kV substation feeding Sweetwater 1, unlocking the full 1.4 GW connection [^13]. Sweetwater 2's 600 MW Large Load Interconnection Agreement was executed on March 17, 2025, backed by a {accent}$4.1M non-refundable plus $26.9M refundable deposit{/} with the transmission provider, and is targeting a late-2027 energization [^14]. NVIDIA has publicly named Sweetwater 1 as the flagship site for its {tag}Vera Rubin DSX{/} reference design [^11,28] — but the May 7, 2026 partnership announcement carried no specific MW commitment for SW1 beyond the headline 5 GW framework figure [^11].

Childress is the only site that is currently a full revenue producer at scale. AEP Texas signed the original 600 MW binding connection agreement in January 2022; ERCOT approved an upsize to 750 MW in July 2024; the Phase 3 substation energized in December 2024; and 650 MW was operational by mid-2025, with 750 MW targeted by end of calendar 2025 [^6,7]. It is the campus running the {tag}NVIDIA Blackwell{/} fleet, and the contracted home for the Microsoft {tag}GB300{/} build-out [^6,10,30].

:::callout(kind=warn, label="Regulatory")
British Columbia's December 21, 2022 Order in Council suspended new BC Hydro connection requests for cryptocurrency mining for an initial 18 months, and the freeze has since been treated as effectively permanent [^23]. IREN's combined 160 MW BC fleet is grandfathered and was explicitly confirmed as unaffected at the time of the order [^43] — but the company cannot grow BC capacity without re-classification, and the path to convert grandfathered crypto load into a recognized AI-Cloud workload through BCUC review remains untested.
:::

The bear case reads this map differently. Of the ~2.94 GW on the slide, only Childress is generating GPU-scale revenue today; Sweetwater 1 is, as of this writing, an energized but empty substation; Sweetwater 2 is paper plus a $31M deposit [^14]; and the 160 MW of BC capacity is stranded by a moratorium that has no published end date [^23]. Framed unkindly, the {accent}"five-site, five-gigawatt"{/} pitch is 2.9 GW of fixed cost waiting for take-or-pay tenants who, with the exception of Microsoft at Childress [^10], haven't yet signed.

Why this matters: IREN's guided AI Cloud run-rate target of {accent}~$3.7B annualized revenue by year-end CY2026{/} [^6] is structurally inseparable from Sweetwater execution. Childress is largely accounted for by the Microsoft contract [^10]; the BC fleet is too small to move the number; and Sweetwater 2 doesn't energize until late 2027. That leaves Sweetwater 1 as the single load-bearing variable — one anchor tenant announcement at SW1 closes the loop between the substation and the guidance, and its absence keeps the entire AI Cloud thesis a forward-looking promise rather than a contracted backlog.

## 03. Two contracts that re-rated the stock

In six months IREN moved from a balance-sheet curiosity to a top-five Western GPU-cloud counterparty. Two contracts did the work: Microsoft signed a $9.7B, five-year right-of-access deal in November 2025 [^10], and NVIDIA followed in May 2026 with a $3.4B cloud contract paired with a warrant that could deliver another $2.1B in equity capital [^11,12]. Both anchor at Childress, both bundle GPUs from a vendor with skin in the game, and both prepay against the buildout. The shape is the same shape that made CoreWeave investable in 2023.

:::stats
- {label: Microsoft contract value, value: $9.7B, note: "5-yr, 20% prepay [^10]"}
- {label: Microsoft annualized run-rate, value: $1.94B, note: "once fully commissioned [^10]"}
- {label: NVIDIA contract value, value: $3.4B, note: "5-yr, ~60 MW Blackwell [^12]"}
- {label: NVIDIA warrant ceiling, value: $2.1B, note: "30M shares at $70 strike [^11]"}
- {label: Dell equipment supply, value: $5.8B, note: "GB300 racks for MSFT deal [^44]"}
- {label: Contracted ARR total, value: $3.1B, note: "as of Mar 31, 2026 [^6]"}
:::

On a $/MW basis these are some of the richest neocloud deals on record. Galaxy's Helios buildout for CoreWeave anchors the low end of the recent distribution at roughly $5.6M/MW of contracted revenue [^47]; IREN's two contracts sit eight to ten times higher because they bundle the GPUs, the colocation, and the operating contract into a single take-or-pay number [^10,12].

- {role: LOWEST, name: "Galaxy Helios / CoreWeave (800 MW)", value: $5.6M/MW}

:::callout(kind=info, label="Warrant vesting")
NVIDIA's right to invest only vests as NVIDIA GPU infrastructure is deployed across IREN campuses, with full vesting at 600,000 GPUs deployed [^15]. The economics turn on velocity of deployment, not the headline $2.1B.
:::

**The Microsoft deal mechanics.** The capacity is ~200 MW of IT load at Childress Horizons 1-4, populated with NVIDIA GB300 NVL72 racks — 72 Blackwell Ultra GPUs per rack, ~132 kW per rack, fully liquid-cooled [^30]. At that density 200 MW implies on the order of 1,515 racks, or roughly 109,000 GPUs once fully phased through 2026. Microsoft prepays 20% — about $1.94B — which on IREN's own math covers approximately 95% of the GPU capex [^10]. Dell sits behind that, supplying $5.8B of GB300 racks, networking, and storage under a separate vendor agreement that makes IREN the customer of record for the silicon [^44]. The press release calls it a "right of access," but with a meaningful prepayment and a five-year term it behaves like {accent}take-or-pay revenue{/} for modeling purposes, which is how Cantor Fitzgerald, JPMorgan, and IREN's own contracted-ARR disclosure all treat it [^31,15,6].

**The NVIDIA deal mechanics.** The cloud piece is smaller but denser: $3.4B over five years, or about $680M/yr in run-rate revenue on roughly 60 MW of air-cooled Blackwell capacity at Childress, ramping early 2027 [^12]. What re-rates the stock is the wrapper around it. NVIDIA took a five-year warrant on up to 30 million IREN ordinary shares struck at $70 — up to $2.1B in fresh cash to IREN if fully exercised [^11]. The warrant vests as GPUs land, not on a calendar schedule [^15]. NVIDIA's CFO has framed these neocloud equity stakes as "investing where it sees a need to ensure compute capacity is being built around its hardware" [^41]; in IREN's case the company is being paid in cash to host the GPUs and paid again in equity for hosting more of them.

**The pattern this fits.** IREN now joins the small club — CoreWeave, Lambda, Crusoe, Nebius — where NVIDIA holds direct equity in the customer that resells NVIDIA GPUs. The strategic logic is leverage: New Street estimates that for every $10B NVIDIA invests in OpenAI, roughly $35B in GPU pull-through follows [^34]. The same flywheel is now spinning at IREN, scaled to a smaller base. Critics call it the {accent}circular-revenue trade{/} and worry that NVIDIA's reported growth is being double-counted across customer and vendor books [^34]. Bulls counter that the alternative — funding the buildout off IREN's own balance sheet at a pre-deal market cap — wasn't a real option.

**Counterpoint.** The sell side is bimodal on exactly this question. JPMorgan rates IREN Underweight with a $46 price target, citing the "circular nature and undefined access to GPUs from Nvidia" [^15]. Cantor Fitzgerald is the loudest bull at a $142 price target, hiked after the Microsoft deal [^31]. Both analysts are looking at the same press releases. The May 12, 2026 upsized $2.6B convertible offering tells you which scenario IREN's CFO is funding for: hardware-heavy, deployment-velocity-constrained, equity-cushion-positive [^16]. Everything downstream in this report — segment mix, capital stack, comp valuation — depends on whether these two contracts convert from {accent}contracted ARR to recognized revenue{/} on the schedule the press releases imply.

## 04. Bitcoin in runoff: the cash cow funds the rocket

IREN is running the cleanest BTC-to-AI segment hand-off in the cohort. Bitcoin mining revenue collapsed from $232.9M in Q1 FY26 to $111.2M in Q3 FY26 [^8,6] — a 52% decline in two quarters — while AI Cloud Services revenue ramped from $7.3M to $33.6M over the same window [^8,6], a 4.6× lift off a tiny base. That hand-off is the entire story of FY26, and the segment chart shows it cleanly.

:::line-chart(title="Quarterly revenue by segment", subtitle="USD millions, FY26 to date", y-unit=$)
x: Q4 FY25,Q1 FY26,Q2 FY26,Q3 FY26
BTC mining: 180.3,232.9,167.4,111.2
AI Cloud: 7.0,7.3,17.3,33.6
:::

The mining decline is not accidental. Management is deliberately decommissioning S19j Pro and earlier ASICs to free megawatts and rack space for liquid-cooled GPU pods. The Q3 FY26 press release attributes the revenue drop to "lower average Bitcoin price combined with decommissioning of mining hardware ahead of GPU installation and billing" [^6]. The macro backdrop helps the decision: network hashprice sat at $39.04/PH/s/day in early May 2026, at or below breakeven for any fleet operating above 19 J/TH [^26]. IREN's ~15 J/TH fleet is still positive-margin, but the cushion is thin — and every ASIC pulled is a megawatt that pays out at AI-cloud unit economics instead.

:::slope(left-label="Aug 2025", right-label="May 2026", unit="EH/s")
| Item                    | Aug 2025 | May 2026 |
|-------------------------|----------|----------|
| IREN operating hashrate | 44.0     | 36.0     |
| Installed hashrate cap  | 50.0     | 50.0     |
:::

The slope tells the operational story: installed capacity is unchanged at 50 EH/s, but the operating fleet has fallen from 44.0 EH/s in August 2025 to roughly 36.0 EH/s in May 2026 [^20,37]. Eight exahash sitting idle is not a stranded asset — it's a deliberate vacancy created to host the GPUs that NVIDIA and Microsoft have already contracted.

Why is this NPV-positive even before counting the GPU billings? Look at the gross-margin spread. In Q3 FY26, Bitcoin Mining produced $75.9M of gross profit on $111.2M of revenue (68% GM); AI Cloud produced $29.0M of gross profit on $33.6M of revenue (86% GM) [^6]. {accent}Every megawatt reallocated from a 68%-margin mining workload to an 86%-margin AI Cloud workload lifts unit economics by roughly 18 percentage points{/} — and that arithmetic ignores the much higher revenue density per MW that liquid-cooled racks deliver versus an ASIC shelf.

:::compare
- {role: LOWEST,  name: "Bitcoin Mining GM",                       value: "68%"}
- {role: HIGHEST, name: "AI Cloud Services GM",                    value: "86%"}
- {role: SUBJECT, name: "Implied lift on every reallocated MW",    value: "+18 pp"}

The counterpoint is the headline P&L. IREN reported a $247.8M net loss in Q3 FY26 and a $155.4M net loss in Q2 FY26 [^6,9], with a $31.8M ASIC impairment in Q2 and additional impairments in Q3 [^9,6]. Adjusted EBITDA of $59.5M in Q3 [^6] is positive but thin against quarterly capex of roughly $1.36B [^15]. ==Decommissioning impairments are real economic cost== — the bear thesis is that IREN is junking machines faster than the GPU revenue is showing up, and that the timing mismatch eats through the equity cushion before Microsoft and NVIDIA cash starts arriving in volume.

The treasury policy is what makes the trade legible. IREN has historically sold 100% of mined coins immediately for fiat and carries zero BTC on its balance sheet [^36]. That choice transmits BTC volatility through quarterly revenue but not through balance-sheet write-downs — the opposite of Marathon Digital's multi-billion BTC reserve, which marks to market every quarter and contaminates the segment economics. For IREN, the mining segment is a pure cash cow in runoff: you can measure exactly how fast it's shrinking, and you can measure exactly how fast AI Cloud is growing, without a treasury overlay distorting the signal.

That is the lead indicator that matters. Management has guided to a $3.7B AI Cloud ARR target by year-end CY2026 [^6]; the question is not whether the contracts exist — they do, anchored by Microsoft and NVIDIA — but whether contracted ARR converts to recognized revenue on schedule. The Q1-to-Q3 FY26 segment hand-off is the first quarter where that conversion is visibly happening on the income statement, and the slope is steeper than the consensus model assumes.

## 05. Power as the moat: ERCOT West and BC Hydro

The structural advantage IREN inherited is not real estate, not buildings, not even GPUs — it is {accent}the contracted price of electrons at the meter{/}. Every other layer of the stack — racks, cooling, networking, even Hopper-class GPUs themselves — has either commoditized or is on a trajectory to. Megawatts behind a fast interconnect have not. Childress, IREN's flagship Texas site, printed a {accent}net all-in power cost of 3.0 cents/kWh in June 2025{/} [^22], with quarterly disclosures bracketing the range at roughly 2.9–3.3 c/kWh through FY25 [^21,22]. For a 750 MW operating fleet that runs ~24/7, every cent off the kWh is approximately $66M of annual gross-margin drop-through.

:::compare
- {role: LOWEST,  name: "IREN Childress net (Jun 2025)", value: "$30/MWh"}
- {role: HIGHEST, name: "ERCOT 2023 system avg",          value: "$48/MWh"}
- {role: SUBJECT, name: "ERCOT 2024 system avg",          value: "$26/MWh"}
:::

The Childress number is net of demand-response credits and reflects a Bitcoin-mining-friendly load profile (the miners are curtailable on minutes' notice), so it is not directly comparable to a hyperscaler PPA on firm load. But the gap between Childress and the ERCOT system average is wide enough that even an apples-to-oranges comparison clears the bar [^22,25]. The mechanism behind the gap is geography.

Childress sits inside the {accent}ERCOT West load zone{/}, the part of the Texas grid that connects the Permian wind belt and West Texas solar to the rest of the state via a long, congested 345 kV transmission path known as the West Texas Export. The export's capacity has not kept pace with the renewables it is supposed to evacuate. ERCOT West curtailed {accent}3.1 TWh of wind and 2.2 TWh of solar in 2024 — about 22% of the state's total renewable curtailment{/} [^25]. Generators paid to spill that energy. A load sited inside the constraint, on the generation side of the West Texas Export, captures what otherwise gets thrown away.

:::bars
- {label: "ERCOT West wind curtailed (2024)",     value: "3.1 TWh", pct: 100}
- {label: "ERCOT West solar curtailed (2024)",    value: "2.2 TWh", pct: 71}
- {label: "All-ERCOT renewables curtailed share", value: "22%",     pct: 22}
:::

IREN does not just buy congested-zone power — it gets paid to absorb it. The fleet is an active participant in ERCOT's Emergency Response Service (ERS) and Controllable Load Resource (CLR) programs, with monthly demand-response revenue running in the {accent}low-single-digit millions per month{/} at Childress [^22]. That is the explicit subsidy that converts a nominal West Texas hub price in the high-$20s/MWh into a {accent}net 3.0 c/kWh delivered cost{/}. The grid wants Childress to be there, and it is willing to pay for the option to switch it off.

The British Columbia fleet runs on a different mechanism but the same principle. Canal Flats (30 MW), Mackenzie (80 MW), and Prince George (50 MW) draw {accent}~98% direct hydro from BC Hydro{/} [^50], for a combined 160 MW that has always been the steadier of IREN's two grid stories. The legacy industrial-transmission tariff (Rate Schedule 1823) was for years among the cheapest grid power available to any large industrial customer in North America. RS 1823 was scheduled to sunset in April 2026 under BCUC review, and IREN's BC sites will transition to whatever successor schedule the regulator approves. The Prince George AI-cloud site inherits that schedule — important, because the 50 MW of NVIDIA Blackwell training fleet there is the only piece of the AI Cloud business running outside Texas.

:::callout(kind=warn, label="Texas SB 6")
Texas Senate Bill 6 was signed June 20, 2025 and took effect immediately. New large loads (≥75 MW) that interconnect after December 31, 2025 must install equipment that lets ERCOT directly curtail the load during firm load-shed events — and these loads sit at the front of the curtailment queue. {accent}Sweetwater 1 (energized May 1, 2026) and Sweetwater 2 (target late 2027) are both exposed; Childress was grandfathered{/} [^24].
:::

The SB 6 risk is more subtle than it first reads. ==SB 6 doesn't take away the cheap power — it lets ERCOT take it back when the grid needs it.== A Sweetwater interconnection priced against $20-something/MWh hub power is still cheap on an LCOE basis; it is just less firm. For Bitcoin-mining load that is a non-issue (Bitcoin miners are already the marginal curtailable load on every grid that has them). For an HPC tenant on a 5-year contract, firmness is what they are paying for, which means the post-2025 sites will carry a cost premium for back-up or behind-the-meter generation that Childress does not.

That premium is the reason the entire AI-data-center cohort is racing to lock in {accent}behind-the-meter gas{/}: CoreWeave's Plano build, Crusoe's Abilene campus for Stargate, Meta's Hyperion at Richland Parish, xAI Memphis. They are paying capex and accepting permitting risk to escape exactly the grid-curtailment exposure that SB 6 has now codified in Texas. IREN inherited cheap West Texas power as a Bitcoin miner and is now learning to defend it as an AI-cloud operator — a defense that runs through ERS revenue, RS 1823 succession in BC, and the political fight over how aggressively ERCOT swings the SB 6 hammer at loads that helped pay for the West Texas Export in the first place.

## 06. The capital stack: $5.7B in convertibles and 30M NVIDIA warrants

The headline reads like a margin call waiting to happen. Shares outstanding crossed 357.4M by Mar 31, 2026 [^6] — a 53% increase year-over-year [^46]. On top of $3.69B of convertible notes already on the balance sheet at quarter-end [^6], IREN priced another $2.6B convertible on May 12, 2026, with a $400M greenshoe that could push the new issue to $3.0B [^16]. Pro-forma convertible debt now approaches $5.7B. NVIDIA holds a warrant for up to 30M additional shares [^11]. Layer in the $6B ATM program [^46] and the cap table looks like a runaway freight train.

It isn't. {accent}The stack reads like a thought-through capital plan, not a panic-driven dilution event.{/} Three things change the arithmetic: customer prepayments that fund GPU capex without equity, a warrant whose strike sits above today's share price and only vests on operational milestones, and a capped-call structure on the new converts that pushes the economic conversion price to nearly twice the reference share price.

:::

The December 2025 deal set the template. IREN closed a $2.3B convertible package on Dec 8, 2025, paired with a $1.6B common-stock raise, and used a chunk of proceeds to retire two earlier convert vintages — the 3.50% notes due 2029 and the 3.25% notes due 2030 [^45]. The gross issuance was eye-catching; the net add to convertible debt was smaller. The May 2026 deal followed the same logic at a different scale: $2.6B priced (upsized from the $2.0B announced May 11), with a $400M greenshoe, a 1.00% coupon, maturity Dec 1, 2033, and a base conversion price of $73.07 — a 32.5% premium over the $55.15 reference price [^16]. Net proceeds clear $2.57B, or $2.96B if underwriters fully exercise the over-allotment.

:::compare
- {role: LOWEST,  name: "Reference share price",          value: "$55.15"}
- {role: HIGHEST, name: "Capped-call cap (economic max)", value: "$110.30"}
- {role: SUBJECT, name: "Base conversion price (no cap)", value: "$73.07"}
:::

The capped call is the technical detail most retail commentary misses. {accent}It cost IREN $174.5M of net proceeds to neutralize dilution between $73.07 and $110.30 — a 100% premium over the reference price{/} [^16]. If the stock settles below $73.07 at maturity, convert holders take cash. Between $73.07 and $110.30, the capped-call counterparties deliver shares to IREN that offset the conversion — existing shareholders are made whole. Above $110.30, those same counterparties absorb the incremental shares above the cap, not IREN's other holders. The premium IREN paid is a put on its own dilution, exercisable only if the stock works.

:::callout(kind=info, label="Dilution math")
At the base conversion price of $73.07, the $2.6B principal converts to ~35.6M shares (~9.7% of the 357.4M base) [^16,6]. NVIDIA's warrant adds up to another 30M shares, but only as GPUs land [^11,15]. Combined gross theoretical dilution from these two instruments alone is ~65M shares, or ~18% of the current base — before any incremental ATM draws against the $6B program [^46].
:::

Customer prepayments and warrants reshape the picture further. Microsoft's $1.94B prepayment covers roughly 95% of the GPU capex tied to the MSFT contract [^6,10] — that is third-party cash, not equity issuance. NVIDIA's warrant only vests as GPUs are deployed, with full vesting tied to the 600,000-GPU partnership target, and the $70 strike sits above the recent $55-ish trading range [^11,15]. It is a contingent equity injection that fires only if execution is on track. Stacked with the $3.6B GPU financing facility at sub-6% rates for the Microsoft contract [^6], the equity portion of total funding is far smaller than the gross convertible headline suggests.

The counterpoint is unavoidable. Convert holders are still real holders. If the stock trades flat-to-down through the 2033 maturity, IREN refinances or settles in cash, and the capped-call premium is a sunk cost. If it trades modestly above $73.07, holders take stock and the capped call kicks in. Only the third scenario — stock rips through $110.30 — is unambiguously positive for existing equity, and that scenario requires Microsoft and NVIDIA execution to deliver. Every quarter the company is not billing AI Cloud near the $3.1B contracted ARR run-rate [^6] is a quarter the convert holders inch closer to taking stock, and the equity cushion thins.

The capital stack is solvent on every reasonable scenario. The {accent}per-share story{/} requires the GPUs to land.

## 07. Versus the cohort: ranking the neoclouds and the BTC-miner pivots

IREN does not have a clean comp set. As a public neocloud it trades at a steep premium to CoreWeave (CRWV) and Nebius (NBIS) on every revenue multiple, because most of its current revenue is still bitcoin mining [^8,9]. As a bitcoin-miner-pivot story it has the largest single hyperscaler anchor in the cohort — Microsoft at $9.7B [^10] — but it ranks middle-of-pack on raw contracted AI megawatts. Cipher leads at 907 MW, Hut 8 at 597 MW; IREN sits at a ~480 MW exit-CY26 target [^40,39,6]. The honest framing is that IREN lives between two cohorts and the multiples on either side don't translate cleanly.

The cleanest cross-cohort frame is the 1-year stock return. The market has already priced the pivot, and ranked the survivors.

That credit-quality lens leads directly to customer concentration, which is the central feature (and central risk) of the entire neocloud asset class.

The neocloud asset class is structurally hyperscaler-tied — that's the source of its investment-grade-adjacent credit profile, and it's the single concentration risk every long has to underwrite. There is no diversified neocloud yet, and the bears will tell you there isn't going to be one.

The other reason the comp set is loose: the implied "miner-gets-acquired-by-neocloud" exit path is no longer free money. Core Scientific shareholders rejected the CoreWeave merger on October 30, 2025 — 203.5M CORZ shares voted against versus 20.8M for, and the deal was terminated [^33]. CORZ now trades independently and ranks below most peers on the 1-year return board. The implication for IREN is direct: any premium being paid today for "M&A optionality" — the read that a CRWV or a hyperscaler eventually buys the whole stack — should be discounted. The market has shown it will reject a bad price even when both sides are willing.

The bear's preferred multiples comparison runs the other direction. IREN trades at roughly 6-7x EV-to-contracted-ARR ($20.7B EV against $3.1B ARR), while CoreWeave trades at <1x EV-to-backlog ($91B EV against $99B backlog) [^27,6]. The denominators are not commensurable — annualized run-rate versus lifetime contracted revenue — but normalize to a 5-year frame and IREN still prints roughly 40% richer than CRWV on the same metric. The market is paying for the optionality (Sweetwater, 2 GW headroom, NVIDIA preferred-partner status) and for the cleanest segment hand-off in the cohort: a miner whose bitcoin revenue is in declared runoff while AI revenue ramps quarter-over-quarter [^6,8].

Why this comp question matters: it collapses to a single bet. Does Sweetwater sign an anchor before consensus moves IREN's FY27 numbers up. The longer the 2 GW Sweetwater campus sits empty, the more the IREN trade looks like execution risk priced at a CoreWeave premium. The sooner it signs, the more the cross-cohort re-rating runs — and the easier it becomes to argue IREN should trade up to APLD's relative multiple, not down to CRWV's.

## 08. The bear case: Culper, customer concentration, circular financing

The serious bear case on IREN does not require believing the Microsoft or NVIDIA contracts are fake. It requires believing four things converge: a forensic short report that has not been fully refuted, a single-customer dependency that mirrors CoreWeave's, a circular-financing loop that ties the entire neocloud cohort to one chip vendor's appetite, and an insider record that says the founders know when to clip the trade. None of those is fatal alone. Stacked, they describe an equity priced for flawless execution.

:::callout(kind=danger, label="Open litigation")
Culper Research published its short report on IREN on July 11, 2024, arguing the stock was 52–79% overvalued and that the Childress, Texas site was structurally unfit for HPC/AI workloads [^17]. On October 21, 2024 the Law Offices of Frank R. Cruz filed a securities class action covering holders from June 20, 2023 to July 11, 2024, alleging IREN overstated its HPC prospects and the readiness of Childress [^35]. In August 2025 IREN paid $20M to settle the long-tail NYDIG litigation — a clean walk-away cost, but more than nothing [^18].
:::

Culper's framing was unusually direct for a forensic short. The firm called IREN "wildly overvalued… worth anywhere from 52% to 79% less than current prices on this sum-of-the-parts basis" [^17], and argued that IREN was spending under $1M per MW on its sites while genuine HPC-ready data centers required $10–20M per MW [^17]. IREN's public response — that the company was "limited in how much we can say" while in commercial discussions — was thin for a public company facing a forensic short [^17]. The class action is unproven and could still be dismissed. But until it is, it is a live item in the legal record that any institutional buyer has to underwrite.

### Customer concentration

Microsoft alone is 61% of IREN's $3.1B contracted ARR [^6,10]. The structural parallel is CoreWeave, where Microsoft was 67% of 2025 revenue [^27] and where the bears have spent two years worrying about a single-customer renegotiation. {accent}IREN inherits the same single-customer dependency CoreWeave has been derated for — only at a higher EV multiple{/}. The contract is real and the cash flows are visible, but the dependency is the dependency: if Microsoft slows its capacity intake by one quarter, the entire IREN top line bends with it.

### The circular-financing question

{flag:red} NVIDIA invests equity into the neocloud cohort, those neoclouds buy NVIDIA GPUs, and the resulting capacity is then resold — sometimes back to NVIDIA itself, sometimes to OpenAI, which NVIDIA has also funded. For every $10B NVIDIA invests in OpenAI, New Street estimates roughly $35B in eventual GPU pull-through [^34]. Critics argue this both inflates NVIDIA's reported growth and creates correlated risk across the cohort — if NVIDIA cannot keep funding customers, every neocloud's contracted backlog gets stress-tested simultaneously. IREN's $3.4B contract with NVIDIA is direct exposure to that loop, not insulation from it [^12].

:::compare

The compare card is the entire bear case in one frame: the company has committed to a decade-scale buildout, raised ~$5.7B in convertible debt against it, and is doing both on top of roughly half a billion in trailing revenue [^7,10,16,45]. The convertibles are not on-balance-sheet leverage in the traditional sense, but they are dilution shares waiting to happen.

### The insider record

In September 2025 — after Microsoft negotiations had clearly begun and roughly seven weeks before the November 3 announcement — co-CEOs Daniel and Will Roberts each sold one million shares above $33, netting roughly $66M between them [^19]. Founder share sales at large public companies are typically executed under pre-arranged 10b5-1 plans, which is the right way to do this and not by itself problematic. But the timing — clipping the trade at then-multi-year highs, weeks before a $9.7B contract announcement — tells investors something about how the founders price the trade [^19].

### The dilution count

Share count was up 53% year-over-year through early 2026 [^46]. The new $6B ATM authorization could add roughly another 37.5% on top of that [^46]. The $2.6B May convertible adds up to 41.1M shares before the capped-call offset, and the capped call only protects shareholders if the stock trades above $110.30 [^16]. ==The capped call is the difference between a thoughtful capital plan and a runaway dilution event== — and it requires the stock to perform.

### Counterpoint

The $20M NYDIG settlement was 19 cents on the dollar of the original $107.8M claim [^18,3], which suggests the SPV ringfencing IREN built in 2022 did hold up under legal stress — exactly the test the Culper claims about Childress are now being subjected to. The class action could be dismissed; the contracts could ramp on schedule; the capped call could land in the money. The bull case is plausible.

The "why this matters" sentence is simple. IREN's equity is priced for flawless execution of two anchor contracts, on schedule, against a balance sheet that has already issued ~$5.7B in convertible debt to fund the buildout [^10,12,16,45]. The bear case is not that any one of these breaks. It is that all of them have to clear simultaneously.

## 09. What would have to be true — and what would break the thesis

The bull and bear cases on IREN converge on a small set of falsifiable propositions over the next 18 months. Strip away the multi-page narratives and the trade reduces to roughly six events you can mark on a calendar. Either they happen or they don't.

:::kv
- {term: "Sweetwater 1 anchor", def: "An announced tenant — hyperscaler or AI lab — committing >=500 MW of Sweetwater 1 capacity at MSFT-style economics. No announcement by Q1 CY27 weakens the entire SW1 thesis [^11,13]."}
- {term: "Microsoft GB300 delivery", def: "On-pace delivery of GPU racks through CY26 such that quarterly AI Cloud revenue exits CY26 above $300M/quarter (~$1.2B run-rate). The published $3.7B ARR target requires acceleration well past that line [^6,10]."}
- {term: "NVIDIA warrant vesting", def: "First milestone tranche vesting in CY26 is the public marker that NVIDIA deployment is on schedule. Stalled vesting flags execution risk [^11,15]."}
- {term: "BC Hydro RS-1823 successor", def: "BCUC approval of a successor industrial tariff that preserves Prince George economics. A materially worse rate compresses the only piece of AI Cloud running outside Texas [^50]."}
- {term: "ASIC decommissioning impairment tail", def: "Q4 FY26 and Q1 FY27 should show the impairment tail flatten as the last legacy ASICs come off the books. Continued large impairments would signal the AI fleet isn't replacing mining revenue fast enough [^6]."}
- {term: "Cantor vs JPMorgan PT convergence", def: "The sell side is bimodal — Cantor at $142, JPMorgan at $46. Convergence (in either direction) is the market's verdict on the contracted-to-recognized revenue conversion [^31,15]."}
:::

The bull's preferred ending: Sweetwater 1 signs an anchor in late 2026, NVIDIA's first warrant tranche vests on schedule, the segment hand-off accelerates with Q4 FY26 AI Cloud revenue cracking $80M, and the capped call on the May 2026 converts lands in the money by 2028. In that world IREN's current ~$20B EV looks cheap on a recognized-revenue basis by FY28, and the $3.7B contracted ARR converts into ~$1.5–2B of recognized FY27 revenue.

The bear's preferred ending: Sweetwater 1 stays empty through 2027, Microsoft slows GB300 intake by a quarter or two, ASIC impairments continue eating earnings, the ATM keeps issuing into a softer stock, and convert holders settle in shares above $73.07 but below $110.30 — handing the capped-call counterparties' offsetting deliveries to IREN at exactly the moment the company needs equity cushion most. In that world the $20M NYDIG settlement turns out to be the cheapest litigation tail of the decade.

The market is currently pricing somewhere between those endpoints. JPMorgan's $46 price target and Cantor's $142 are not analytical disagreement about facts — they are different assumptions about whether Sweetwater clears [^31,15]. The same data set produces both numbers. That bimodality is unusual at a $19B market cap; it usually resolves within a year of contract signings. Watch for the resolution.

:::quote(attr="The IREN trade, distilled")
The company has signed real contracts with real counterparties for real GPUs at a real site that is already powered. What it has not yet done is convert that backlog into recognized revenue at the pace the equity is priced for. The next six earnings reports are when that question gets answered.
:::

Why this matters beyond IREN: the cohort of bitcoin-miner pivots is the only group of public Western infrastructure operators with cheap power, fast permitting, and a willingness to do hyperscaler take-or-pay deals at sub-2030 timelines. If IREN's specific trade works, every name in the Section 07 ranking re-rates. If it fails, the entire {accent}miner-pivot-to-neocloud{/} thesis takes a body blow at the same time NVIDIA's circular financing comes under harder regulatory scrutiny. The $19B market cap is the market's bet that the first scenario is more likely than the second. The next four quarters will say.
