## Highlights (Top 5 Must-Read)

### 1. MM-IssueLoc: A Controlled Benchmark for Evaluating Visual Evidence in Multimodal Repository-Level Issue Localization
**Authors**: Shaoxiong Zhan, Shi Hu, Boyu Feng, et al.
**arXiv**: https://arxiv.org/abs/2607.15205v1
**TL;DR**: Introduces a 652 issue-PR-instance benchmark spanning 23 languages to test whether coding agents can use visual evidence (screenshots, diagrams) in issues to localize the right code. Even the strongest retriever only reaches 33.86 function Acc@10, and text-dominant performance doesn't transfer cleanly to visual-evidence cases.
**Why it matters**: A concrete, controlled measurement of a gap coding agents are known to have — grounding a bug report's images, not just its text — directly relevant to how agentic SWE benchmarks should be built.

### 2. Plover: Steering GUI Agents through Plan-Centric Interaction
**Authors**: Madhumitha Venkatesan, Shicheng Wen, Jiajing Guo, et al.
**arXiv**: https://arxiv.org/abs/2607.15193v1
**TL;DR**: Externalizes a GUI agent's task plan as a persistent, inspectable, revisable artifact instead of hidden internal state, letting a human or the agent itself repair a failing plan mid-execution.
**Why it matters**: Shows many "autonomous" GUI-agent failures are structurally repairable once the plan is visible — a reusable pattern for making any long-horizon agent loop debuggable rather than a black box.

### 3. Benchmarking Multimodal Large Language Models for Scientific Visualization Literacy
**Authors**: Patrick Phuoc Do, Chau M. Ta, Chaoli Wang
**arXiv**: https://arxiv.org/abs/2607.15176v1
**TL;DR**: Tests six MLLMs on 49 assessment items built from 18 scientific visualizations across 8 techniques and 11 task types, finding no model exhibits uniform "SciVis literacy" — quantitative estimation and texture-based visualizations are consistent weak points.
**Why it matters**: Scientific-figure reading is a real deployment use case (research assistants, data analysis copilots) that generic VQA benchmarks don't stress-test; this quantifies exactly where current MLLMs still can't be trusted with a chart.

### 4. CRISP: Constrained Refinement via Iterative Squeezing Process for Robust Medical Image Segmentation under Domain Shift
**Authors**: Yizhou Fang, Pujin Cheng, Yixiang Liu, Xiaoying Tang, Longxi Zhou
**arXiv**: https://arxiv.org/abs/2607.15231v1
**TL;DR**: Proposes a target-free refinement framework that assumes segmentation-rank stability of positive regions holds under distribution shift, and iteratively squeezes noisy predictions toward this constraint. Delivers HD95 reductions of up to 7.0% on cardiac MRI across multi-center domain shifts, with no access to target-domain labels.
**Why it matters**: Domain shift is the single biggest reason medical-imaging models fail on deployment; a target-free fix (no re-annotation, no re-training on the new site) is the practically deployable kind of robustness gain.

### 5. Quantifying Training Membership Information in the Hyperspherical Embedding Geometry of Face Recognition Models
**Authors**: Ünsal Öztürk, Sébastien Marcel
**arXiv**: https://arxiv.org/abs/2607.15084v1
**TL;DR**: Runs a 180-model factorial study of face-recognition embedding geometry and finds the number of training identities — not backbone architecture or loss-head choice — has by far the largest effect on how easily an attacker can tell member from non-member faces via embedding-space membership inference.
**Why it matters**: A direct, actionable privacy-risk lever for anyone training or auditing biometric models: dataset scale, a variable teams often don't treat as a privacy knob, dominates membership-inference vulnerability.
