# arXiv AI Research - 2026-04-28

## Highlights (Top 5 Must-Read)

### 1. Universal YOCO for Efficient Depth Scaling
**Authors**: (arXiv group submission)
**arXiv**: https://arxiv.org/abs/2604.01220
**TL;DR**: Standard transformers struggle to scale inference-time compute efficiently because conventional looping strategies pile up FLOPs and inflate the KV cache alongside model depth. The paper integrates efficient-attention architectures with recursive computation to scale depth without paying the usual cache and compute tax.
**Why it matters**: Provides a concrete architectural recipe for cheaper test-time scaling — directly relevant to the current race to ship reasoning models that "think longer" without blowing inference budgets.

### 2. Thinking Without Words: Efficient Latent Reasoning with Abstract Chain-of-Thought
**Authors**: (arXiv group submission)
**arXiv**: https://arxiv.org/abs/2604.22709
**TL;DR**: Proposes Abstract Chain-of-Thought, a discrete latent reasoning post-training mechanism in which the LM emits a short sequence of tokens drawn from a reserved vocabulary in lieu of a natural-language CoT before producing the answer. Cuts the token budget for reasoning while preserving accuracy.
**Why it matters**: One of the cleanest demonstrations yet that reasoning need not be verbalised — implications for inference cost, interpretability, and the "faithfulness of CoT" debate.

### 3. Expert Upcycling: Shifting the Compute-Efficient Frontier of Mixture-of-Experts
**Authors**: (arXiv group submission)
**arXiv**: https://arxiv.org/abs/2604.19835
**TL;DR**: Mixture-of-Experts has become the dominant scaling architecture, but training large MoEs from scratch is expensive. Expert Upcycling shows how to convert a dense pre-trained model into a stronger MoE by replicating and specialising experts, advancing the compute-efficient frontier.
**Why it matters**: Practical recipe for getting MoE quality without paying the from-scratch training bill — useful for groups who already have strong dense checkpoints.

### 4. Cyber Defense Benchmark: Agentic Threat Hunting Evaluation for LLMs in SecOps
**Authors**: (arXiv group submission)
**arXiv**: https://arxiv.org/abs/2604.19533
**TL;DR**: A benchmark measuring how well LLM agents perform the core SOC analyst task of threat hunting. Evaluates frontier models (Claude Opus 4.6, GPT-5, Gemini 3.1 Pro, Kimi K2.5, Gemini 3 Flash) on 26 campaigns covering 105 procedures. The best model (Claude Opus 4.6) submits correct flags for only 3.8% of malicious events.
**Why it matters**: Concrete, hard, real-world agent benchmark where frontier models flatline near zero — a reality check on agent capability claims.

### 5. Involuntary In-Context Learning: Exploiting Few-Shot Pattern Completion to Bypass Safety Alignment in GPT-5.4
**Authors**: (arXiv group submission)
**arXiv**: https://arxiv.org/abs/2604.19461
**TL;DR**: Demonstrates that safety alignment relies on behavioural training that can be overridden when sufficiently strong in-context patterns compete with learned refusal behaviours. Introduces an attack class — Involuntary In-Context Learning (IICL) — that exploits this on GPT-5.4.
**Why it matters**: Empirical evidence that "shallow alignment" generalises to the latest frontier model; complements the gradient-analysis literature on why RLHF stays surface-level.
