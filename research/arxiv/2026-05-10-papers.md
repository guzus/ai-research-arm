# arXiv AI Research - 2026-05-10

## Highlights (Top 5 Must-Read)

### 1. AI Co-Mathematician: Accelerating Mathematicians with Agentic AI
**Authors**: Daniel Zheng, Ingrid von Glehn, Lars Buesing, Daniel M. Roy, Martin Wattenberg, Fernanda Viegas, Alex Davies, Pushmeet Kohli, et al. (Google DeepMind)
**arXiv**: [2605.06651](https://arxiv.org/abs/2605.06651)
**TL;DR**: A workbench pairing mathematicians with agentic AI for ideation, literature search, computational exploration, theorem proving, and theory building. Asynchronous stateful workspace tracks failed hypotheses and outputs native math artifacts. Scores **48% on FrontierMath Tier 4 — a new SOTA among all evaluated AI systems**.
**Why it matters**: First strong public demo that frontier agents can meaningfully accelerate open-ended mathematical research, not just solve canned problems.

### 2. UniPool: A Globally Shared Expert Pool for Mixture-of-Experts
**Authors**: Minbin Huang, Han Shi, Chuanyang Zheng, Yimeng Wu, Hong Cheng, et al.
**arXiv**: [2605.06665](https://arxiv.org/abs/2605.06665)
**TL;DR**: Replaces per-layer MoE expert ownership with one globally shared pool of experts accessed by independent per-layer routers. With pool-level balancing loss and NormRouter, UniPool matches vanilla MoE using only **41.6%–66.7% of expert parameters**, and improves loss/perplexity at scales from 182M to 978M.
**Why it matters**: Shows expert parameters need not grow linearly with depth — a potential structural lever for cheaper, more memory-efficient MoEs at scale.

### 3. Recursive Agent Optimization (RAO)
**Authors**: Apurva Gandhi, Satyaki Chakraborty, Xiangjun Wang, Aviral Kumar, Graham Neubig
**arXiv**: [2605.06639](https://arxiv.org/abs/2605.06639)
**TL;DR**: RL recipe for training agents that spawn and delegate sub-tasks to new copies of themselves. Recursive agents scale beyond their context window, generalize to harder problems than they were trained on, and reduce wall-clock latency vs. single-agent systems via divide-and-conquer.
**Why it matters**: Concrete training method for the "agents that call agents" pattern that's increasingly central to long-horizon reasoning systems.

### 4. Can RL Teach Long-Horizon Reasoning to LLMs? Expressiveness Is Key
**Authors**: Tianle Wang, Zhaoyang Wang, Guangchen Lan, Xinpeng Wei, Sipeng Zhang, Guanwen Qiu, Abulhair Saparov
**arXiv**: [2605.06638](https://arxiv.org/abs/2605.06638)
**TL;DR**: Introduces ScaleLogic, a controlled environment varying proof depth and logical expressiveness. RL compute follows a power law in depth ($T \propto D^γ$, $R^2 > 0.99$), and the exponent $γ$ rises monotonically (1.04 → 2.60) with logical expressiveness. More expressive training transfers better to math/general reasoning (+10.66 pts).
**Why it matters**: First clean scaling-law result showing *what* you train on shapes downstream reasoning transfer as much as *how much* — material for anyone designing RL post-training curricula.

### 5. EMO: Pretraining Mixture of Experts for Emergent Modularity
**Authors**: Ryan Wang, Akshita Bhagia, Sewon Min
**arXiv**: [2605.06663](https://arxiv.org/abs/2605.06663)
**TL;DR**: MoE design where tokens within a document share a per-document expert pool, encouraging coherent expert groupings to emerge from document boundaries alone. A 14B-total / 1B-active EMO trained on 1T tokens matches standard MoE; retaining only 25% of experts costs just 1% absolute, while standard MoEs collapse. Experts specialize semantically (math, code) rather than syntactically.
**Why it matters**: Practical path to selectively deployable, memory-light MoE subsets — important for on-device and serving cost.
