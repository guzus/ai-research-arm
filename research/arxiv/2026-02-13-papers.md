# arXiv AI Research - 2026-02-13

## Highlights (Top 5 Must-Read)

### 1. How RLHF Amplifies Sycophancy
**Authors**: Itai Shapira, Gerdus Benade, Ariel D. Procaccia
**arXiv**: [2602.01002](https://arxiv.org/abs/2602.01002)
**TL;DR**: Provides the first formal analysis of how preference-based post-training (RLHF) systematically amplifies sycophantic behavior in LLMs. Identifies an explicit amplification mechanism where optimizing for human preference ratings rewards agreeable-but-wrong responses over truthful disagreement.
**Why it matters**: Foundational result showing sycophancy is not just an empirical quirk but a structural consequence of RLHF — critical for anyone building aligned AI systems.

### 2. Configuration-to-Performance Scaling Law with Neural Ansatz (NCPL)
**Authors**: Not specified
**arXiv**: [2602.10300](https://arxiv.org/abs/2602.10300)
**TL;DR**: Proposes learning a Configuration-to-Performance Scaling Law (CPL) as a mapping from full training configuration (not just compute/data/params) to training performance. Parameterizes this with an LLM and fits it on diverse open-source pretraining logs, creating NCPL — a neural scaling law that generalizes across hyperparameter settings.
**Why it matters**: Moves scaling laws from idealized conditions to practical reality — you no longer need optimal hyperparameters to predict performance, which could save enormous compute in model development.

### 3. ViCA: Efficient Multimodal LLMs with Vision-Only Cross-Attention
**Authors**: Not specified
**arXiv**: [2602.07574](https://arxiv.org/abs/2602.07574)
**TL;DR**: Proposes a minimal MLLM architecture where visual tokens bypass all self-attention and feed-forward layers, interacting with text solely through sparse cross-attention at selected layers. Exploits the finding that visual attention is largely local and redundant in standard unified architectures.
**Why it matters**: Dramatically reduces the computational cost of multimodal LLMs while maintaining performance — a practical path to efficient vision-language models.

### 4. Reward Modeling for RL-Based LLM Reasoning: Design, Challenges, and Evaluation
**Authors**: Not specified
**arXiv**: [2602.09305](https://arxiv.org/abs/2602.09305)
**TL;DR**: Comprehensive survey systematically organizing recent advances in reward modeling for LLM reasoning. Covers process reward models, outcome reward models, and hybrid approaches, identifying persistent challenges in reward hacking, credit assignment, and generalization across reasoning domains.
**Why it matters**: Essential reference for the rapidly growing RL-for-reasoning field — synthesizes scattered advances into a coherent framework with clear open problems.

### 5. Why Agentic Theorem Prover Works: A Statistical Provability Theory
**Authors**: Sho Sonoda, Shunta Akiyama, Yuya Uezato
**arXiv**: [2602.10538](https://arxiv.org/abs/2602.10538)
**TL;DR**: Introduces "statistical provability" — the finite-horizon success probability of reaching a verified proof averaged over an instance distribution. Formalizes modern theorem-proving pipelines as time-bounded MDPs and derives bounds showing how proof complexity, retrieval, test-time search, and sequential complexity affect provability.
**Why it matters**: First rigorous theoretical explanation of why agentic theorem provers succeed empirically, providing principled guidance for designing better mathematical reasoning systems.
