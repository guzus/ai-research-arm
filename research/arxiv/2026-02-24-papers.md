# arXiv AI Research - 2026-02-24

## Highlights (Top 5 Must-Read)

### 1. MoSE: Mixture of Slimmable Experts for Efficient and Adaptive Language Models
**Authors**: Nurbek Tastan et al.
**arXiv**: https://arxiv.org/abs/2602.06154
**TL;DR**: Introduces a novel MoE architecture where each expert has a nested, slimmable structure that can be executed at variable widths. This enables conditional computation over both expert selection and expert capacity, allowing a single pretrained model to support a continuous spectrum of accuracy-compute trade-offs at inference time.
**Why it matters**: Shifts the Pareto frontier for MoE efficiency — matches standard MoE at full width while achieving comparable performance with significantly fewer FLOPs at reduced widths, offering a practical path to adaptive inference.

### 2. Training Large Reasoning Models Efficiently via Progressive Thought Encoding
**Authors**: Zeliang Zhang, Xiaodong Liu et al. (Microsoft Research)
**arXiv**: https://arxiv.org/abs/2602.16839
**TL;DR**: Proposes a parameter-efficient fine-tuning method that progressively encodes intermediate reasoning into fixed-size vector representations, eliminating the need to backpropagate through full-cache rollouts. Achieves +19.3% over LoRA-based fine-tuning and +29.9% over base LRMs on mathematical benchmarks.
**Why it matters**: Solves a critical bottleneck for training reasoning models — RL training no longer requires expensive long autoregressive rollouts, making reasoning model training accessible at lower memory budgets.

### 3. FeatureBench: Benchmarking Agentic Coding for Complex Feature Development
**Authors**: Qixing Zhou, Jiacheng Zhang, Haiyang Wang et al.
**arXiv**: https://arxiv.org/abs/2602.10975
**TL;DR**: Introduces a 200-task benchmark from 24 open-source repos that evaluates agentic coding on feature-level (not just bug-fix) development. Claude Code + Opus 4.5 resolves only 11.0% and Codex + GPT-5.1-Codex only 12.5% of tasks, compared to 74.4% on SWE-bench for the same models.
**Why it matters**: Accepted at ICLR 2026 — reveals that current agentic coding systems remain far from solving real-world feature development, exposing a major gap beyond bug-fixing benchmarks.

### 4. SafeNeuron: Neuron-Level Safety Alignment for Large Language Models
**Authors**: Zhaoxin Wang et al.
**arXiv**: https://arxiv.org/abs/2602.12158
**TL;DR**: Identifies safety-related neurons, freezes them during preference optimization, and forces the model to construct redundant safety representations across the network. Significantly improves robustness against neuron pruning attacks while preserving general capabilities.
**Why it matters**: Addresses a critical vulnerability — current safety alignment is concentrated in sparse neuron subsets and easily bypassed; SafeNeuron distributes safety throughout the network.

### 5. LLM-WikiRace: Benchmarking Long-term Planning and Reasoning over Real-World Knowledge Graphs
**Authors**: Juliusz Ziomek, William Bankes, Lorenz Wolf, Shyam Sundhar Ramesh, Xiaohang Tang, Ilija Bogunovic
**arXiv**: https://arxiv.org/abs/2602.16902
**TL;DR**: Models must navigate Wikipedia hyperlinks from source to target page, requiring look-ahead planning and real-world knowledge reasoning. Gemini-3, GPT-5, and Claude Opus 4.5 achieve superhuman performance on easy tasks, but the best model (Gemini-3) succeeds on only 23% of hard games.
**Why it matters**: Demonstrates that even frontier models with strong world knowledge still struggle with long-horizon planning — planning capability, not knowledge, is the bottleneck.
