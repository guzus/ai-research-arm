## Highlights (Top 5 Must-Read)

### 1. Audio-Visual Flamingo: Open Audio-Visual Intelligence for Long and Complex Videos
**Authors**: Sreyan Ghosh, Arushi Goel, Kaousheik Jayakumar, et al. (NVIDIA)
**arXiv**: https://arxiv.org/abs/2607.16107v1
**TL;DR**: A large open audio-visual model trained with curriculum learning and explicit temporal grounding for long-form video understanding, jointly reasoning over sound and vision rather than treating audio as a bolt-on modality.
**Why it matters**: Most "multimodal" LLM releases are vision-text; a strong open audio-visual model that handles long video closes a real capability gap for anyone building agents that watch and listen.

### 2. Loop the Loopies!
**Authors**: Zitian Gao, Yilong Chen, Yihao Xiao, et al.
**arXiv**: https://arxiv.org/abs/2607.16051v1
**TL;DR**: Introduces Loopie, a looped Transformer combined with Mixture-of-Experts that reportedly reaches gold-medal-level performance on IMO and IPhO problem sets by reusing depth through recurrence instead of scaling parameter count.
**Why it matters**: If the result holds up, it's evidence that architectural recurrence — not just more parameters or more RL — can buy competition-grade reasoning, which changes the cost calculus for reasoning models.

### 3. Frontier Language Models Struggle to Copy: Text Can Be Better Viewed in 2D
**Authors**: Haodong Wen, Yiran Zhang, Yingfa Chen, Kaifeng Lyu
**arXiv**: https://arxiv.org/abs/2607.16072v1
**TL;DR**: Shows that even frontier LLMs fail at exact long-span copying because of positional-encoding biases baked into 1D RoPE, and proposes 2D-RoPE, which places text on a grid instead of a line so the model generalizes to far longer copy spans.
**Why it matters**: A basic, surprising failure mode in models otherwise considered strong at "in-context" tasks, with a concrete architectural fix rather than a prompting workaround.

### 4. Understanding Reasoning from Pretraining to Post-Training
**Authors**: Jingyan Shen, Ang Li, Salman Rahman, et al.
**arXiv**: https://arxiv.org/abs/2607.16097v1
**TL;DR**: Uses chess and math as controlled testbeds to show pretraining loss predicts post-RL performance, and that RL mostly amplifies moves the base model could already make on easy problems while surfacing latent correct moves on harder ones.
**Why it matters**: Gives a mechanistic account of what RL post-training is actually doing to a base model's reasoning, useful for anyone deciding where to spend compute — more pretraining vs. more RL.

### 5. When Do Multi-Agent Systems Help? An Information Bottleneck Perspective
**Authors**: Wendi Yu, Lianhao Zhou, Xiangjue Dong, et al.
**arXiv**: https://arxiv.org/abs/2607.16133v1
**TL;DR**: A theoretical, information-theoretic framework showing multi-agent systems outperform a single strong agent mainly when communication bandwidth constraints force useful compression of context, not simply from having more agents.
**Why it matters**: Cuts against the reflexive "add more agents" instinct in agent-system design with a principled account of when decomposition actually helps.
