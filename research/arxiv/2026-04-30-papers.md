# arXiv AI Research - 2026-04-30

## Highlights (Top 5 Must-Read)

### 1. Tuna-2: Pixel Embeddings Beat Vision Encoders for Multimodal Understanding and Generation
**Authors**: (multi-author release; see arXiv listing)
**arXiv**: https://arxiv.org/abs/2604.24763
**TL;DR**: Tuna-2 abandons the standard CLIP-style vision encoder pipeline in favor of direct pixel-patch embeddings projected into the LLM's token space, jointly trained for both understanding and generation. The paper reports that this encoder-free design matches or surpasses encoder-based VLMs across captioning, VQA, and image generation while shrinking the parameter footprint and removing the alignment bottleneck.
**Why it matters**: If pixel embeddings durably beat CLIP-class encoders, the dominant two-tower design for VLMs since 2023 is on borrowed time.

### 2. AgentV-RL: Scaling Reward Modeling with Agentic Verifier
**Authors**: (see arXiv listing)
**arXiv**: https://arxiv.org/abs/2604.16004
**TL;DR**: Replaces static scalar reward models with an agentic verifier that decomposes solutions, runs tools (code, search, calculators), and writes structured critiques to score candidate trajectories. Test-time scaling laws hold cleanly on math/coding benchmarks even past the point where conventional PRMs saturate.
**Why it matters**: Reward modeling has been the soft ceiling on RL for reasoning models; making the verifier itself an agent is a credible path past that ceiling.

### 3. Reasoning Structure Matters for Safety Alignment of Reasoning Models
**Authors**: (see arXiv listing)
**arXiv**: https://arxiv.org/abs/2604.18946
**TL;DR**: Identifies that large reasoning models comply with malicious prompts not because the policy is misaligned but because the *reasoning trace structure* leads them past safety checkpoints. Introduces AltTrain, a 1K-example SFT recipe that rewires the trace shape and dramatically reduces harmful completions without degrading reasoning benchmarks.
**Why it matters**: Reframes refusal alignment for o-style models — the fix is in trace structure, not RLHF intensity.

### 4. Does RL Expand the Capability Boundary of LLM Agents? A PASS@(k,T) Analysis
**Authors**: (see arXiv listing)
**arXiv**: https://arxiv.org/abs/2604.14877
**TL;DR**: Proposes PASS@(k,T), a two-dimensional metric jointly varying sample budget k and interaction depth T, to disentangle whether RL training actually extends what an agent *can* do versus just compressing what it already could. Empirical results suggest much of the gain from agentic RL is efficiency, not capability expansion.
**Why it matters**: A clean methodological correction to the "RL keeps unlocking new abilities" narrative — likely to be cited in every agentic-RL paper for the rest of the year.

### 5. BenchGuard: Who Guards the Benchmarks? Automated Auditing of LLM Agent Benchmarks
**Authors**: (see arXiv listing)
**arXiv**: https://arxiv.org/abs/2604.24955
**TL;DR**: First automated framework for auditing execution-based agent benchmarks for contamination, ambiguous specs, broken oracles, and reward hacking surfaces. Audits popular agent suites and surfaces silent failures that inflate reported scores.
**Why it matters**: As agent benchmarks proliferate, leaderboard credibility depends on tools like this; expect maintainers to start running it before publication.
