## Highlights (Top 5 Must-Read)

### 1. Program-as-Weights (PAW): A Programming Paradigm for Fuzzy Functions
**Authors**: Wentao Zhang, Liliana Hotsko, Woojeong Kim, Pengyu Nie, Stuart Shieber, Yuntian Deng
**arXiv**: https://arxiv.org/abs/2607.02512
**TL;DR**: Introduces "fuzzy-function programming" — compiling a natural-language function spec into a compact, locally-executable neural artifact instead of calling an LLM per input. A 4B compiler trained on a new 10M-example dataset (FuzzyBench) emits parameter-efficient adapters for a frozen 0.6B Qwen3 interpreter, which then matches the performance of directly prompting Qwen3-32B while using roughly 1/50th the inference memory and running at 30 tokens/s on a MacBook M3.
**Why it matters**: Reframes the foundation model from a per-input problem solver into a one-time tool builder, turning expensive repeated LLM calls into cheap, reusable, offline artifacts — a genuinely different deployment pattern for LLM capability.

### 2. A rubric-based controlled comparison of frontier language models on expert-authored clinical reasoning tasks
**Authors**: Samiha A. Ismail, Fan X. Chen, Ali Merali
**arXiv**: https://arxiv.org/abs/2607.02175
**TL;DR**: Built five clinician-authored clinical scenarios with 184 MECE weighted-rubric criteria and tested GPT 5.4, Claude Opus 4.7, and Gemini 3.1 Pro. Mean rubric pass rates were only 0.47/0.39/0.37 — and critically, the highest-stakes weight-5 criteria passed at just 32-42% versus 80-90% for low-stakes criteria; 56 of 108 critical criteria were satisfied by no model at all.
**Why it matters**: A small but rigorous benchmark showing frontier models get the easy parts of clinical reasoning right and the parts that actually matter wrong — a sharper signal than aggregate scores on saturated multiple-choice medical benchmarks.

### 3. SkillFuzz: Fuzzing Skill Composition for Implicit Intents Discovery in Open Skill Marketplaces
**Authors**: Jinwei Hu, Yi Dong, Youcheng Sun, Xiaowei Huang
**arXiv**: https://arxiv.org/abs/2607.02345
**TL;DR**: Individually-benign LLM-agent "skills" (reusable natural-language instruction docs) can combine into unintended "implicit intents" that per-skill marketplace audits miss. SkillFuzz treats skill compositions as a fuzzing target, using contract-guided Monte Carlo Tree Search over planning artifacts as an execution-free oracle; it surfaces 1,000+ distinct implicit intents under a fixed query budget and confirms 80%+ of the highest-risk flagged compositions at execution time.
**Why it matters**: A concrete security methodology for exactly the emerging risk surface of skill/plugin marketplaces that agentic pipelines increasingly rely on.

### 4. Ask the Right Comparison: Bias-Aware Bayesian Active Top-k Ranking with LLM Judges
**Authors**: Jian Xu, Delu Zeng, John Paisley, Qibin Zhao
**arXiv**: https://arxiv.org/abs/2607.02104
**TL;DR**: Casts LLM-judge ranking as Bayesian inference with explicit judge-specific bias covariates (verbosity, position) under a shrinkage prior, plus a top-k-aware active acquisition rule. Tested across 16 real LLM judges (GPT, Gemini, DeepSeek, Llama, Phi-4, and Claude Haiku/Sonnet/Opus): naive vote aggregation plateaus at a wrong top-k on biased judges regardless of budget, while the bias-aware model lifts recall from ~0.5-0.6 to 0.84-1.0 for cheap/mid-tier judges.
**Why it matters**: A practical, budget-efficient fix for one of the most common LLM-as-judge failure modes (verbosity/position bias), validated across a genuinely broad judge roster.

### 5. Learning to Move Before Learning to Do: Task-Agnostic pretraining for VLAs
**Authors**: Junhao Shi, Siyin Wang, Xiaopeng Yu, Li Ji, Jingjing Gong, Xipeng Qiu
**arXiv**: https://arxiv.org/abs/2607.02466
**TL;DR**: Argues Vision-Language-Action models conflate "how to move" (physical competence) with "what to do" (semantic alignment) — only the latter needs language supervision. Task-Agnostic Pretraining (TAP) first learns motor priors from cheap unlabeled interaction data via a self-supervised inverse-dynamics objective, then grounds them in language with minimal expert data; on SIMPLER it matches models trained on 1M+ expert trajectories using orders of magnitude less labeled data (+10% absolute over standard behavior cloning), and on a real WidowX arm it retains 25% success under camera perturbations where internet-scale baselines collapse to 0%.
**Why it matters**: A data-efficiency argument backed by real-robot evidence that could meaningfully cut the expert-demonstration bottleneck holding back VLA scaling.
