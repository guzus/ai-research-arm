# arXiv AI Research - 2026-07-17

## Highlights (Top 5 Must-Read)

### 1. Pretraining Data Can Be Poisoned through Computational Propaganda
**Authors**: Victoria Graf, Hannaneh Hajishirzi, Noah A. Smith, David Kohlbrenner, Kyle Lo
**arXiv**: https://arxiv.org/abs/2607.15267v1
**TL;DR**: Shows that pretraining data poisoning is feasible at web scale through public discussion interfaces (forums, comment sections) rather than requiring privileged access to crawl pipelines. Introduces HalfLife, a method for estimating how much adversarial content from these interfaces survives into web-crawled LM training corpora.
**Why it matters**: Establishes third-party webpage content — not just adversary-controlled domains — as a concrete, low-cost attack vector against frontier pretraining data, with direct implications for corpus curation and provenance filtering.

### 2. RoboTTT: Context Scaling for Robot Policies
**Authors**: Yunfan Jiang, Yevgen Chebotar, Ruijie Zheng, Fengyuan Hu, Yunhao Ge, Jimmy Wu, Tianyuan Dai, Scott Reed, Li Fei-Fei, Yuke Zhu, Linxi "Jim" Fan
**arXiv**: https://arxiv.org/abs/2607.15275v1
**TL;DR**: Integrates Test-Time Training into Vision-Language-Action policies to scale visuomotor context to 8K timesteps — three orders of magnitude beyond prior policies — without increasing inference latency, enabling one-shot in-context imitation from human video and on-the-fly policy improvement. Reports an 87% gain over single-step baselines and a completed five-minute, ten-stage assembly task.
**Why it matters**: Positions context length as a new, largely untapped scaling axis for robot foundation models, alongside parameter count and data volume.

### 3. In-Place Tokenizer Expansion for Pre-trained LLMs
**Authors**: Jimmy T. H. Smith, Tarek Dakhran, Alberto Cabrera, Simon S. Lee, Paul Pak, Aditya Tadimeti, Tim Seyde, Maxime Labonne, Alexander Amini, Mathias Lechner
**arXiv**: https://arxiv.org/abs/2607.15232v1
**TL;DR**: A recipe for upgrading a pre-trained model's tokenizer after training priorities shift: continuing BPE merges on target-language corpora, initializing new embedding rows as means of source sub-tokens, then a two-stage (embedding-only, then full-model) continued pretraining. Applied to LFM2-8B, encodes Hindi/Vietnamese in 2.4–2.6x fewer tokens with an estimated 2.2–3.7x per-character decode speedup.
**Why it matters**: A practical fix for a real deployment pain point — tokenizer inefficiency on underserved languages discovered only after a model is already trained and shipped.

### 4. Can We Trust Item Response Theory for AI Evaluation?
**Authors**: Han Jiang, Sunbeom Kwon, Jinwen Luo, Ziang Xiao, Susu Zhang
**arXiv**: https://arxiv.org/abs/2607.15190v1
**TL;DR**: Stress-tests IRT-based benchmark scoring — increasingly used to compare AI models — under the regime mismatch typical of AI evals (few models, many items, skewed capability distributions). Across 18,000 simulated conditions and four estimators, classical estimators become infeasible at scale while scalable estimators produce unreliable inferences with small or non-normal model sets.
**Why it matters**: A methodological warning aimed squarely at the benchmark-design community: a popular psychometric tool may be silently misapplied to the exact conditions AI evaluation creates.

### 5. Partition, Prompt, Aggregate: Statistical Self-Consistency in Language Models
**Authors**: Patrik Wolf, Thomas Kleine Buening, Andreas Krause, Celestine Mendler-Dünner
**arXiv**: https://arxiv.org/abs/2607.15277v1
**TL;DR**: Tests whether LLM population estimates obey the law of total probability by partitioning a population into finer subpopulations and aggregating sub-estimates back up. Finds widespread violations — the "macro fallacy" — where fine-grained subpopulation estimates align better with human reference data than the model's own direct population-level estimate.
**Why it matters**: Shows models often hold the right knowledge at a granular level but fail to propagate it into consistent aggregate judgments, a distinct failure mode from simple factual error that matters for any LLM used for estimation or survey-style reasoning.
