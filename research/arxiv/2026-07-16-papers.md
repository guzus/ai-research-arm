# arXiv AI Research - 2026-07-16

## Highlights (Top 5 Must-Read)

### 1. TRACE: Turn-level Reward Assignment via Credit Estimation for Long-Horizon Agents
**Authors**: Leitian Tao, Baolin Peng, Wenlin Yao, Tao Ge, Hao Cheng, Mike Hang Wang, Jianfeng Gao, Sharon Li
**arXiv**: https://arxiv.org/abs/2607.13988v1
**TL;DR**: Introduces a dense credit-assignment method for agentic RL that represents rollouts as state transitions at tool-call boundaries rather than treating the whole trajectory as one sparse-reward unit. On the closed-web BrowseComp-Plus benchmark, pure RL with TRACE lifted Qwen3-4B from 7.2 to 35.6 and Qwen3-30B-A3B from 8.4 to 42.6.
**Why it matters**: Turn-level credit assignment is a core bottleneck for training reliable long-horizon tool-using agents, and this is one of the largest reported RL gains on a search-agent benchmark this cycle.

### 2. VideoRAE: Taming Video Foundation Models for Generative Modeling via Representation Autoencoders
**Authors**: Zhihao Xie, Junfeng Wu, Xinting Hu, Junchao Huang, Li Jiang
**arXiv**: https://arxiv.org/abs/2607.14088v1
**TL;DR**: Builds a representation autoencoder on top of frozen video foundation models, supporting both continuous latents (for diffusion transformers) and discrete multi-codebook tokens (for autoregressive generators). Reports state-of-the-art class-to-video gFVD scores of 40 (AR) and 93 (DiT) on UCF-101, converging roughly 5x faster than competing baselines.
**Why it matters**: Reusing frozen video foundation-model representations as a shared latent space for both AR and diffusion video generators is a notable architectural unification with a large efficiency win.

### 3. Transforming Rank: How Architecture Navigates the Spectral Pathologies of Depth
**Authors**: Katie Everett
**arXiv**: https://arxiv.org/abs/2607.14018v1
**TL;DR**: Analyzes how Transformer feedforward components, skip connections, and normalization jointly preserve (or destroy) gradient rank as depth grows, unifying the normalization-placement literature and explaining why rank collapses under Post-Norm but plateaus under Pre-Norm.
**Why it matters**: A first-principles account of a scaling pathology that has driven real architecture decisions (Pre- vs Post-Norm) across every major LLM training stack.

### 4. Do Agent Optimizers Compound? A Continual-Learning Evaluation on Terminal-Bench 2.0
**Authors**: Wenxiao Wang, Priyatham Kattakinda, Soheil Feizi
**arXiv**: https://arxiv.org/abs/2607.14004v1
**TL;DR**: Runs a continual-learning evaluation of agent-optimization methods (prompt/workflow optimizers) across repeated rounds, finding gains only compound when the loop includes explicit regression control. Their method, RELAI-VCL, reaches a 76.4% lifelong average pass rate versus 66.0% for GEPA and 64.6% for a meta-harness baseline.
**Why it matters**: Directly tests a widely-assumed premise — that iterative agent self-improvement loops keep paying off — and shows it's false without regression guards, which is an important methodological warning for anyone building self-improving agent pipelines.

### 5. Deep Interaction: An Efficient Human-AI Interaction Method for Large Reasoning Models
**Authors**: Hefeng Zhou, Jinxuan Zhang, Jiong Lou, Yuxin Liu, Chaochao Lu, Jingjing Qu, Jie Li
**arXiv**: https://arxiv.org/abs/2607.14049v1
**TL;DR**: Lets users directly edit specific erroneous steps within a chain-of-thought while preserving the correct steps around them, instead of forcing a full regeneration. Reports over 25% improvement in correction success rate and roughly 40% lower token usage on STEM reasoning tasks.
**Why it matters**: A practical, low-overhead interaction pattern for human-in-the-loop correction of reasoning models that could meaningfully cut inference cost in agentic and tutoring settings.
