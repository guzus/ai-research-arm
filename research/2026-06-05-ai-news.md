## Breaking News

### **Bipartisan "Great American Artificial Intelligence Act" Discussion Draft Lands In The House — Reps. Obernolte (R-CA) & Trahan (D-MA), Joined By Four Co-Sponsors, Release A 269-Page Text That Would Impose Mandatory Catastrophic-Risk Plans, Safety-Incident Reporting And Independent Audits On Frontier Developers While Preempting State Laws That "Specifically Regulate The Development" Of AI Models For Three Years (Use/Deployment Laws Left To States); Authorizes $100M/yr For A Center For AI Standards And Innovation And Codifies The NAIRR**

**Date:** Thursday, June 4, 2026 | **Sources:** [Roll Call — Bipartisan AI draft proposes three-year preemption of state laws](https://rollcall.com/2026/06/04/bipartisan-ai-draft-proposes-three-year-preemption-of-state-laws/), [The Hill — House lawmakers introduce draft for national AI framework](https://thehill.com/policy/technology/5909898-national-ai-framework-draft/), [Axios — What's inside the House draft bill to regulate AI](https://www.axios.com/2026/06/04/house-draft-bill-regulate-ai), [Nextgov/FCW — Lawmakers propose AI framework that would preempt state laws for 3 years](https://www.nextgov.com/artificial-intelligence/2026/06/lawmakers-propose-ai-framework-would-preempt-state-laws-3-years/413975/), [FedScoop — Bipartisan 'Great American AI Act' draft proposes new federal AI governance framework](https://fedscoop.com/bipartisan-great-american-ai-act-draft-proposes-new-federal-ai-governance-framework/), [Obernolte press release](https://obernolte.house.gov/media/press-releases/obernolte-trahan-release-discussion-draft-great-american-ai-act), [Trahan press release](https://trahan.house.gov/news/documentsingle.aspx?DocumentID=3783), [PYMNTS — House Members Propose Replacing State AI Laws With National Standard](https://www.pymnts.com/artificial-intelligence-2/2026/house-members-propose-replacing-state-ai-laws-with-national-standard/)

- **Who & what:** Reps. **Jay Obernolte (R-CA)** and **Lori Trahan (D-MA)** unveiled the long-expected framework as a **discussion draft** (released for stakeholder/public feedback before formal introduction), joined by **Suhas Subramanyam (D-VA), Scott Franklin (R-FL), Scott Peters (D-CA) and Erin Houchin (R-IN)**. The text runs **269 pages** and lands days after Trump's June 2 EO establishing *voluntary* federal frontier-model reviews.
- **Four pillars:** (1) **frontier-AI model governance**; (2) **workforce-impact data collection** (directs Census Bureau + BLS to add AI use/adoption questions to federal surveys); (3) **cybersecurity** fortification; (4) **R&D** — authorizes **$100M/fiscal year for a Center for AI Standards and Innovation** and formally **codifies the National AI Research Resource (NAIRR)** in NSF.
- **Mandatory duties on large frontier developers:** publish and follow **catastrophic-risk management plans**, **report serious/critical safety incidents** to the government, and face **penalties for noncompliance**; compliance verified via **independent third-party audits**. Also adds penalties for using AI to **impersonate government officials**.
- **State preemption (the fight):** Preempts state laws "specifically regulating the **development**" of an AI model, with a **three-year sunset**. Crucially, preemption **does not** reach laws on the **use or deployment** of AI — states keep authority over how systems are used within their borders, but lose authority over how models are built.
- **Reaction split immediately:** **ITI's** Jason Oxman thanked the sponsors for "bipartisan leadership"; **Public Citizen** called it "a disastrous proposal that Big Tech is celebrating," **Americans for Responsible Innovation's** Brad Carson called preemption a "generational mistake," and flight-attendant/labor unions urged Congress to reject it.

**Read-through:** This is the substantive federal counterweight to a year of state-by-state AI rulemaking (Colorado AI Act effective June 30; California/NY proposals) and the *legislative* complement to Trump's voluntary EO. The structure — **mandatory frontier-safety duties traded for a three-year development-preemption window** — is a deliberate grand bargain: it gives labs the single national standard they've lobbied for while handing safety advocates audit/incident-reporting teeth the EO lacked. The development-vs-deployment line is the load-bearing compromise (and the likely battleground): it preserves state consumer/worker/child-safety enforcement on *uses* while federalizing the *building* of frontier models. As a discussion draft it isn't law, but it reframes the 2026 preemption fight from "moratorium yes/no" (the failed 2025 budget-bill rider) to "what do labs owe in exchange."
