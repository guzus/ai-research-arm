### Former Google Engineer Convicted in First-Ever US AI Espionage Case
**Date:** January 30, 2026

A federal jury in San Francisco convicted former Google software engineer Linwei Ding (also known as Leon Ding), 38, on seven counts of economic espionage and seven counts of theft of trade secrets — marking the first conviction on AI-related economic espionage charges in U.S. history, according to the Department of Justice.

**What Was Stolen:**
- More than 2,000 pages of confidential information related to Google's Tensor Processing Units (TPUs), Graphics Processing Units (GPUs), and SmartNIC network interface cards
- Ding accessed and exfiltrated the files between May 2022 and April 2023 while still employed at Google
- He copied data from Google source files into Apple Notes on his company-issued MacBook, converted them to PDFs, and uploaded them to his personal Google Cloud account to evade detection

**China Connections:**
- While still at Google, Ding founded Shanghai Zhisuan Technologies Co. in China and told investors the startup could replicate Google's AI supercomputing technology
- He applied to a Shanghai-based "talent" program sponsored by the PRC government aimed at growing China's AI industry
- Trial evidence showed Ding intended to benefit two entities controlled by the Chinese government by assisting with AI supercomputer development and custom machine learning chip R&D

**How He Was Caught:**
- Ding asked a former intern to swipe his badge at Google's offices while he was in China
- His scheme unraveled in late 2023 when Google locked him out after discovering he gave a public presentation in China to investors about his startup
- FBI executed a search warrant at his home in January 2024; he was arrested two months later

**Sentencing:**
- Faces up to 10 years per count of trade secret theft and 15 years per count of economic espionage (14 counts total)
- Released pending sentencing; next court appearance February 3, 2026

**Reactions:**
- FBI Assistant Director Roman Rozhavsky: "Linwei Ding betrayed both the U.S. and his employer by stealing trade secrets about Google's AI technology on behalf of China's government."
- Google VP Lee-Anne Mulholland: "We're grateful to the jury for making sure justice was served today, sending a clear message that stealing trade secrets has serious consequences."

### NHTSA Investigates Waymo After Robotaxi Strikes Child Near Elementary School
**Date:** January 29-30, 2026

The National Highway Traffic Safety Administration opened a formal investigation into Waymo after one of its driverless vehicles struck a child near an elementary school in Santa Monica, California on January 23.

**The Incident:**
- Occurred during school drop-off hours within two blocks of an elementary school
- A child ran across the street from behind a double-parked SUV and was struck by the Waymo vehicle
- The vehicle was operating autonomously with no human safety supervisor (5th Generation ADS)
- The child sustained minor injuries, stood up immediately, and walked to the sidewalk

**Waymo's Response:**
- Claims its technology "immediately detected the individual as soon as they began to emerge from behind the stopped vehicle"
- Vehicle braked from approximately 17 mph to under 6 mph before contact
- Waymo argued a fully attentive human driver would have made contact at 14 mph

**NHTSA Investigation Scope:**
- Whether Waymo exercised "appropriate caution" given proximity to school during drop-off hours
- Analysis of "intended behavior" of Waymo vehicles in school zones
- Post-impact response assessment

**Broader Waymo Safety Concerns:**
- NTSB separately opened investigation after Waymo robotaxis illegally passed stopped school buses in Austin, Texas at least 19 times since the school year started
- Waymo recalled 3,000+ vehicles in December to fix software causing vehicles to drive past stopped school buses
- Separate January 25 incident in Los Angeles where a Waymo vehicle (in manual mode) struck parked cars

### Google DeepMind Launches Project Genie — Interactive AI World Model
**Date:** January 29, 2026

Google DeepMind released Project Genie, an experimental prototype built on the Genie 3 world model that generates explorable, interactive 3D environments from text or image prompts in real time — available to Google AI Ultra subscribers ($250/month) in the U.S.

**How It Works:**
- Genie 3 generates photorealistic environments that respond to user input in real time
- Unlike video generators, creates explorable worlds users can navigate freely
- Generates frames auto-regressively based on prompts and ongoing user interactions
- Runs at 20-24 frames per second at 720p resolution
- Environments remain consistent for several minutes; memory recalls changes for up to a minute
- Generations limited to 60 seconds per session

**Three Modes:**
1. **World Sketching** — draw and describe environments
2. **Exploration** — navigate generated worlds in first-person, third-person, or isometric view
3. **Remixing** — regenerate variations using revised prompts

**Evolution:**
- Genie 1: demonstrated concept of generating game-like environments (limited interactivity)
- Genie 2: improved visual quality (still primarily a video generator)
- Genie 3: true real-time interaction with free navigation and instant response to actions

**Potential Applications:**
- Interactive media and gaming prototyping
- Educational environments (e.g., exploring historical eras)
- Training autonomous vehicles in simulated scenarios
- Stepping stone toward AI agents capable of reasoning in physical environments
