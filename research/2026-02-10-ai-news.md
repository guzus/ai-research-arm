### OpenAI Begins Testing Ads in ChatGPT for Free and Go Users
**Date:** February 9–10, 2026

OpenAI officially launched ad testing in ChatGPT, marking a major strategic shift for the company and drawing immediate criticism from rivals.

**Key Details:**
- Ads appear at the **bottom of answers** in ChatGPT when there's a relevant sponsored product or service based on the user's current conversation
- Testing is limited to **logged-in adult users on Free and Go tiers** in the U.S.
- **Plus, Pro, Business, Enterprise, and Education tiers** will not see ads
- Ad targeting uses the topic of the conversation, past chats, and past interactions with ads
- Advertisers do **not** have access to chats, chat history, memories, or personal details — they only receive aggregate performance data (views/clicks)
- Users can dismiss any ad and provide feedback on why

**Rival Response:**
Anthropic fired back immediately, emphasizing that it makes money through subscriptions and business contracts rather than ads, positioning Claude as the privacy-respecting alternative. Google DeepMind CEO Demis Hassabis told TechCrunch he was "surprised" OpenAI is "rushing forward" with ads.

### Melius Research Downgrades Microsoft: "AI Is Eating Microsoft"
**Date:** February 9, 2026

Melius Research downgraded Microsoft to Hold from Buy, issuing a report titled "Satya Lost the AI Narrative" that warns Microsoft's Copilot strategy is failing and the company risks being disrupted by the very AI revolution it helped fund.

**Key Arguments:**
- Analyst **Ben Reitzes** set a $430 price target, warning that Microsoft's per-seat Copilot pricing model is **vulnerable to disruption** by AI agent technologies
- Microsoft may need to **give Copilot away for free** just to stay relevant — hurting growth and margins in its most profitable Productivity segment
- Free cash flow is expected to **fall vs. Wall Street estimates** as AI capex spending escalates
- This is Microsoft's **second downgrade** in recent weeks, following similar concerns from other analysts

**Why It Matters:**
The downgrade signals growing Wall Street skepticism that Microsoft's massive AI investments ($145B+ capex in 2026) are translating into competitive AI products, even as rivals like Anthropic and OpenAI ship increasingly capable tools.

### OpenAI Launches GPT-5.3-Codex — First Model Rated "High" for Cybersecurity Risk
**Date:** February 5, 2026

OpenAI released GPT-5.3-Codex, its most capable agentic coding model, which combines GPT-5.2-Codex and GPT-5.2 training stacks. Notably, it is the first model OpenAI has classified as "High" for cybersecurity under its Preparedness Framework — triggering unprecedented safety controls.

**Key Capabilities:**
- **25% faster** than GPT-5.2-Codex while setting new highs on key benchmarks
- Combines frontier coding performance with reasoning and general-purpose intelligence
- First model that was **instrumental in creating itself** — the Codex team used early versions to debug training, manage deployment, and diagnose test results
- Available on paid ChatGPT plans (app, CLI, IDE extension, web); **API access delayed** due to cybersecurity concerns

**Cybersecurity Classification:**
- First OpenAI model to hit **"High" capability** in the Cybersecurity domain
- Under OpenAI's framework, "High" means a model that "removes existing bottlenecks to scaling cyber operations" — potentially automating end-to-end attacks against hardened targets or discovering exploitable vulnerabilities
- OpenAI deployed its **most comprehensive cybersecurity safety stack** to date, including safety training, automated monitoring, trusted access controls, and threat intelligence pipelines
- OpenAI stressed it has no "definitive evidence" the model can fully automate cyberattacks, but is taking a cautious approach

### OpenAI Launches Frontier — Enterprise AI Agent Platform
**Date:** February 5, 2026

Alongside GPT-5.3-Codex, OpenAI launched Frontier, a new enterprise platform for building, deploying, and managing AI agents across business systems.

**Key Features:**
- **Business Context** connects enterprise systems — data warehouses, CRM, internal apps — so AI agents work with the same information as employees
- **Agent identities** allow scoped access so agents act on behalf of users without over-permissioning
- Compatible with agents from **OpenAI, third-parties (Google, Microsoft, Anthropic), and custom-built** solutions
- Initial customers include **Uber, State Farm, Intuit, and Thermo Fisher Scientific**

**Strategic Context:**
Enterprise customers account for roughly 40% of OpenAI's business, with the company targeting 50%. The Frontier platform positions OpenAI to compete directly with Anthropic's enterprise tools that triggered the SaaS selloff earlier in the week.

JPMorgan strategists published a major note arguing that the historic software stock collapse — triggered by AI disruption fears — has overshot and created buying opportunities.

**By the Numbers:**
- Software sector has suffered its **largest non-recessionary 12-month drawdown in 30+ years** (-34%)
- Approximately **$2 trillion** wiped from software market cap from peak
- Software's weight in the S&P 500 fell from **12.0% to 8.4%**

**JPMorgan's Argument:**
- Software stocks are being **"sentenced before trial"** — the market is pricing in unrealistic near-term AI disruption
- Enterprise software benefits from **entrenched advantages**: high switching costs, multi-year contracts, and additive AI integration rather than substitution
- The firm recommends rotating into high-quality, AI-resilient names like **Microsoft (MSFT)** and **CrowdStrike (CRWD)**

Despite the software sector collapse, the AI bull market remains resilient: hyperscalers' 2026 capex is up 24% ($660B planned), S&P 500 EPS is 12% higher YoY, and S&P 500 futures were up 0.18% on Monday morning.

## Research & Privacy

### Anthropic's "Anonymous" Interviewer Tool De-Anonymized by Northeastern Researcher
**Date:** February 10, 2026

A Northeastern University professor demonstrated that Anthropic's Interviewer tool — which published 1,250 "anonymized" interviews — can be partially de-anonymized using publicly available LLMs, raising questions about AI-conducted research privacy.

**Key Findings:**
- **Tianshi Li**, assistant professor of computer science, focused on the scientist subset of interviews
- From 24 interviews that mentioned specific scientific studies, she was able to **de-anonymize 25%** — associating interviews with specific papers and, in some cases, identifying the specific scientist
- Li described the process as **"surprisingly easy"** using a publicly available LLM
- She noted that "people can really be easily influenced by the way AI is asking a question" and may share identifying details gradually without realizing it

**Anthropic's Response:**
A spokesperson stated that interviewees were "external professionals who first agreed to participate and were informed that their responses to the interview questions would be made publicly available."
