## Breaking News

### OpenAI Amends Pentagon Contract Amid Backlash Over Surveillance Concerns
**Date:** March 3, 2026 | **Sources:** [Business Insider](https://www.businessinsider.com/openai-amending-contract-with-pentagon-amid-backlash-mass-surveillance-anthropic-2026-3), [Fortune](https://fortune.com/2026/03/03/sam-altman-openai-pentagon-renegotiating-deal-anthropic/)

OpenAI CEO Sam Altman posted an internal memo on X acknowledging the company's Pentagon deal announced on Feb 28 was "rushed" and "opportunistic." OpenAI is now amending the contract to explicitly prohibit domestic surveillance of US persons (consistent with the Fourth Amendment, FISA, and the National Security Act) and barring use by defense intelligence agencies such as the NSA, National Geospatial Intelligence Agency, and Defense Intelligence Agency without a separate contract amendment. The amendments also add restrictions on commercially purchased data such as cell phone location records and fitness app information. Legal experts have questioned how enforceable these new restrictions will be in practice.

**Date:** March 2, 2026 | **Sources:** [Fortune](https://fortune.com/2026/03/02/anthropic-claude-dario-amodei-number-one-app-store-openai-chatgpt-sam-altman-department-war/), [Euronews](https://www.euronews.com/next/2026/03/02/cancel-chatgpt-ai-boycott-surges-after-openai-pentagon-military-deal), [The Independent](https://www.the-independent.com/tech/cancel-chatgpt-ai-war-claude-anthropic-b2930007.html)

### U.S. Supreme Court Refuses to Hear AI Copyright Authorship Case
**Date:** March 2, 2026 | **Sources:** [Holland & Knight](https://www.hklaw.com/en/insights/publications/2026/03/the-final-word-supreme-court-refuses-to-hear-case-on-ai-authorship), [Engadget](https://www.engadget.com/ai/the-supreme-court-doesnt-care-if-you-want-to-copyright-your-ai-generated-art-171849407.html), [IPWatchdog](https://ipwatchdog.com/2026/03/02/supreme-court-denies-thalers-latest-attempt-register-copyright-ai-generated-image/)

The U.S. Supreme Court denied certiorari in *Thaler v. Perlmutter* (Case No. 25-449), refusing to hear whether AI can qualify as an author under U.S. copyright law. This upholds lower court rulings that human authorship is a fundamental requirement of the Copyright Act. The case involved Dr. Stephen Thaler's attempt to register copyright for an image autonomously generated by his DABUS AI system. Works using AI tools may still be copyrightable if a human provides sufficient creative input.

### U.S. Supreme Court: No Copyright for Purely AI-Generated Works
**Date:** March 2, 2026

*(See full coverage in Breaking News section above.)*

The denial of certiorari in *Thaler v. Perlmutter* effectively settles the question for now: purely AI-generated works without human creative input cannot receive U.S. copyright protection. Congress could still amend the law, but no legislative action is imminent.
