### Meta Announces 6.6 GW Nuclear Energy Deals for AI
**Date:** January 9, 2026

Meta announced landmark agreements to extend and expand the operation of three nuclear power plants, boost the development of new advanced nuclear technology, and foster job growth in several American communities. The three deals will support up to 6.6 gigawatts of new and existing clean energy by 2035.

**Scale:**
- A single gigawatt can power about 750,000 homes, meaning this represents enough energy to power approximately 5 million homes
- Meta's agreements with Vistra, TerraPower, and Oklo — along with one signed with Constellation Energy last year — make Meta one of the most significant corporate purchasers of nuclear energy in American history

**Key Partners:**

**Vistra:** Meta will buy more than 2.1 gigawatts of energy from two operating Vistra nuclear power plants in Ohio, plus energy from expansions at the two Ohio plants and a third Vistra plant, Beaver Valley, near Pittsburgh, Pennsylvania.

**Oklo:** The deal with Oklo will help to develop a 1.2 gigawatt power campus in Pike County, Ohio, to support Meta's data centers in the region. This advanced nuclear technology campus is expected to come online as soon as 2030.

**TerraPower:** Meta's deal with TerraPower will provide funding for two nuclear projects that could begin generating power by 2032, and Meta could obtain rights for more energy from up to six of TerraPower's other nuclear energy projects targeted for delivery by 2035.

**Purpose:**
- These energy deals are planning to eventually power Meta's Prometheus supercluster computing system being built at a data center in New Albany, Ohio
- Meta expects Prometheus to come online sometime in 2026
- The deals mark Meta's efforts to secure the energy needed to power its AI infrastructure as the company marches toward CEO Mark Zuckerberg's goal of developing superintelligence

## New Model Releases

### Falcon-H1R 7B Reasoning Model
**Date:** January 5, 2026

The Technology Innovation Institute (TII) in Abu Dhabi unveiled Falcon-H1R 7B, a compact AI model that delivers performance comparable to systems up to seven times its size.

**Key Capabilities:**
- Despite its modest 7 billion-parameter size, Falcon-H1R 7B matches or outperforms state-of-the-art reasoning models that are 2-7× larger
- Outperforms larger open-source models from Microsoft (Phi 4 Reasoning Plus 14B), Alibaba (Qwen3 32B), and NVIDIA (Nemotron H 47B)
- Built on a hybrid Transformer-Mamba2 backbone architecture
- Features a 256k token context window in standard vLLM deployments

**Performance Benchmarks:**
- Mathematics: Achieved 88.1% on AIME-24, surpassing ServiceNow AI's Apriel 1.5 (15B) which scored 86.2%
- Coding: Achieved 68.6% on the LCB v6 benchmark, claimed to be the highest among all tested models
- Efficiency: Up to 1,500 tokens/sec/GPU at batch 64, nearly doubling the speed of China's Qwen3-8B

**Training Methodology:**
- Two-stage, data-driven pipeline starting from the Falcon-H1-7B backbone
- Training on curated datasets containing step-by-step long-form reasoning traces across mathematics, coding, and science
- Refinement using the GRPO (Group Relative Policy Optimization) algorithm

**Availability:**
Released as open-source under the Falcon TII License, accessible via Hugging Face with a full technical report.

### DeepSeek R1 Paper Update
**Date:** January 2026

DeepSeek quietly overhauled the technical documentation for its flagship R1 model, expanding its whitepaper by over 60 pages to reveal the full training recipe. DeepSeek-R1 has released a major updated research paper with significant improvements in performance, training, and evaluation.

**Key Improvements:**
- The model now surpasses previous baselines like OpenAI-o1 and Qwen Preview
- Shows emergent behavior such as self-reflection and step-by-step reasoning
- On challenging tasks such as mathematics, coding, and STEM reasoning, DeepSeek-R1 now demonstrates emergent reasoning patterns
- Introduces intermediate checkpoints called Dev1, Dev2, and Dev3
- Dramatically expands evaluation to include datasets such as MMLU, DROP, GPQA, ChatBotArena style control, and more

**Transparent Research Approach:**
In a move rarely seen in corporate AI research, the updated technical paper includes a detailed "Unsuccessful Attempts" section, explicitly admitting that Monte Carlo Tree Search (MCTS) failed to deliver results in general reasoning.

**Future Model Plans:**
According to a report from The Information, DeepSeek's next flagship, tentatively dubbed "V4," is scheduled for a mid-February launch.

### Federal-State AI Regulation Conflict
**Date:** December 2025 - January 2026

In December 2025, President Trump signed an executive order titled "Ensuring a National Policy Framework for Artificial Intelligence," which proposes to establish a uniform federal policy framework for AI that preempts state AI laws deemed inconsistent with that policy.

**Key Details:**
- The Executive Order directs the Attorney General to establish an AI litigation task force to challenge state AI laws deemed inconsistent with the Executive Order's language, including on grounds of unconstitutional regulation of interstate commerce and federal preemption
- The executive order specifically mentions Colorado's law banning "algorithmic discrimination," suggesting it may force AI models to produce false results in order to avoid a "differential treatment or impact" on protected groups
- Thirty-eight states passed legislation this year to deal with the explosive growth of artificial intelligence, including on topics such as preventing the misuse of AI in elections and regulating how the technology disperses medical information

**State Laws Taking Effect January 1, 2026:**
Despite the federal executive order, California's Transparency in Frontier Artificial Intelligence Act and Texas's Responsible Artificial Intelligence Governance Act are two prominent examples of several state AI laws that went into effect on January 1, 2026. California's AB 489 took effect on January 1, 2026, and aims to prevent deceptive representations by AI systems by prohibiting technology from using specified terms that imply users are receiving care from licensed health care professionals when no such human oversight exists.
